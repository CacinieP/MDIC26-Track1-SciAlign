# Functional Stability of Tetracycline: Practical Information for Its Use in Bacterial Selection

## ABSTRACT

Genetic manipulation is widely employed in laboratories for purposes such as gene function characterization, the development of novel traits in transformed microorganisms, and the production of biotechnologically relevant compounds. A key step in this process is the selection of mutant strains, often facilitated by antibiotics. Tetracyclines are commonly used; however, their aqueous solutions tend to precipitate and turn brown over time, potentially compromising stability and efficacy. This necessitates frequent in situ preparation. The present study aimed to evaluate the functional stability of tetracycline hydrochloride for selecting transformed tetracycline-resistant bacterial strains in both liquid and solid media. A tetracycline stock solution and supplemented agar plates were prepared and used to cultivate resistant and non-resistant strains of Escherichia coli and Arcobacter butzleri. This allowed for assessment of antibiotic stability and utility over a 200-day period. Additionally, the minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC) of tetracycline was determined for each strain. Results indicated that the tetracycline-containing materials maintained functional stability for at least 175 days post-preparation, effectively supporting the selection of resistant strains in genetic manipulation experiments. These findings obviate the need for the continual and time-consuming preparation of tetracyclinesupplemented culture media, thereby enhancing the practicality of employing this antibiotic in genetic modification studies.

## 1 | Introduction

The use of genetic engineering (also known as gene cloning, gene manipulation, and genetic modification) is nowadays a routine methodology used in a multitude of laboratories for many purposes. Through this technology, DNA fragments can be eliminated and/or introduced into an organism.

Genetic manipulation has proven to be a useful tool in many sectors, including basic research on gene structure and functional characterization; the production of useful biotechnological molecules [1] or biotechnologically interesting processes in food, pharmaceutic, and chemical industries [1]; medical diagnosis and treatment; the construction of transgenic organisms; and bioremediation [2]. One of the principal methods used for genetic modification is the use of vectors, with plasmids being the most common. Plasmids are defined as small, circular, double-stranded, and selfreplicative extracellular DNA molecules. They are composed of at least one replication origin (ori), one selective marker (mainly antibiotic resistance genes), and, optionally, a multiple restriction site or multicloning site (MCS) [3, 4].

The use of antibiotic resistance genes for the selection of genetically modified mutants constitutes a method that is both simple and powerful, which is widely used [3]. The most commonly used antibiotics for mutant selection in procaryotes are ampicillin, kanamycin, and tetracycline [4]. The genes that are introduced into plasmids as selection markers confer resistance to the host bacterium by different mechanisms. Typically, the genes that are introduced to confer ampicillin resistance encode a β-lactamase, which enzymatically inactivates the antibiotic. Conversely, genes conferring kanamycin resistance predominantly promote resistance through enzymatic modification.

Tetracyclines, first discovered in the 1940s, are broad-spectrum bacteriostatic antibiotics useful against Gram positive and Gram negative bacteria (including anaerobes, some intracellular bacteria such as Rickettsia and Chlamydia, and Mycoplasma spp.) and some protozoa like Giardia lamblia, Plasmodium falciparum, or Entamoeba histolytica [5–8]. Their low cost has further contributed to their extensive utilization [7]. Their mechanism of action involves the introduction of the antibiotic into the microorganisms by both passive diffusion and active transport, resulting in the reversible binding to the bacterial 30S ribosome subunit and the subsequent blocking of the binding of aminoacyl-tRNA to the acceptor site of the ribosome. This process leads to the inhibition of protein synthesis [5–9]. To date, three mechanisms of resistance to tetracyclines have been described: (i) reduction of intracellular antibiotic concentration by impairing influx or increasing the efflux systems, (ii) ribosome protection, and (iii) enzymatic modification and inactivation. It has been demonstrated that the first two are the main mechanisms [5–8]. The genes most frequently associated with this resistance are: efflux systems related genes (tet(A), tet(B), tet(C), tet(D), tet(E), tet(G), tet(K), and tet(L)); genes that confer ribosomal protection (tet(M), tet(O), tet(Q), tet(S), tet(T), and tet(W)), and genes that generate enzymatic inactivation (tet(X) and tet(34)) [7, 10]. Among them, the ribosomal protection tetracycline resistance gene tet(O) is one of the most commonly used in genetic modification. It was first described in Campylobacter spp., but has since been detected in other bacterial genera, including Streptococcus, Enterococcus, Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus, Peptostreptococcus, and Mobiluncus [7, 9]. It shares approximately three quarters of the amino acid sequence with the other main ribosomal protection protein Tet(M) [8, 9].

In addition to the gene employed for mutant selection, it is also important to consider the specifications of the antibiotic used. In this instance, tetracycline (0.231 g/L) is significantly less soluble in water than its hydrochloride salt (50–100 g/L), as evidenced by data available in PubChem (https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih. gov/). This makes the manipulation of the latter more efficient in a laboratory setting. The challenges associated with working with tetracycline arise from its low stability in water solutions (where it becomes turbid due to the hydrolysis and precipitation of the antibiotic); its photosensitivity; and its temperature-, pH-, and humidity-dependent degradation [11–13]. Consequently, the majority of authors recommend the preparation of this antibiotic immediately prior to use, in order to avoid the aforementioned issues.

The aim of this work is therefore to determine the functional stability of tetracycline hydrochloride as an antibiotic for mutant selection. This involves the measurement of the time it takes from antibiotic preparation to its ineffectiveness for the selection of genetically modified mutants. The findings of this study will provide a robust basis for the selection of tetracycline as a resistance marker in future genetic modification experiments, as they obviate the need for the repeated preparation of culture media supplemented with this antibiotic, a procedural burden that has often constituted a significant deterrent to its adoption.

## 2 | Material and Methods

## 2.1 | Bacterial Strains and Tetracycline Stock Solution Preparation

In this study, two distinct Escherichia coli strains were used: E. coli DH5α (DSM 6897), a widely used strain in genetic modification assays which exhibits susceptibility to tetracycline, and a genetically modified DH5α strain that carries the pIMM24 plasmid, which contains the tetO tetracycline resistance gene and the Ampr ampicillin resistance cassette. The study was also performed with two strains of Arcobacter butzleri: the wild type A. butzleri RM4018 strain (CCUG 30485) and the genetically modified A. butzleri RM4018 mutant strain that contains the pIMM24 plasmid. The pIMM24-carrying strains were constructed in our laboratory (article under preparation).

E. coli DH5α was routinely grown on Luria-Bertani (LB) agar (Condalab, Torrejón de Ardoz, Spain) and A. butzleri RM4018 on Brain Heart Infusion (BHI) broth (Oxoid, Basingstoke, UK) supplemented with 1.5% bacteriological agar (Scharlau, Sentmenat, Spain). The tetracycline-resistant strains E. coli DH5α + pIMM24 and A. butzleri RM4018 + pIMM24 were grown, respectively, on LB and BHI agar, supplemented with 100 μg/mL ampicillin (CAS: 69-52-3; Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). All the strains were incubated for 24–48 h at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under aerobic conditions.

The tetracycline stock solution was prepared by mixing tetracycline hydrochloride (CAS: 64-75-5; Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA) with distilled water until it was fully dissolved, yielding a final concentration of 15 mg/mL. The solution was filtersterilized using a 0.22 nm PES filter, after which it was aliquoted and stored at −20°C until use.

## 2.2 | Determination of Tetracycline Minimal Inhibitory Concentration

The tetracycline minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC) was obtained by the gradient strip diffusion method (MIC Test Strip, Liofilchem, Teramo, Italy). The MIC determination for E. coli DH5α strains was carried out in accordance with the guidelines of the European Committee on Antimicrobial Susceptibility Testing (EUCAST) [14]. In the absence of established recommendations for Arcobacter, the protocol for A. butzleri strains was modified as follows. Bacterial suspensions were first prepared by inoculating 6 mL of BHI broth with 1 to 3 colonies and incubating at 30°C for 48 h. Thereafter, blood agar plates (Columbia agar with 5% sheep blood; BD, Heldelberg, Germany) were inoculated by pouring 750 μL of the suspension onto the surface. After air-drying, the MIC test strip was collocated, and the plates were incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 to 5 days. The strains were classified as susceptible or resistant according to the clinical breakpoints v14.0 proposed by the EUCAST for Enterobacterales in the case of E. coli strains, and for Campylobacter jejuni/coli in the case of A. butzleri.

## 2.3 | Tetracycline Functional Stability Assay

The tetracycline functional stability limit was determined as the point at which the tetracycline had degraded sufficiently in the medium to render the selection of tetracycline-resistant strains impossible. In order to establish this limit, two experiments were carried out over a total period of 200 days: one in liquid medium and the other in solid medium.

For the liquid assay, 5 mL of BHI broth supplemented with tetracycline stock solution to a final concentration of 15 μg/mL was used. After thawing, the stock solution was kept at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ A single colony of each testing strain (tetracycline-susceptible or -resistant) was inoculated in duplicate and incubated for 24 h at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with shaking. Following that, a check for growth was performed.

To perform the stability assay on solid medium, BHI agar plates supplemented with tetracycline from the stock solution to the same final concentration of 15 μg/mL were previously prepared and maintained refrigerated at 4°C until use. The tested strains were inoculated on both BHI agar and tetracyclinesupplemented BHI agar plates and incubated during 24 h at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for growth checking.

The experiments were performed approximately every 15 days, starting from the preparation of the tetracycline stock solution and the tetracycline-supplemented BHI agar plates for both the wild and tetracycline-resistant A. butzleri RM4018 and E. coli DH5α strains.

## 4 | Discussion

The MIC values observed in this study are in agreement with findings from prior research, which reported that tet(O) possessing bacteria (such as A. butzleri, Campylobacter jejuni, C. coli, Enterococcus spp., and Streptococcus spp.) and tet(M) carrying E. coli mutant strains exhibited an elevated MIC, compared to strains lacking those resistance genes [8, 15, 16]. According to recent studies, the MIC for A. butzleri strains typically ranges from 1 to 4 μg/mL [16].

Contrary to the findings reported by other authors, no color change was observed between the first and the last day of the study in either the liquid stock solution of the antibiotic or the solid medium with antibiotic [11, 12]. As observed in other studies, antibiotic precipitation was evident in the tetracycline stock solution approximately 16 to 24 h after its preparation [12]. However, the reduction in antibiotic concentration during the storage period did not appear to compromise the efficacy of the antibiotic for the selection of mutant strain, which goes in line with that reported by Mason et al. [11].

The maintenance of the antibiotic's efficacy demonstrated in this study is potentially attributable to several factors considered during the experimental design, including the storage conditions of the stock solution and solid medium $( 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , in darkness, and at neutral pH). It has been documented that high temperatures, light exposure, and extremely acidic (pH < 2) or alkaline (pH > 7) media significantly accelerate tetracycline degradation [12, 17, 18]. The stability of the antibiotic is also dependent on the time elapsed between solution preparation and its use [12, 13, 17, 18]. In previous studies, where antibiotic solution was stored at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10 days [13], at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark with a pH of 2 for 22 days [12], at room temperature for 6 days [18], or at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 72 days [17], a reduction of approximately 30% of the initial tetracycline concentration was reported in all cases. Furthermore, the working concentration of tetracycline used in the present assay (15 μg/mL) is slightly higher than that used by other researchers for the selection of tetracycline-resistant mutants (5–10 μg/mL) [19, 20]. The tetracycline concentration used in this study may also have had an effect on the longer efficacy of the antibiotic, mainly because a higher concentration will require a longer degradation time until it becomes ineffective for mutant selection [17].

From the data obtained, it can be concluded that, under the storage conditions employed in this study, the tetracycline hydrochloride concentration of the stock solution and solid medium remains above 8μg/mL for a period of at least 175days after their preparation. This finding indicates that the tetracycline hydrochloride has a half-life of 164days, which is comparable to the 120days reported previously for oxytetracycline by Xuan et al. [17].

This study demonstrates that a tetracycline hydrochloride stock solution of 15 mg/mL and a solid culture medium supplemented with 15 μg/mL tetracycline, stored in both cases in the dark at 4°C until use, are suitable for the selection of tetracyclineresistant strains carrying tet(O) in genetic modification experiments. They maintain their effectiveness for at least 175 days after their preparation, thereby eliminating the need for the repeated and time-consuming preparation of tetracyclinesupplemented materials in genetic modification studies, leading to greater efficiency in both time and resource utilization and supporting the broader application of this antibiotic in such assays.
