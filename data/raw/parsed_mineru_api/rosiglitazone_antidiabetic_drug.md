# Therapeutic potential of low-dose rosiglitazone in periodontitis: attenuation of inflammation and bone loss through macrophage reprogramming

<!-- image-->

Li-Na Zhou1,2, Zheng Zhang1,2\* and Zhi-Tao Wang1,2\*

## Abstract

Objectives This study aimed to investigate the effects of different dosages of the peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma (PPAR-γ) agonist rosiglitazone on macrophage polarization, the expression of related inflammatory factors, and alveolar bone loss in the periodontal tissues of rats with experimental periodontitis, thereby providing experimental evidence supporting the potential use of rosiglitazone as a therapeutic agent for periodontitis.

Materials and methods Forty rats were randomly assigned to four groups (n=10 per group): control group (C group), periodontitis group (P group), low-dose rosiglitazone treatment group (L group), and high-dose rosiglitazone treatment group (H group). After two weeks of rosiglitazone treatment via intraperitoneal injection, mandibles were scanned by micro-CT to quantify alveolar bone loss, with measurements of bone volume fraction (BV/TV), cementoenamel junction to alveolar bone crest (CEJ-ABC) distance, and bone mineral density (BMD). Macrophages examined using immunofluorescence to quantify M1 and M2 proportions, and immunohistochemistry was performed to evaluate expression of IL-1β, IL-10 and osteoclast.

Results Both the L and H groups significantly reduced the M1/M2 macrophage ratio and IL-1β expression in periodontal tissues compared to the P group (p<0.001), with no statistically significant differences observed between L and H groups. The L group exhibited a marked increase in M2 macrophage population and IL-10 expression relative to both the P and H groups (p<0.001). The number of osteoclasts was significantly lower in both L and H groups compared to the P group (p<0.0001), though no significant difference was detected between the L and H regimens. These findings were consistent with the Micro-CT analysis of BV/TV and CEJ-ABC distance. Notably, the BMD in the L group was significantly higher than that in both P (p<0.0001) and H (p<0.05) groups.

Conclusion Both low- and high-dose rosiglitazone effectively attenuated alveolar bone loss and mitigated associated periodontal tissue inflammation in experimental periodontitis by modulating macrophage polarization and regulating inflammatory cytokine expression. Notably, low-dose rosiglitazone may offer superior benefits in

promoting tissue repair during the advanced stages of periodontal inflammation. ## Introduction

Periodontitis is an osteometabolic disorder disease, which characterized by a dysregulated host immune response to periodontal pathogens in periodontal tissues and progressive alveolar bone loss, ultimately leading to tooth loss. It imposes a substantial economic burden on global oral disease management [1–4]. It has been confirmed that the pathogenesis of periodontitis is driven by an exaggerated immune reaction to periodontal pathogens. The concept of osteoimmunology underscores that immune cells and bone cells coexist and interact within the shared bone microenvironment through cytokines and signaling molecules, thereby cooperatively maintaining skeletal homeostasis. Notably, immune dysfunction can destabilize the bone marrow niche, and conversely, alterations in the osseous milieu can feedback to disrupt immune cell activity and cytokine profiles. Within this framework, macrophages—as highly plastic and dynamic innate immune cells—occupy a central position within the osteoimmune network [5–7]. Macrophages can polarize into pro-inflammatory M1 or anti-inflammatory M2 phenotypes in response to specific microenvironmental cues. These distinct macrophage subtypes and their associated cytokines interact extensively with mesenchymal stem cells, osteoblasts, and osteoclasts, thereby playing a central role in the alveolar bone resorption and regeneration processes during periodontal inflammation [8]. Upon stimulation by lipopolysaccharide (LPS), macrophages polarize toward the M1 phenotype and secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, and various chemokines. This response promotes the differentiation and maturation of osteoclasts, contributing to the destruction of inflamed periodontal tissues [9]. In contrast, in an IL-4-dominated milieu, macrophages tend to polarize into the M2 phenotype, secreting antiinflammatory cytokines including IL-10 and IL-4, as well as osteogenic factors that facilitate the repair and regeneration of periodontal structures [10–13]. Both M1 macrophages, which are essential for combating pathogens in the early inflammatory phase, and M2 macrophages, which are critical for resolving inflammation and promoting tissue repair in later stages, are indispensable for periodontal homeostasis. Consequently, an imbalance of M1/M2 polarization, particularly the sustained activation of M1 macrophages without transition to the M2 phenotype, can lead to immune dysregulation. This environment intensifies inflammatory processes in the periodontium and promotes the destruction of alveolar bone [14].

Rosiglitazone, a peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor (PPAR) agonist and a member of the thiazolidinedione (TZD) class of compounds, was previously widely used for the treatment of type 2 diabetes. Type 2 diabetes and periodontitis share the activation of key signaling pathways—including nuclear factor kappa-B (NFκB), activator protein-1 (AP-1), and signal transducer and activator of transcription (STAT) proteins—which amplify inflammatory feedback loops and contribute to tissue damage. Rosiglitazone can inhibit the release of inflammatory mediators by suppressing these pathways [15–17]. Notably, rosiglitazone has been shown to promote the generation and activation of anti-inflammatory M2 macrophages via the PPAR-γ pathway, thereby reducing the M1/M2 ratio [18]. This mechanism has driven investigations exploring the therapeutic potential of rosiglitazone in experimental models of periodontitis. Rosiglitazone treatment (10  mg/kg ) in experimental periodontitis rat models has been demonstrated to alleviate periodontal inflammation and attenuate tissue destruction [19]. However, rosiglitazone has been demonstrated to induce apoptosis in both osteoblasts and osteocytes, as well as the inhibition of osteogenic differentiation from bone marrow stromal cells, diverting them instead toward adipogenesis [20–22]. Furthermore, systemic rosiglitazone administration over two weeks was found to adversely affect periapical lesions by promoting osteocyte apoptosis in mice [23], indicating that rosiglitazone may also exert negative effects on bone metabolism. Therefore, this study aimed to determine whether reducing the rosiglitazone dose could maintain therapeutic efficacy while mitigating skeletal risks. We specifically compared a lower, less-characterized dose (5 mg/kg) with the conventional higher dose (10 mg/kg) for their effects on macrophage polarization and periodontal repair, to evaluate if the lower dose provides a comparable or superior therapeutic profile. Our findings may offer preliminary evidence for developing safer, dose-optimized immunomodulatory strategies targeting the PPAR-γ pathway in periodontitis.

## Materials and methods Animals and grouping

A total of 40 six-week-old male Sprague-Dawley rats were randomly assigned to four groups by a simple way using a random number table: control group (C group), experimental periodontitis group (P group), low-dose rosiglitazone treatment group (L group) and high-dose rosiglitazone treatment group (H group). All animals used in this study were obtained from Wuhan Servicebio Biotechnology Co., Ltd and housed under standard conditions (12-h light/dark cycle, 24 ± 2 °C, 40–50% humidity) with ad libitum access to food and water. The study protocol was approved by the Medical Ethics Committee of Tianjin Stomatological Hospital (Approval No. PH2019-B-008).

## Induction of experimental periodontitis and rosiglitazone treatment

Rats were anesthetized using a surgical dose of sodium pentobarbital (35 mg/kg). Sterile 2 − 0 black braided ligature wires were tied around the cervical region of the left mandibular first molars to induce periodontitis [19]. After recovery, all animals were allowed free access to food and water. One week after ligation, intraperitoneal drug administration was initiated and performed daily at a fixed time. After two weeks of treatment, all rats were humanely euthanized. This was performed under deep anesthesia (induced by an intraperitoneal injection of 150  mg/kg sodium pentobarbital), followed by cervical dislocation upon confirmation of unconsciousness.

The experimental procedures for each group were conducted as follows:

C group: No ligation; intraperitoneal injection of PBS containing 10% DMSO.

P group: Ligation; intraperitoneal injection of PBS containing 10% DMSO.

L group: Ligation; intraperitoneal injection of 5 mg/ kg rosiglitazone in PBS with 10% DMSO.

H group: Ligation; intraperitoneal injection of 10 mg/ kg rosiglitazone in PBS with 10% DMSO.

## Hematoxylin and Eosin (H&E) staining

Periodontal tissue samples were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde, dehydrated through a graded ethanol series, and embedded in paraffin. Serial horizontal Sect. (3 μm thickness) along the mesiodistal direction were obtained from the experimental periodontal tissues for H&E staining and histological observation (Leica Aperio VERSA 8). Five non-overlapping regions were randomly selected in 40x magnification fields to record the mean number of leukocytes for quantitative analysis. Additionally, a semiquantitative histopathological damage score was assigned to each entire section based on established criteria (e.g., extent of inflammatory infiltration, tissue architecture disruption).

## Micro-Computed Tomography (Micro-CT) scanning

Mandibles were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for 48  h and decalcified in 14% EDTA solution for three weeks. Alveolar bone was scanned using a Bruker SkyScan 1174 Micro-CT system (Bruker, Kontich, Belgium) with a high-resolution of 9-µm. For alveolar bone morphometry, a three-dimensional volume of interest (VOI) was defined as the interradicular bone between the mesial root of the first molar and the distal root of the third molar. Quantitative assessment of alveolar bone destruction was performed by measuring the bone volume fraction (BV/TV), the distance from the cementoenamel junction to the alveolar bone crest (CEJ-ABC), and bone mineral density (BMD), using Scanco Evaluation Software and ImageJ software [20].

## TRAP staining

Tartrate-resistant acid phosphatase (TRAP) staining was performed using a commercial TRAP staining kit (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO), conducted to identify osteoclasts in periodontal tissues, following a previously described protocol [24]. Multinucleated TRAP-positive cells located on or adjacent to the bone surface were counted manually as osteoclasts.

Immunofluorescence staining for M1 and M2 macrophages Double-label immunofluorescence staining was performed on periodontal tissue sections using a two-step method. The following antibody combinations were applied: anti-CD68 + anti-iNOS for M1-like macrophage detection while anti-CD68 + anti-CD206 for M2-like macrophage detection. Sections were incubated with anti-CD68 (1:100, ab213363, Abcam) and anti-iNOS (1:150, ab283655, Abcam), or anti-CD68 and anti-CD206 (1:200, ab64693, Abcam). Nuclei were counterstained with DAPI. Images were acquired using a Zeiss LSM 510 fluorescence microscope (Jena, Germany). Doublepositive cells were counted, and the M1/M2 ratio was calculated.

## Immunohistochemical Staining for IL-1β and IL-10

Immunohistochemical detection of the pro-inflammatory cytokine IL-1β (1:200, ab9722, Abcam) and the antiinflammatory cytokine IL-10 (1:250, ab9969, Abcam) was performed using the streptavidin-biotin complex (SABC) method, as previously described [25]. Sections were scanned with an Aperio Scanscope virtual slide scanner (Aperio Technologies Inc., Vista, CA, USA). Brown staining was considered indicative of positive expression, and the mean optical density (OD) was quantified using Image-Pro Plus 6.0 software.

## Data Analysis

All samples were evaluated in a blinded manner by the same examiner. Statistical analyses were performed using SPSS 26.0. One-way ANOVA with Bonferroni post hoc test for pairwise comparisons was used to determine significant differences among the four groups. Statistical significance was defined as $p { < } 0 . 0 5$

## Results

## Histological analysis via H&E staining

H&E staining of periodontal tissues surrounding the left mandibular first molar revealed distinct histological differences among the four groups (Fig. 1A/B). In C group, gingival fibers were orderly arranged, inflammatory cell infiltration was minimal, and alveolar bone exhibited well-preserved morphology with no significant resorption. In contrast, P group showed disrupted collagen fibers, dense inflammatory cell aggregation, and pronounced reduction in alveolar bone height compared to C group. Both L and H groups demonstrated milder gingival structural damage and greater alveolar bone height relative to P group. Histopathological analysis with leukocyte cell number and periodontal tissue damage score revealed parallel trends. The L and H groups exhibited significantly fewer leukocyte cells $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ and lower damage scores $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ compared to the P group, with no statistically significant difference between the L and H groups.

## Micro-CT evaluation of mandibular bone loss

Micro-CT analysis of the mandibles was performed, and BV/TV, CEJ-ABC distance, BMD were used to quantify alveolar bone mass (Fig.  2A/B). C group exhibited significantly higher BV/TV values than P, L and H groups $( p < 0 . 0 0 0 1 )$ . Both L and H groups showed greater bone volume compared to P group $( p < 0 . 0 0 0 1 )$ . A similar trend was observed in the CEJ-ABC distance. Notably, BMD was significantly higher in both the L and H groups compared to the P group $( p < 0 . 0 0 0 1 )$ , with the L group demonstrating a superior outcome relative to the H group $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ .

## Osteoclast expression in periodontal tissues

TRAP staining revealed that the number of osteoclasts was significantly lower in both rosiglitazone-treated groups than in P group $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ , though it remained higher than that in C group. No statistically significant difference in osteoclast count was observed between L and H groups (Fig. 2C/D).

## Macrophages polarization in periodontal tissues

Macrophage subpopulations in periodontal tissues were quantified under 400× magnification (Fig. 3A/B). P group contained significantly more M1 macrophages than C, L, and H groups $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ . The highest number of M2 macrophages was observed in L group, followed by H, P, and C groups $( p < 0 . 0 5 )$ . The M1/M2 ratio was highest in P group, followed by C, H and L groups. The ratio was significantly reduced both in L and H groups, however, no significant difference was observed between L and H groups.

## Pro-inflammatory Cytokine IL-1β and Anti-inflammatory Cytokine IL-10 Expression Levels in Periodontal Tissues Cytokine IL-10 Expression Levels in Periodontal Tissues

Immunohistochemical staining was performed to evaluate the expression of the pro-inflammatory cytokine IL-1β (Fig. 4A) and the anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10 (Fig.  4B). IL-1β expression was significantly lower in both treatment groups (L and H) compared to P group $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ , with no significant difference between the two rosiglitazone groups, although expression was slightly higher in H group than L group. IL-10 expression was highest in L group $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ , followed by H group. No significant difference in IL-10 expression was detected between H and P groups.

## Discussion

The critical role of macrophages in the pathogenesis of inflammatory and bone metabolic disorders has been well-established, positioning macrophage modulation as a promising therapeutic target for periodontitis [26]. Previous clinical studies have reported elevated M1/M2 ratios and increased expression of M1-related cytokines in the periodontal tissues of patients with chronic periodontitis compared to periodontally healthy individuals [25, 27]. Studies in rat models of experimental periodontitis have shown that both clodronate liposomes—a macrophage-depleting agent, and the polarization modulator rosiglitazone effectively reduce alveolar bone loss. However, a critical divergence was observed during the healing phase: rosiglitazone promoted bone repair and regeneration, whereas clodronate liposomes impaired these processes [28]. These findings underscore the indispensable roles of both M1 and M2 macrophages—the former in combating pathogens during the early inflammatory phase and the latter in facilitating tissue repair during later stages, which highlight the therapeutic potential of precisely regulating macrophage polarization to orchestrate a complete inflammatory response.

PPAR-γ is predominantly expressed in adipocytes, with lower levels detected in other cell types such as macrophages, T cells, and neutrophils [15]. Existing reports about PPAR-γ expression in periodontal tissues during inflammation are conflicting. Some studies have observed upregulated PPAR-γ in both experimental periodontitis models and human chronic periodontitis tissues, whereas others have reported the opposite [29–32].

This discrepancy may be attributed to the low affinity of endogenous PPAR-γ agonists, which might be insufficient to fully activate PPAR-γ signaling [33–35]. The exogenous PPAR-γ agonist rosiglitazone potently and specifically promotes macrophage polarization toward the anti-inflammatory M2 phenotype, thereby exerting anti-inflammatory and tissue-reparative effects. An eight-days course of rosiglitazone was shown to significantly reduce alveolar bone loss in experimental periodontitis [19]. However, the previous clinical application of rosiglitazone for type 2 diabetes has been linked to adverse effects on bone metabolic processes [36–38].

Since PPAR-γ activation drives progenitor cell differentiation toward adipogenesis at the expense of osteogenesis, prolonged rosiglitazone use disrupts this balance and promotes osteocyte apoptosis [35]. One study inducing periapical lesions in mice after one, two, and three weeks of rosiglitazone pre-treatment found that two weeks of administration resulted in more pronounced osteocyte apoptosis and expanded periapical destruction [23]. The 2-week treatment regimen in this study was motivated by the theoretical peak period of rosiglitazone’s pro-apoptotic side effect on osteocytes. It was therefore designed specifically to evaluate its anti-inflammatory action at this critical juncture.

Given these dualistic effects of rosiglitazone, this study aimed to evaluate whether dosage influences its antiinflammatory and tissue-reparative functions. In the present study, both low- and high-dose rosiglitazone attenuated the progression of experimental periodontitis, as evidenced by reduced inflammatory cell infiltration, decreased osteoclast activity, and improved bone parameters (increased BV/TV and BMD). Notably, the BMD in the low-dose group was significantly higher than that in the high-dose group (p < 0.05), a finding that merits particular attention. BMD, defined as the mineral content per unit area of bone, is a key determinant of bone strength [39]. Clinical evidence has established a positive correlation between periodontitis and reduced BMD [40]. However, the impact of TZDs, including rosiglitazone, on BMD in vivo remains poorly characterized and highly controversial. A prior studies have indicated that the pro-apoptotic effect of rosiglitazone on pituitary tumor cells in vivo requires oral administration at high doses (20–50  mg/kg/day), whereas lower doses (5  mg/kg/day) are ineffective [41]. This differential dose response supports our hypothesis that the pro-apoptotic effect of rosiglitazone on bone cells (including osteocytes and osteoblasts) may be less pronounced at lower doses. Consequently, during the treatment of experimental periodontitis, low-dose rosiglitazone may exert a weaker negative impact on local bone metabolism within periodontal tissues. This concept of dose- and durationdependent effects is further illustrated by a study by Sorocéanu et al., in which diabetic mice treated with rosiglitazone (3 mg/kg/day) for 90 days showed no significant change in BV/TV and BMD at day 45, but a significant decline by day 90. Notably, that study reported that the adverse skeletal effects of rosiglitazone were mediated primarily through the promotion of osteoblast and osteocyte apoptosis, rather than a direct effect on osteoclasts [38]. Collectively, the results suggest that prolonged lowdose rosiglitazone treatment can exacerbate its negative impact on bone metabolism, whereas this particular adverse effect has not been reported with short-term, low-dose administration.

Assessment of macrophage polarization revealed that both rosiglitazone doses reduced the M1/M2 ratio in periodontal tissues, though not to the level of the control group, aligning with the bone loss and osteoclast activity findings. Both low and high doses of rosiglitazone significantly reduced the number of M1 macrophages while increasing that of M2 macrophages in experimental periodontitis. This result suggests that rosiglitazone does not act exclusively on either the M1 or M2 polarization process but rather tends to modulate the M1/M2 balance. Our findings are concordant with previous research by

Chen et al., which demonstrated that a PPAR-γ agonist could correct macrophage dysfunction within a high-glucose environment, specifically, restoring the M1/M2 balance in diabetic rats—possibly through the upregulation of Angptl4 [34]. Beyond this, the present work uncovered that the low-dose group showed higher counts of both M1 and M2 macrophages than the high-dose group, yet the M1/M2 ratio did not differ significantly between treatment groups. These findings suggest that low-dose rosiglitazone treatment may remarkably enhance the polarization efficiency of macrophages, over all without altering the final polarization balance.

We further evaluated the expression levels of the early pro-inflammatory cytokine IL-1β and the mid-to-late anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10, that both known to be elevated in periodontitis patients [42]. IL-1β, a key macrophage-derived alarmin, promotes leukocyte recruitment, inflammation, and tissue repair [43, 44]. Both rosiglitazone at low and high doses suppressed IL-1β expression, with the low-dose group showing a more pronounced though statistically insignificant reduction. This apparent inconsistency between a greater M1 count and lower IL-1β level in the low-dose group may be explained by an existing study, which demonstrated that M2 macrophages, especially those polarized via PPAR-γ activation, can suppress the secretion of pro-inflammatory molecules—such as CCL-3 and TNF-α from neighboring M1 cells in an indirect co-culture systems [17]. This anti-inflammatory bystander effect could account for the reduced inflammatory milieu despite increased M1 presence. Additionally, IL-10—mainly produced by M2 macrophages downregulates pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6 [12, 45]. The significantly elevated IL-10 level in the L group compared with H and P groups, corresponds to the concurrently observed increase in M2 macrophage numbers, a relationship consistent with the established concentrationdependent effect of IL-10 on M2 phenotype induction [45]. Studies in vivo confirmed that M2-Exos reduce bone loss in experimental periodontitis via the IL-10/IL-10R pathway. Collectively, these cytokines profiles suggest that low-dose rosiglitazone treatment may dampen pro-inflammatory responses during the early phase of the inflammatory response to limit tissue damage while enhancing anti-inflammatory signals to promote regeneration in the late stage.

The concept of osteoimmunology posits that the interaction between various immune cells and bone cells underpins the pathogenesis of bone metabolic disorders, such as periodontitis and osteoporosis. Beyond macrophages, other immune populations in periodontal tissues—including T cells, B cells, innate lymphoid cells, and dendritic cells—also critically influence alveolar bone destruction and regeneration [46]. Our study demonstrates that rosiglitazone mitigates periodontal inflammatory damage by promoting macrophage polarization toward the M2 phenotype and modulating the expression of IL-1β and IL-10. This observed effect must be interpreted within the broader framework of periodontal osteoimmunology, wherein multiple immune cell types interact to regulate the RANKL/OPG axis. The therapeutic outcome likely stems from a dual mechanism: a direct effect on macrophage polarization and an indirect influence on the broader immune microenvironment. By reducing pro-inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-1β) and enhancing anti-inflammatory signals (e.g., IL-10), rosiglitazone-modulated macrophages may subsequently suppress the differentiation and function of other osteoclastogenic immune cells, such as Th17 lymphocytes. Furthermore, rosiglitazone can inhibit RANKL-induced osteoclast differentiation by blocking the binding of NFATc1 to its own promoter, thereby reducing RANKL-stimulated autoamplification of NFATc1 [47]. Consequently, the observed shifts in macrophage polarization and the reduction in alveolar bone loss likely represent the integrated outcome of rosiglitazone’s actions on multiple immune signaling pathways. The specific signaling cascades and the interplay between these direct and indirect mechanisms remain to be fully elucidated in future studies.

In summary, periodontitis involves immune dysfunction and dysbiotic biofilm driving the inflammatory destruction of periodontal tissues. The PPAR-γ agonist rosiglitazone can modulate macrophage polarization, thereby partially restore macrophage functional homeostasis (M1/M2 balance), and inhibit NF-κB, AP-1, and STAT pathways to suppress inflammatory mediator release, thereby attenuating periodontal tissue damage. This study demonstrates that short-term (2-week) administration of both low- and high-dose rosiglitazone alleviates the progression of experimental periodontitis, reduces alveolar bone destruction, and regulates macrophage polarization. Notably, our findings indicate that the low-dose rosiglitazone regimen achieves a more favorable therapeutic profile than the high-dose regimen, offering effective anti-inflammatory and tissuerepair benefits while reducing adverse skeletal effects. However, this short-term study has inherent limitations: its two-dose design does not establish a complete doseresponse relationship, and the mechanistic insights are constrained by our focus on macrophage polarization. Therefore, future studies should include comprehensive dose-ranging investigations with multiple lower doses to define the optimal therapeutic window, alongside evaluations of long-term safety and broader immunomodulatory mechanisms involving other immune cell types.

## Conclusion

Although the adverse effects of rosiglitazone in the context of periodontitis therapy remain unclear, our findings provide preliminary evidence that short-term, low-dose rosiglitazone may serve as a promising adjunctive strategy for the early intervention of periodontitis. However, the precise dose-response relationship and optimal therapeutic window require further definition through studies incorporating broader dose ranges.
