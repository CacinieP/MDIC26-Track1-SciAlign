# Comparison of Infectious Complications After Transrectal Prostate Biopsy Under Two Antibiotic Prophylaxis Regimens: Ceftriaxone Plus Cefixime Versus Ciprofloxacin Plus Gentamicin

Ahmet Yıldırım Balık | Emre Ediz | Burak Ayvacık | Arda Taşkın Taşkıran | Ekrem Başaran Dursun Baba

Department of Urology, Duzce University Faculty of Medicine, Duzce, Türkiye

Correspondence: Ahmet Yıldırım Balık (ayildirimbalik@gmail.com)

Received: 30 December 2025 | Revised: 17 February 2026 | Accepted: 12 March 2026

Keywords: antibiotic prophylaxis | cephalosporins | fluoroquinolones | infectious complications | prostate biopsy

## ABSTRACT

Objectives: To compare the effectiveness and safety of two antibiotic prophylaxis regimens, ciprofloxacin plus gentamicin and ceftriaxone plus cefixime, in preventing infectious complications after transrectal ultrasound-guided prostate biopsy. Methods: The study retrospectively reviewed 705 consecutive patients who underwent transrectal ultrasound-guided prostate biopsy between January 2020 and January 2024. Group 1 (n = 374) received ciprofloxacin plus gentamicin, and Group 2 (n = 331) received ceftriaxone plus cefixime. All patients underwent standard rectal preparation with povidone-iodine, and needle-tip disinfection with formalin. The primary outcome was the development of post-biopsy infectious complications, while secondary outcomes included sepsis and infection-related hospitalization.

Results: The two groups were comparable in terms of age, body mass index, prostate-specific antigen levels, and comorbidities. The overall rate of post-biopsy infection was 1.4%, occurring in 1.1% patients in Group 1 and 1.8% patients in Group 2 (p = 0.61). The incidence of complicated urinary tract infection was 0.3% versus 0.9%, and infection-related hospitalization occurred in 0.5% versus 0.3% of patients in Group 1 and Group 2, respectively, with no cases of sepsis observed. In univariable analyses, diabetes mellitus, histopathological chronic prostatitis, and pre-biopsy antibiotic use were not significantly associated with post-biopsy infection.

Conclusion: The combination of ceftriaxone and cefixime showed a safety profile broadly consistent with the combination of ciprofloxacin and gentamicin for biopsy prophylaxis. Considering increasing fluoroquinolone resistance and growing restrictions, this cephalosporin-based regimen may be considered a feasible option for transrectal ultrasound-guided prostate biopsy when implemented together with strict antiseptic measures.

## 1 | Introduction

Transrectal ultrasound-guided prostate biopsy (TRUS-PB) is a generally safe standard diagnostic procedure, but may lead to several complications. Most are self-limiting, such as hematospermia, hematuria, rectal bleeding, and acute urinary retention, but more serious events, including urinary tract infections and sepsis, may also occur [1]. Thus, guidelines recommend the use of antibiotic prophylaxis before transrectal prostate biopsy.

However, following the European Commission/EMA decisions prompted by resistance and safety concerns, EAU guidelines no longer recommend fluoroquinolones [2]. Nevertheless, the EAU position paper on the prevention of infectious complications following prostate biopsy notes that fluoroquinolone prophylaxis may still be considered in centers with low local resistance rates, although such use falls outside the European Union directive, and no universally accepted resistance threshold has yet been defined [3]. Despite these restrictions, fluoroquinolones continue to be used for biopsy prophylaxis in some centers, particularly where ciprofloxacin susceptibility remains high [4–6].

These developments have led to a marked rise in the clinical use of alternative prophylactic antibiotics, such as ceftriaxone, cefpodoxime, and fosfomycin [7–9]. In our institution, fluoroquinolones were historically preferred for biopsy prophylaxis due to established practice patterns and local resistance rates that were relatively low in previous years. However, due to increasing resistance rates and following the decisions of the European Medicines Agency and the recommendations of the EAU guidelines, and taking into account concerns regarding infectious safety, the prophylaxis protocol was gradually switched to a cephalosporin-based regimen with oral cefixime as the main component.

In this context, the present study was designed to evaluate two antibiotic prophylaxis regimens used for TRUS-PB in our clinic, namely ceftriaxone plus cefixime and ciprofloxacin plus gentamicin, which have not been directly compared in previous studies. This study aimed to compare these regimens in terms of post-procedural infectious complications and thereby contribute to the identification of safe and effective prophylaxis strategies in an era of increasing fluoroquinolone resistance.

## 2 | Materials and Methods

## 2.1 | Study Design and Patient Selection

This study retrospectively reviewed the data of patients who underwent TRUS-PB at the Duzce University Faculty of Medicine Hospital, a tertiary care center. Ethical approval was obtained from the Clinical Research Ethics Committee of Duzce University Faculty of Medicine (approval number: 2025/97). Men who underwent TRUS-PB between January 2020 and January 2024 because of elevated serum prostate-specific antigen (PSA) levels and/or suspicious findings on digital rectal examination were screened for eligibility. Patients with evidence of an active urinary tract infection, ongoing immunosuppressive therapy, non-protocol antibiotic prophylaxis, or incomplete follow-up data were excluded. After applying these criteria, 705 men were included in the study.

Written informed consent was obtained from all patients before biopsy, and the study was conducted in accordance with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki. In patients with suspected prostate cancer, multiparametric magnetic resonance imaging (mpMRI) was performed, and targeted biopsies were added in cases with lesions scored PI-RADS 3–5. Biopsy indication was determined based on serum PSA levels, digital rectal examination findings, and mpMRI results.

## 2.2 | Biopsy Protocol and Antibiotic Prophylaxis

All patients received a laxative enema on the morning of the procedure for colonic preparation. Before biopsy, 10% povidone-iodine solution was applied rectally, followed by a lubricant gel. Biopsies were performed in the left lateral decubitus position using single-use 18-gauge needles under TRUS guidance. For local anesthesia, bilateral periprostatic nerve block was performed by administering 10 mL of 1% lidocaine. Between each biopsy core, the needle (with the notch exposed) was immersed in a 10% formalin solution for approximately 5 s. Gently swirling the needle dislodged the biopsy specimen directly into the bottle while simultaneously disinfecting the needle tip. Subsequently, the needle was gently wiped with a sterile gauze to remove excess formalin. No rinsing with saline or additional manual interventions was performed during these steps.

A systematic biopsy was performed, typically using a standard 12-core template; however, in a subset of patients, non-standard core counts (such as 6, 20, or > 20) were obtained based on clinical indication. On the day of the procedure, all patients were managed on an outpatient basis, observed for 6 h after biopsy, and monitored for early signs of complications.

Patients were divided into two groups according to the prophylaxis regimen. In Group 1 (n = 374), patients received oral ciprofloxacin 500 mg on the evening before the biopsy (approximately 12 h pre-procedure), intramuscular gentamicin 80 mg immediately before the biopsy, and then continued oral ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily to complete a total 3-day regimen (including the pre-procedure dose). In Group 2 (n = 331), patients received oral cefixime 400 mg on the evening before the biopsy (approximately 12 h pre-procedure), intramuscular ceftriaxone 1 g immediately before the biopsy, and then continued oral cefixime 400 mg once daily to complete a total 3-day regimen (including the pre-procedure dose).

## 2.3 | Outcome Measures

The primary outcome of the study was post-biopsy infection, defined as any infectious complication occurring within 30 days after the procedure. Secondary outcomes included both uncomplicated and complicated urinary tract infection (UTI), sepsis, and infection-related hospitalization.

Post-biopsy complications were identified by retrospectively screening all institutional records, including admissions to any unit, laboratory results, and prescriptions within a 30- day period. To capture external events, electronic prescriptions and healthcare utilization records integrated with the National Health System were reviewed in accordance with patient consent. Additionally, during follow-up visits, patients were actively interviewed regarding infectious symptoms, antibiotic use, and external healthcare visits.

Post-biopsy infection was classified as the presence of either complicated or uncomplicated UTI. Uncomplicated UTI was defined as new-onset lower urinary tract symptoms such as dysuria, increased urinary frequency, urgency, or suprapubic tenderness in the absence of fever, accompanied by pyuria (≥ 5 leukocytes per high-power field or positive leukocyte esterase on dipstick) and/or significant bacteriuria $( \ge 1 0 ^ { 5 } \mathrm { C F U / m L } )$ Complicated UTI included infections presenting with pyuria and/or bacteriuria along with systemic symptoms such as fever, chills, malaise, nausea/vomiting, or flank pain. Sepsis was defined according to the Sepsis-3 criteria as life-threatening organ dysfunction following UTI, clinically characterized by an increase in the Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score of ≥ 2 points [10, 11].

## 2.4 | Statistical Analysis

Continuous variables were assessed for normality using the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test, skewness/kurtosis statistics, and visual inspection of histograms. Normally distributed data are presented as mean ± standard deviation, whereas non-normally distributed data are reported as median (interquartile range). Between-group comparisons were performed using the independent samples t-test or the Mann–Whitney U test, as appropriate. Categorical variables were compared using the Pearson's chi-square test or Fisher's exact test. In addition, univariate logistic regression analysis was performed to explore potential predictors of post-biopsy infection, with odds ratios (ORs), 95% confidence intervals, and p values reported for each covariate. Statistical significance was set at $p { < } 0 . 0 5$ . All analyses were conducted using IBM SPSS Statistics, version 26 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA).

## 3 | Results

Age, body mass index (BMI), total PSA, and prostate volume were comparable between the two groups (all $p { > } 0 . 0 5 )$ . Free PSA levels were higher in Group 2 (3.28 ± 4.54 vs. 2.16 ± 2.53; $\scriptstyle { p = 0 . 0 0 8 ) }$ . Groups were similar regarding comorbidities, biopsy history, recent antibiotic use, and prior UTI (all p > 0.05). Nonmalignant pathology was more frequent in Group $\scriptstyle \left( p = 0 . 0 2 2 \right)$ whereas ISUP grade 1 cancer was more common in Group 2 $\scriptstyle ( p = 0 . 0 4 7 ) ;$ however, the rate of clinically significant cancer (ISUP ≥ 2) was similar between the groups (p = 0.203). Although the distribution of biopsy techniques (standard, fusion, and ‘other’ protocols involving non-standard core counts such as 6, 20, or > 20) varied between the cohorts, the total number of biopsy cores obtained, representing the procedural trauma burden, was statistically similar between Group 1 (13.83 ± 2.30) and

Group $2 \left( 1 3 . 9 5 \pm 1 . 9 8 ; p = 0 . 4 9 \right)$ . Furthermore, the histopathological presence of chronic prostatitis did not differ significantly between the groups (33.4% vs. 39.6%; p = 0.11). Patient demographics and clinical characteristics are summarized in Table 1.

Post-biopsy infection rates were low in both groups. Overall, post-biopsy infection occurred in 10 patients (1.4%), 4 in Group 1 and 6 in Group 2 (p = 0.61; Fisher's exact test). The absolute risk difference between the groups was 0.7% (95% CI: −1.1% to 2.5%). Complicated post-biopsy infection was observed in four patients (0.6%), with no significant difference between the groups. No sepsis was recorded in either group. Infection related hospitalization was required in three patients (0.4%; 2 in Group 1 and 1 in Group 2), and this also did not differ significantly between the groups. When stratified by biopsy technique (Standard, Fusion, and Other), no statistically significant difference was observed in infection rates among the subgroups $\left( p > 0 . 0 5 \right)$ . Specifically, in the Fusion group, a mean of $3 . 0 4 \pm 0 . 8 1$ additional targeted cores were obtained beyond the standard template; however, this increased procedural burden did not lead to a higher infection rate compared to the standard 12-core standard biopsy (1.2% vs. 1.5%). Detailed data on post-biopsy infections are presented in Table 2.

Among the 10 patients who developed post-biopsy infection, 7 patients recovered with outpatient treatment, whereas 3 patients required short-term hospitalization. A total of 6 patients had no comorbidities, while the others had hypertension, coronary artery disease, and/or diabetes. Urine culture and antibiotic susceptibility testing were performed for all 10 patients (100%) who developed infectious complications. Cultures were negative in 2 patients (20%), whereas causative pathogens were isolated in the remaining 8 cases: Escherichia coli in 6 patients (60%), Pseudomonas spp. in 1 patient (10%), and Enterococcus spp. in 1 patient (10%). Among these 8 patients with documented bacterial growth, resistance to the administered prophylactic regimen was identified in only 2 cases (25%), while the isolates in the remaining 6 cases (75%) were susceptible to the prophylactic antibiotics used. A detailed description of patients who developed post-biopsy infection is provided in Table 3.

Non-infectious complications were also recorded. Massive hematuria was rare in both groups: it occurred in 3 of 374 patients in Group 1 (0.8%) and in 5 of 331 patients in Group 2 (1.5%), for an overall rate of 8 of 705 patients (1.1%). Most of these cases were managed conservatively, and only 1 patient required hospitalization. Acute urinary retention was observed in 7 of 705 patients in the entire cohort (approximately 1.0%). Regarding the specific antiseptic protocols, including rectal povidone-iodine cleansing and formalin disinfection, no procedure-related complications, such as chemical irritation or equipment malfunction, were observed in any patient.

We explored potential predictors of post-biopsy infection using the univariable logistic regression to estimate unadjusted odds ratios. No covariate reached statistical significance. Compared with the ciprofloxacin plus gentamicin group (reference), ceftriaxone plus cefixime group was not statistically associated with post-biopsy infection (OR 1.71; 95% CI 0.48–6.11; p = 0.410). Similarly, diabetes mellitus (OR 1.75; 95% CI 0.37–8.36; $\textstyle p = 0 . 4 8 5 )$ , the histopathological presence of chronic prostatitis (OR 1.17; 95% CI 0.33–4.19; p = 0.807), and pre-biopsy antibiotic use (OR 1.06; 95% CI 0.13–8.49; p = 0.957) were not statistically associated with infection. Due to the low number of infection events (n = 10), multivariable modeling was not performed. Given the limited number of events, this analysis was conducted as an exploratory assessment.

## 4 | Discussion

In this study, we found no significant difference in post-biopsy infection or hospitalization rates between the regimens, and no sepsis events. To our knowledge, this is the first study to directly compare these two prophylaxis regimens. These findings indicate that the overall risk profile in our series was very low and lies at the lower end of the ranges reported in recent series and systematic reviews, which describe infection rates after transrectal prostate biopsy between 0.1% and 7.0% and sepsis rates between 0.3% and 3.1% [6, 12–15].

Several studies comparing regimens that include gentamicin, amikacin, prulifloxacin, levofloxacin, and cefixime have reported similar infection rates across different protocols [16–18]. The combination of fluoroquinolones and aminoglycosides, which was also utilized in this study, is a conventionally used regimen to broaden antimicrobial coverage against Gramnegative pathogens and benefit from synergistic bactericidal effects. However, the global surge in fluoroquinolone resistance and stringent regulatory restrictions have necessitated the adoption of alternative strategies. In this context, the ceftriaxone plus cefixime regimen implemented in this study represents a strategic choice structured in light of pharmacokinetic data and current guideline recommendations. Indeed, clinical studies have previously demonstrated that the addition of ceftriaxone to prophylaxis protocols significantly reduces infectious complications, specifically by targeting fluoroquinolone-resistant Gram-negative pathogens [19]. Furthermore, it is reported in recent literature that a transition to oral cephalosporins following systemic ceftriaxone administration is also utilized [20].

Building on this effectiveness, the study protocol relies on combining single-dose parenteral ceftriaxone and oral cefixime, both included as distinct alternatives in the EAU guidelines, based on the logic of ‘loading dose’ followed by oral maintenance (step-down or switch therapy) [2, 21]. Parenteral ceftriaxone was selected because its high bioavailability and rapid prostatic penetration achieve sustained concentrations well above the MIC for target pathogens during the highest-risk period of the procedure [22, 23]. Consequently, the primary goal of this protocol was to exploit the pharmacokinetic advantage of parenteral administration for immediate bactericidal effect, combined with the logistical convenience of oral maintenance.

Rectal swab culture guided targeted prophylaxis has been proposed as an effective alternative in patients with high fluoroquinolone resistance; however, it has been emphasized that such targeted strategies cannot be implemented in all centers because of logistical and cost-related constraints [24]. As a result, many institutions have shifted towards alternative agents. For example, Mendo López et al., reported that switching from ciprofloxacin to ceftriaxone reduced the rate of UTI from 4.2% to 1.2% and the rate of infection-related hospitalization from 2.3% to 0.1% [8]. In our clinic, by contrast, the gradual transition from routine ciprofloxacin plus gentamicin prophylaxis to ceftriaxone plus cefixime prophylaxis was not associated with a reduction in post-biopsy infection rates, and a meaningful difference between the regimens was not observed. A plausible explanation is that the relatively lower local gentamicin resistance rates compared to other antibiotics, and the augmented prophylactic effect provided by gentamicin addition, may have contributed to this outcome.

In a meta-analysis by Pilatz et  al., patients receiving fluoroquinolone-based prophylaxis were categorized into singledose, 1 day, 3 day, and 5–7 day regimens, and it was shown that single-dose protocols lasting less than 24 h were associated with significantly higher infection rates compared with regimens extended over 3 days [14]. In contrast, more recent comparative studies, such as those by Anwar et al., (comparing single dose vs. 3-day) and Kalkanlı et al., (comparing single dose vs. 7-day), reported that extending the duration of prophylaxis did not yield a statistically significant difference in infectious complication rates [25, 26]. Considering the risks of increased antibiotic resistance and adverse events associated with prolonged exposure, this study adopted a prophylaxis strategy extended to 3 days. This approach avoids the insufficiency of single-dose regimens while mitigating the risks of prolonged therapy, thereby representing a safe and effective ‘balance point’ consistent with the current literature.

An important aspect in the prevention of infectious complications is intrarectal cleansing with povidone-iodine and strict antiseptic measures, this cephalosporin-based prophylaxis may be considered as a potential alternative strategy in the era of fluoroquinolone resistance. Future prospective and multicenter studies are warranted to further validate these findings.
