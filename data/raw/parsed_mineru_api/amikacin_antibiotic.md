# Amikacin pharmacokinetics, target attainment, and predictors in critically ill children: a retrospective cohort

Chin Chiang Toh1  · Jian Lynn Lee2  · Mohd Makmor Bakry1  · Noraida Mohamed Shah1  · Shamin Mohd Safan1

Received: 19 February 2026 / Revised: 18 March 2026 / Accepted: 8 April 2026

## Abstract

We aimed to quantify amikacin pharmacokinetics (PK) and early target attainment using routine third-dose therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM) in pediatric intensive care unit (PICU) patients and to identify clinical covariates associated with volume of distribution (Vd) and clearance (CL). We conducted a retrospective cohort study of PICU patients who received intravenous amikacin between January 2022 and December 2023. Individual PK parameters were estimated using third-dose peak and trough concentrations via the Sawchuk-Zaske method. Clinical and laboratory covariates were evaluated for associations with Vd and CL. Among 210 patients, mean Vd was $0 . 5 9 { \pm } 0 . 3 0 \mathrm { L / k g }$ and mean CL was $0 . 0 8 { \pm } 0 . 0 3 \mathrm { L / k g / h }$ . Early target under attainment was frequent, with 23.3% of $C _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ and 7.6% of $C _ { \mathrm { m i n } }$ values outside therapeutic targets. Independent predictors of Vd included age, creatinine clearance (CrCl), and alanine aminotransferase (ALT), while CL was independently associated with age, CrCl, urea, and ALT. Conclusion: Critically ill children demonstrate expanded Vd and increased CL, contributing to inadequate early amikacin exposure under standard dosing. Early PK-guided dosing and model-informed strategies may improve target attainment and individualized therapy in this high-risk population. What is Known: Amikacin PK in children is variable, and standard dosing may not consistently achieve therapeutic targets. Critically ill pediatric patients often experience altered drug disposition and clearance, which can afect amikacin exposure and response. What is New: This study quantifed amikacin PK parameters in critically ill children receiving routine TDM in the PICU. Expanded Vd and increased CL were identifed, with age, CrCl, urea, and ALT associated with variability in amikacin PK.

## Introduction

Amikacin remains an essential aminoglycoside, frequently used as part of combination therapy with cephalosporins or carbapenems for severe Gram-negative infections in pediatric intensive care settings [1]. Its bactericidal activity is concentration-dependent, supported by a substantial post-antibiotic efect, whereby higher peak concentrations accelerate bacterial killing [2]. Achieving a peak plasma concentration to minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) ratio of at least 8 to 10 is strongly associated with microbiological clearance and improved clinical outcomes [3]. Conversely, excessive trough exposure is a recognized driver of nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity [4, 5]. Therefore, careful optimization of dosing regimens is critical to ensure high peak concentrations for efcacy while minimizing trough concentrations to reduce toxicity.

In current practice, amikacin is administered as a highdose (15–20 mg/kg), extended-interval regimen to maximize concentration-dependent killing efect [6–8]. However, achieving the intended pharmacodynamic targets in children is challenging due to substantial physiological variability such as developmental changes in extracellular fuid volume and renal maturation leading to substantial variability in volume of distribution (Vd) and clearance (CL) [9]. In critical illness, these maturational diferences are further amplifed and complicated by capillary leak and fuid overload [10], organ dysfunction [11], and the use of extracorporeal supports [12]. Together, these factors lead to early peak exposures frequently deviating from expected values under standard age-based and weight-based dosing as a result of altered distribution and accelerated elimination [13]. Therapeutic targets commonly adopted in clinical practice include a peak concentration of 20–50 mg/L to ensure adequate bacterial killing and a trough concentration below 5 mg/L to minimize nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity risks [2, 14]. Despite the routine use of therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM), published reports consistently show that target amikacin peak concentration remains difcult to achieve in pediatric intensive care unit (PICU) populations [15, 16].

While neonatal pharmacokinetics (PK) of amikacin are well characterized [17], data describing its disposition in post-neonatal (aged 1 month to 18 years) critically ill children remain limited. Contemporary real-world PK data derived from routine TDM in general PICU cohorts are scarce, creating uncertainty around the performance of current empiric dosing strategies and the clinical determinants of interindividual PK variability. Addressing these knowledge gaps is crucial to support precision dosing approaches in this vulnerable population. Therefore, our study aimed to quantify amikacin PK parameters and early target attainment using routine third-dose TDM in PICU patients and to identify clinical covariates associated with variability in Vd and CL.

## Methods

## Study design and patient population

We conducted a retrospective observational study of critically ill pediatric patients who received at least 3 doses of intravenous amikacin in the PICU at Tunku Azizah Hospital, the largest tertiary pediatric referral center in Malaysia, which receives critically ill cases from across the country. Data from patients admitted between January 2022 and December 2023 were retrospectively reviewed. All patients aged 1 month to 18 years who were treated with amikacin during their PICU stay were eligible. Neonates managed in a neonatal intensive care unit (ICU) were excluded, as they follow distinct dosing protocols.

This study was conducted in accordance with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki. Ethics approval was obtained from the Medical Research and Ethics Committee (MREC) of Malaysia (MREC reference number: 23–02727- HEX) and the Universiti Kebangsaan Malaysia Research Committee (Ethics reference number: JEP-2023–726). A waiver of informed consent was granted due to the retrospective design of the study.

## Amikacin dosing and monitoring protocol

Standard empiric dose of amikacin was 15 mg/kg (maximum 1500 mg) administered once every 24 h and was adjusted via TDM. For this study, we recorded the actual dosing regimen for each patient. TDM was performed according to unit protocol: a trough and a peak concentration were obtained after achieving steady state (at least three consecutive maintenance doses administered at the same dosing interval). Trough concentration was defned as the sample obtained within 30 min or immediately before the third dose, and peak concentration was measured immediately after the end of the infusion (the infusion duration was standardized at 1 h according to institutional protocol) [8, 18].

Amikacin concentrations in plasma were measured using two automated immunoassay platforms over the study period. Prior to May 2022, analyses were performed using a particleenhanced turbidimetric inhibition immunoassay (PETINIA) on the ARCHITECT c-system (Abbott Diagnostics, Abbott Park, IL, USA), with a validated lower limit of quantifcation (LLOQ) of 2 mg/L. From May 2022 onwards, amikacin concentrations were determined using a kinetic interaction of microparticles in solution (KIMS) immunoassay on the cobas c-system (Roche Diagnostics GmbH, Mannheim, Germany), with a validated LLOQ of 0.8 mg/L.

## Data collection

Amikacin TDM records were screened to include only patients admitted to the PICU. For each patient, only the frst set of peak and trough concentrations collected following initiation of amikacin therapy was included in the analysis. Repeated TDM concentration following dose adjustment, as recommended by the TDM team, was excluded to avoid confounding. Additionally, patients with incomplete data (i.e., only a single drug concentration or invalid samples) were excluded to ensure suitability for PK analysis.

Clinical data were extracted from electronic medical records and included demographic variables (age, sex, body weight), indication for amikacin, dosing regimen, and markers of organ function. In particular, renal function was assessed by recording serum creatinine (SCr) at amikacin initiation and calculating estimated creatinine clearance (CrCl) using the Modifed Schwartz formula [19].

## Pharmacokinetic analysis

A two-stage pharmacokinetic analysis approach was employed. In the frst stage, individual PK parameters were estimated for each patient using measured peak and trough plasma concentrations obtained at steady state. A one-compartment model with frst-order elimination following intermittent intravenous infusion was assumed.

The elimination rate constant $( K _ { \mathrm { e } } )$ was calculated for each patient using the Sawchuk-Zaske method derived from two measured concentrations [20, 21]:

$$
K e = \frac { l n C _ { \mathrm { p e a k } } - l n C _ { \mathrm { t r o u g h } } } { T }\tag{1}
$$

where $C _ { \mathrm { p e a k } }$ is the measured peak concentration, $C _ { \mathrm { t r o u g h } }$ is the measured trough concentration, and T is the time interval between peak and trough sampling.

The maximum concentration at the end of infusion $( C _ { \mathrm { m a x } } )$ was back-extrapolated from the observed peak concentration to correct for elimination occurring between infusion completion and sampling:

$$
C _ { \mathrm { m a x } } = C _ { \mathrm { p e a k } } \times e ^ { K e t }\tag{2}
$$

where t is the time elapsed between infusion completion and peak sampling.

The minimum concentration $( C _ { \mathrm { m i n } } )$ during the dosing interval was estimated as:

$$
C _ { \mathrm { m i n } } = C _ { \mathrm { m a x } } \times e ^ { - K e \tau }\tag{3}
$$

where 𝜏 is the dosing interval.

The Vd and CL were calculated using standard equations and normalized to body weight:

$$
V d = { \frac { D o s e } { C _ { \mathrm { m a x } } ( 1 - e ^ { - K e \tau } ) } }\tag{4}
$$

$$
C L = K e \times V d\tag{5}
$$

In the second stage, individual PK parameter estimates were summarized at the population level and used for descriptive analysis and exploration of relationships between PK parameters and patient-specifc covariates.

## Statistical analysis

Descriptive statistics were used to summarize patient characteristics and PK parameters. Continuous variables are presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD) and categorical variables as counts (percentages). Univariable linear regression models for PK parameters were performed to explore associations between PK parameters (Vd, CL) and potential covariates: gender, age, SCr, CrCl, urea, albumin, total protein, and liver enzymes (alkaline phosphatase (ALP) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT)). We then constructed multivariable linear regression models for Vd and CL (separately) to identify independent predictors. Covariates with $p < 0 . 1$ in univariable analysis were considered in the multivariable model, with a backward elimination approach to arrive at a parsimonious set of predictors $( p < 0 . 0 5$ considered signifcant) [22]. To avoid mathematical coupling, body weight was excluded from multivariable analysis of weight-normalized Vd (L/kg) and CL (L/kg/h). Regression results are reported as regression coefcients with 95% confdence intervals and p values. All statistical analyses were conducted using SPSS (version 29) with a signifcance level of $p { < } 0 . 0 5$

Additionally, a target attainment analysis was performed to evaluate the clinical appropriateness of initial dosing regimens. Using each patient’s individual PK parameters, we calculated $C _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ and $C _ { \mathrm { m i n } }$ for the administered dose. These simulated concentrations were then assessed against predefned therapeutic targets: a $C _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of 20–50 mg/L and a $C _ { \mathrm { m i n } }$ < 5 mg/L, as recommended in clinical pharmacokinetic references and institutional guidelines [2, 14]. We calculated the proportion of patients who achieved both targets simultaneously, as well as the proportions who were below or above the desired range for each individual parameter. This analysis provides insight into the efectiveness of empiric dosing practices within our cohort and identifes areas for potential optimization.

This study was conducted and reported in accordance with the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology (STROBE) statement.

## Results

## Patient characteristics and amikacin dosing

A total of 210 pediatric patients met the inclusion criteria (Fig. 1). Key demographics, clinical characteristics, amikacin indication, and dosing are summarized in Table 1. Briefy, the cohort was 65.2% male (n = 137) and 34.8% female (n = 73). Ages ranged from 1 month to 16 years, with a mean age of $3 . 5 6 \pm 3 . 8 1$ years. Patients’ weights ranged from 3 kg (infants) up to 76 kg (adolescents), refecting the broad pediatric spectrum. The study population represented a heterogeneous tertiary PICU case mix, in which admissions were most commonly related to the need for ventilatory support and intensive care management rather than a single underlying diagnosis. The majority of patients (70%) received amikacin as empiric therapy for suspected severe infection (such as sepsis or nosocomial infection), while the remaining 30% were treated for culture-confrmed infections where amikacin was deemed necessary (often Gram-negative bacteremia).

The mean amikacin dose administered was $1 4 . 0 5 \pm 2 . 1 3$ mg/kg in a single-daily dose. ## Pharmacokinetic parameters

Using the peak and trough concentrations at the third dose, individual PK parameters were calculated for each patient and subsequently summarized at the population level (Table 2). The mean derived $K _ { \mathrm { e } }$ across patients was $0 . 1 4 { \pm } 0 . 0 3 \mathrm { h } ^ { - 1 }$ , and the typical half-life in our cohort was $5 . 0 3 \pm 1 . 0 0 \mathrm { h }$ . The mean Vd normalized to body weight was $0 . 5 9 { \pm } 0 . 3 0 \mathrm { L / k g }$ , and the mean CL was $0 . 0 8 \pm 0 . 0 3$ L/kg/h.

Amikacin concentrations at steady state reveal substantial variability in achieving therapeutic targets (Figs. 2 and 3). Out of 210 patients, 49 patients (23.3%) had an $C _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ outside the desired 20–50 mg/L range. Specifcally, 17.1% (36 patients) had subtherapeutic level (< 20 mg/L) and 6.2% (13 patients) had supratherapeutic level (> 50 mg/L). In contrast, $C _ { \mathrm { m i n } }$ values were generally within target range: only 7.6% of patients (16 out of 210) had a $C _ { \mathrm { m i n } }$ above 5 mg/L. Notably, inadequate peak exposure was substantially more frequent than excessive trough concentrations, suggesting underdosing or enhanced clearance rather than early toxicity risk as the dominant issue at treatment initiation. A scatterplot of measured concentrations versus time after dose (Fig. 4) demonstrated amikacin concentrations declined with increasing time after dose, consistent with frst-order elimination. However, the large variability observed suggests heterogeneous drug disposition associated with critical illness.

## Covariate analysis

We investigated the relationships between patient-specifc factors and PK parameters of amikacin. In univariable analyses, the Vd was signifcantly associated with age, SCr, CrCl, urea, ALP, and ALT levels. Multivariable linear regression analysis identifes age $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ , CrCl $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ , and ALT $( p < 0 . 0 0 1 )$ as independent predictors of Vd (Table 3).

## Discussion

This retrospective cohort study provides contemporary evidence on amikacin disposition in critically ill pediatric patients. Our fndings demonstrate increased Vd and CL, resulting in approximately one quarter of patients failing to achieve the recommended therapeutic $C _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ range despite standard weight-based once-daily dosing. Multivariable analyses identifed age and renal function as statistically signifcant covariates associated with Vd and CL, with additional associations observed for ALT.

This aligns with critical illness pathology, where systemic infammation and aggressive fuid resuscitation expand extracellular volume, resembling neonatal distribution pattern [26]. Notably, fuid overload of up to 20% relative to admission weight is common in PICU patients [27], plausibly contributing to extracellular expansion and increased distribution of hydrophilic agents such as amikacin. Such expansion of Vd lowers peak concentrations for a given dose and explains why subtherapeutic $C _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ values were more common than supratherapeutic exposures in our cohort. Similar increases in Vd have been reported in other critically ill pediatric populations, including mixed ICU cohorts [28], and more recent data also support the notion that critically ill children have larger distribution volumes compared to their healthier counterparts [29].

The observed CL (0.08 L/kg/h) exceeds values reported in pediatric populations (approximately 0.068 L/kg/h) [30] and supports the growing recognition of augmented renal clearance (ARC) in PICU settings [31, 32]. ARC is particularly common among critically ill children with trauma, burns, or sepsis, conditions often associated with elevated cardiac output and reduced systemic vascular resistance. A scoping review estimated that up to 78% of critically ill pediatric patients exhibit ARC during their ICU stay, a prevalence notably higher than in adult populations, placing these patients at substantial risk for antibiotic underexposure [33]. Conversely, a small proportion of children demonstrated elevated trough concentrations, refecting impaired renal elimination and illustrating the bidirectional PK variability typical of critically ill patients. Clinically, enhanced CL reduces amikacin half-life and eliminates drug more rapidly, predisposing patients to low $C _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ unless adequately dosed. This interaction between expanded Vd and increased CL creates a dual mechanism of underexposure that is unlikely to be addressed by conventional fxed weight-based dosing strategies in critically ill pediatric patients. As aminoglycoside efcacy is driven by concentration-dependent bactericidal activity, suboptimal peak exposures may result in inadequate early microbial killing and compromised clinical outcomes. This is particularly critical in critically ill patients, in whom failure to achieve target concentrations promptly may delay infection control, increase the risk of treatment failure, and contribute to excess mortality [34].

The multivariable regression analysis provides mechanistic support for the covariate-PK parameter relationships. The identifed factors associated with amikacin PK variability are consistent with established principles of developmental PK and the pathophysiology of critical illness. Increasing age was independently associated with reductions in both Vd and CL, with each additional year corresponding to a decrease of 0.19 L/kg and 0.002 L/kg/h, respectively. These findings reflect maturational influences, including agerelated changes in body composition and progressive renal maturation, which collectively reduce extracellular fuid volume and normalize weight-adjusted renal drug elimination [35, 36].

Renal function emerged as a key determinant of amikacin CL. Each 10 mL/min increase in CrCl was associated with a 0.002 L/kg/h increase in CL, underscoring the dominant role of glomerular fltration in aminoglycoside elimination. Conversely, higher urea concentrations were independently associated with reduced CL, likely refecting impaired renal excretory capacity in patients with renal dysfunction. The concordant associations of estimated CrCl and urea with amikacin CL align with its known elimination pathway, as amikacin is predominantly excreted unchanged by the kidneys [9]. Although CrCl is classically linked to aminoglycoside CL, we also evaluated CrCl on Vd because in critically ill children higher CrCl can refect a hyperdynamic/ARC phenotype that often coexists with capillary leak and fuid resuscitation, expanding extracellular water and increasing apparent Vd [37, 38]. Additionally, because our Vd was derived using a Sawchuk-Zaske two-point, one-compartment approach, Vd is mathematically coupled to the elimination rate constant that is closely related to renal function, so CrCl may also partly capture this kinetic coupling inherent to routine peak-trough-based estimation.

The identifcation of ALT as a signifcant predictor of both Vd and CL is noteworthy. Although the magnitude of the efect per unit increase in ALT was small, elevated liver enzymes in critically ill children may refect the severity of systemic illness rather than intrinsic hepatic drug handling. In this context, hepatic dysfunction, captured as a component of established organ dysfunction severity scores such as the Pediatric Logistic Organ Dysfunction (PELOD) score, serves as a marker of global physiological derangement, systemic infammation, endothelial dysfunction, and capillary leak [39]. These pathophysiological processes are closely linked to extracellular fuid expansion and altered drug disposition [40, 41]. Thus, the observed association between ALT and amikacin PK likely serves as a proxy for illness severity rather than refecting hepatic metabolism. Taken together, these regression fndings serve to inform covariate selection and prioritization for future population pharmacokinetic analyses, providing a biologically plausible framework for covariate testing in subsequent nonlinear mixed-efects modelling.

This study provides valuable real-world data on amikacin use and PK in a broad cohort of critically ill children. Nevertheless, several limitations should be acknowledged. First, this study was conducted at a single center, which may limit generalizability to other PICUs with diferent patient profles or clinical practices. However, as the largest pediatric referral center in Malaysia, our cohort represents a broad spectrum of critically ill children, supporting national relevance. Second, a key methodological limitation relates to the use of a two-stage PK analysis approach based on routine TDM data. While this approach is pragmatic and well suited to describing central tendencies of PK parameters in clinical practice, it has limited ability to characterize interindividual variability and residual unexplained variability, particularly in the context of dynamic and heterogeneous pathophysiology associated with critical illness. Consequently, future studies incorporating population PK modelling are warranted. Third, the retrospective design relied on routinely documented clinical data, which may be subject to inconsistencies, particularly in relation to exact dosing times and blood sampling timestamps. A prospective study design would allow for more rigorous data collection, thereby improving the reliability and validity of PK parameter estimation. Lastly, amikacin concentrations were measured using more than one assay platform; systematic inter-assay diferences may have afected measured concentrations and the derived PK parameters; therefore, platform-related bias cannot be excluded. Future studies should standardize the analytical method to avoid potential assay-related bias.

## Conclusion

Critically ill pediatric patients demonstrated substantial variability in amikacin disposition, characterized by expanded Vd and enhanced CL that frequently led to subtherapeutic peak concentrations under standard dosing. A smaller subset exhibited impaired elimination and elevated trough concentrations, refecting the bidirectional nature of PK alterations in critical illness. Age and renal function emerged as key determinants of both Vd and CL, with additional exploratory associations observed for ALT, suggesting that routinely available biochemical markers may provide further insight into altered drug distribution.

These fndings underscore the limitations of conventional weight-based dosing in this population. Incorporating early PK parameter estimation and model-informed initial dosing strategies, ideally supported by population PK models that integrate clinically relevant covariates, may optimize precision dosing and improve target attainment in this high-risk population.
