# Incidence and management of pyrexia syndrome in patients treated with dabrafenib and trametinib: a retrospective study

Justin Komisarof1  · Jayesh Menon2  · Aryn Andrzejewski2  · Deborah Mulford2

Received: 13 November 2025 / Accepted: 25 March 2026 / Published online: 29 March 2026   
© The Author(s) 2026

## Abstract

Purpose The combination of dabrafenib and trametinib has been used to treat a variety of cancers featuring BRAF mutations. The most common adverse efect is pyrexia syndrome. We aimed to determine the incidence of pyrexia syndrome and severe/ complicated pyrexia syndrome and investigate outcomes associated with pyrexia syndrome as well as pyrexia management. Methods In this retrospective cohort study, individuals who received dabrafenib and trametinib from 2015 to 2022 at a large academic medical center were identifed. Chart review was performed to determine if patients developed pyrexia syndrome, the severity of pyrexia syndrome, time of onset of pyrexia, time on treatment, how pyrexia was managed, and why treatment was discontinued.

Results 88 patients were included in the fnal analysis. 34 patients (38.6%) developed pyrexia syndrome and 14 patients (15.9%) developed severe/complicated pyrexia syndrome. Most patients who developed pyrexia syndrome did so in their frst month of treatment. Patients who developed pyrexia syndrome were more likely to discontinue treatment due to an adverse event than patients who did not (32.3% vs 22.2%). Patients who developed pyrexia syndrome spent a longer mean period on treatment (16.9 months) than patients who did not (9.0 months). Pyrexia syndrome was managed with dose modifcations and interruptions and several agents including steroids, antipyretics, and colchicine.

Conclusion Pyrexia syndrome is a common adverse event in patients on dabrafenib and trametinib. Patients who develop pyrexia syndrome unexpectedly stayed on treatment longer in our cohort, highlighting the importance of early recognition and supportive management to maintain patients on therapy.

Keywords Pyrexia · Severe/complicated pyrexia · BRAFV600E · Targeted therapy · Dabrafenib and trametinib

## Introduction

The combination of dabrafenib and trametinib has been used to treat a variety of malignancies featuring BRAF V600E and V600K mutations, most frequently melanoma [1, 2]. The most common adverse effect in patients on dabrafenib/trametinib is pyrexia syndrome, which occurs in approximately 50% of melanoma patients in the metastatic setting, and approximately 60% of melanoma patients in the adjuvant setting [3, 4]. Pyrexia syndrome is defned by the Canadian Working Group as fever > 38 °C and/or chills, rigors, and night sweats ± fu-like symptoms [4]. The working group further defned severe/complicated pyrexia as pyrexia syndrome requiring hospitalization, or pyrexia complicated by Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE) grade 2 or higher dehydration, hypotension, renal dysfunction, confusion, or vomiting [4]. While pyrexia syndrome is seen most frequently with dabrafenib/ trametinib, it is also seen in approximately 20% of patients on other BRAF/MEK inhibitor combinations such as vemurafenib/cobimetinib and encorafenib/binimetinib, suggesting that pyrexia syndrome is caused by the efect of signaling pathway blockade rather than an individual targeted therapy agent [3, 5, 6]. Pyrexia syndrome secondary to dabrafenib/ trametinib is not specifc to any single cancer, occurring in approximately 50% of patients with non-small-cell lung cancer (NSCLC) and 37% of patients with anaplastic thyroid cancer [7, 8].

Pyrexia syndrome is usually treated with antipyretics and holding the ofending medications. To prevent recurrent episodes of pyrexia, steroids and dose reductions are used, although new evidence suggests that colchicine may also play a role in pyrexia prophylaxis [9–11]. It is uncommon that patients will require discontinuation of therapy [9]. Severe/complicated pyrexia syndrome is typically thought of as a rare clinical entity, although its incidence has not been formally studied. However, cases of extreme systemic infammatory response syndrome (SIRS) and subsequent distributive shock have been reported, illustrating that severe/complicated pyrexia syndrome can be a lifethreatening phenomenon [12]. In this study, our objective was to report the incidence over time of pyrexia syndrome and severe/complicated pyrexia syndrome associated with dabrafenib/trametinib at a large academic medical center. We investigate the likelihood that pyrexia syndrome will lead to discontinuation of treatment. We explore the potential prognostic signifcance of pyrexia syndrome. Finally, we discuss current management of pyrexia syndrome and severe/complicated pyrexia syndrome.

## Methods

## Study design

In this retrospective cohort study, data was collected on cancer patients treated with the combination of dabrafenib and trametinib at a large academic medical center between 01/01/2015 and 11/22/2022. This protocol was approved by the institutional review board (IRB).

## Participants

All patients treated with dabrafenib and trametinib were eligible for inclusion in the study. Patients were excluded from the trial if incomplete outcomes data was available during the time they were on dabrafenib/trametinib. Patients did not provide informed consent as the study was declared exempt by the IRB.

## Procedures/outcomes

For each identifed patient, data was collected by the research team on whether the patient developed pyrexia syndrome, the number of severe/complicated episode(s), the timing of all episodes of pyrexia syndrome, and the number of hospitalizations due to severe/complicated pyrexia syndrome. Additional information was collected on the type of malignancy, duration of combination therapy, why treatment was ultimately discontinued, and management strategies used to address pyrexia.

Episodes of pyrexia syndrome were identifed using the Canadian Working Group defnition of pyrexia, which is defned as fever > 38° C and/or chills, rigors, night sweats, or fu-like symptoms in the absence of an infectious source. Severe/complicated pyrexia was identifed by episodes of pyrexia syndrome requiring hospitalization or meeting criteria for grade 2 or higher dehydration, hypotension, renal dysfunction, confusion, or vomiting as defned by the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE).

For each clinical episode of pyrexia, chart analysis was performed to rule out that the episode was due to infection rather than pyrexia syndrome. Episodes of pyrexia were attributed to infection in cases where patients presented with a fever and/or multiple sets of blood cultures positive for pathogenic organisms, positive blood culture from a central line, or positive urine culture (monomicrobial with > 100,000 CFU) with urine analysis consistent with infection and urinary tract infection symptoms. Episodes were also attributed to infection if the patient tested positive for HIV, Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B, RSV, infuenza, COVID-19, C. dif toxin, tick-borne illnesses, or tuberculosis. Patients were also determined to have an infection if they had cerebrospinal fuid (CSF) culture positive or lumbar puncture with CSF studies consistent with bacterial or viral meningitis or encephalopathy with meningeal signs documented on exam, clinical signs consistent with pneumonia with chest x-ray or CT scan consistent with pneumonia or empyema, abdominal CT scan with abscesses or consistent with colitis, evidence of cardiac vegetation on transthoracic echocardiogram or transesophageal echocardiogram, cellulitis documented on exam, a fuid collection with positive culture following aspiration, or a dental abscess documented on exam. All cases in which the presence of infection was ambiguous were sent for independent review by an infectious disease specialist.

## Statistical analysis

Data was analyzed on a per-patient basis using descriptive frequencies. Patients for whom outcomes data was incomplete were censored. Standard performance measures were calculated for the entire study population and the following subgroups – patients who did not develop pyrexia, patients who developed pyrexia, and patients who developed severe/ complicated pyrexia. These measures included incidence and prevalence of pyrexia, likelihood of therapy being stopped due to disease progression or adverse event, mean months on therapy, and median month in which pyrexia developed. Continuous variables were assessed using two-tailed Student’s t-test, and p < 0.05 was used to denote a statistically signifcant result. Analyses were performed using the software package R.

## Results

To better understand the prevalence of pyrexia syndrome and severe/complicated pyrexia syndrome, we performed a retrospective chart review on 109 patients treated at our institution with dabrafenib and trametinib. 88 of these patients had complete outcomes data and were included in the fnal analysis. Mean age at treatment initiation was 59 years old. 54 patients were male (61.4%) and 34 were female (38.6%). Melanoma was the most frequently treated malignancy making up 73.9% of the data set, followed by lung cancer (11.4%), thyroid cancer (10.2%), and glioma (3.4%). 86% of patients were treated for metastatic/unresectable disease and 14% of patients received adjuvant treatment. The greatest number of pyrexia episodes experienced by any individual patient was 7.

26.4% of patients discontinued dabrafenib and trametinib due to an adverse event. Unsurprisingly, patients who developed pyrexia were more likely than patients who did not develop pyrexia to discontinue therapy due to an adverse event (32.4% vs 22.2%, p = 0.29, chi-squared test), and patients who developed severe/complicated pyrexia were even more likely to discontinue treatment (42.9%, p = 0.12, chi-squared test) (Fig. 2a).

51% of patients in the data set discontinued dabrafenib and trametinib due to disease progression. Patients who developed pyrexia were more likely to discontinue therapy due to disease progression than patients who did not develop pyrexia (58.8% vs 46.3%, p = 0.25, chi-squared test), but patients with severe/complicated pyrexia were slightly less likely to discontinue therapy due to disease progression (42.9%, p = 0.82, chi-squared test) (Fig. 2b). Notably, 8 patients died while still on dabrafenib and trametinib. 2 of these patients had experienced pyrexia and 6 had not.

Several strategies were used to keep patients with pyrexia on treatment (Table 2). Of these 34 patients, 22 (65%) had either dabrafenib or trametinib dose-reduced or had treatment temporarily interrupted and then restarted. 23 patients (68%) were treated with acetaminophen, NSAIDs, or a combination of both. 3 patients (9%) were treated with steroids, although many patients were already on steroids at baseline. 1 patient (3%) was treated with colchicine. Finally, 5 patients (15%) did not receive any prophylactic interventions.

## Discussion

Among patients at our institution treated with dabrafenib and trametinib for various cancers featuring BRAF V600E mutations, 38.6% developed pyrexia syndrome and 15.9% developed severe/complicated pyrexia. Previously, rates of severe/complicated pyrexia have not been described, but this result illustrates the challenge in managing these patients. It can be very challenging to diferentiate between severe/complicated pyrexia and sepsis, and patients should be evaluated in a hospital with multidisciplinary care including oncology and infectious disease. Notably, 44% of patients who developed pyrexia syndrome went on to experience multiple episodes of pyrexia. This demonstrates the need for efective pyrexia prophylaxis in these patients to enable them to stay on an efective treatment.

As expected, patients who developed pyrexia, especially severe/complicated pyrexia, were more likely to discontinue treatment due to an adverse event. However, surprisingly, patients who developed pyrexia had a longer overall duration of therapy than patients who did not. Furthermore, patients who developed severe/complicated pyrexia were found to remain on dabrafenib/trametinib signifcantly longer than patients who did not develop pyrexia. This observation is the opposite of what might be expected, specifcally that patients who developed a serious adverse event would be more likely to discontinue therapy earlier than patients who did not. One possibility is that patients are more likely to develop pyrexia the longer they remain on treatment. This would be the case if patients were equally likely to develop pyrexia at any point in their treatment course. However, we found that the median time to develop pyrexia was 1 month, and that it was very unlikely for patients to develop pyrexia for the frst time late in their clinical course. Another potential explanation for the phenomenon of patients who develop pyrexia remaining on treatment for longer is that pyrexia syndrome is a positive prognostic indicator of treatment efcacy. This is worthy of further investigation in a prospective setting. Notably, a similar efect across multiple cancers has been seen in patients treated with immunotherapy in which patients who experience an immune-related adverse event appear to have better outcomes [13].

There is currently insufficient evidence to determine the best practices for pyrexia management and prophylaxis in patients on dabrafenib and trametinib. There does not appear to be signifcant utility in starting prophylaxis prior to the frst episode of pyrexia. When a patient frst develops pyrexia, dabrafenib and trametinib should be held, and the patient should be treated with antipyretics and supportive care as needed, with antibiotics administered if there is any concern for infection or clinical instability [8]. Antipyretics are not particularly efective at preventing recurrent episodes of pyrexia, although they are important in the management of mild pyrexia [14]. Dose reduction and treatment interruption has been successful in reducing pyrexia incidence [3, 14, 15]. However, this strategy could lead to inefective treatment of the underlying malignancy. Corticosteroids have been used for pyrexia prophylaxis and appear efective [4, 15, 16], although their efcacy has not been proven in a randomized clinical trial, nor has an optimum dosing strategy been identified. Patients could also potentially be harmed by prolonged exposure to steroids. Therefore, patient-specific factors should always be considered in determining an appropriate prophylaxis plan. Finally, based on preliminary data that suggests a possible mechanism of paradoxical MAPK-pathway activation in pyrexia syndrome, colchicine has been tried for pyrexia prophylaxis based on its efcacy in treating familial Mediterranean fever, in which the MAPK pathway is also activated [17]. In a limited case series of patients, colchicine has been efective, although again no randomized trials have been conducted [10].

Ultimately, our fndings suggest that early recognition and multidisciplinary management of pyrexia syndrome— particularly severe or complicated cases—can help patients remain on efective treatment longer. Use of dose adjustments, antipyretics, corticosteroids, and emerging agents like colchicine may all play a role in providing efective supportive care. Given the importance of dabrafenib and trametinib in the treatment of malignancies featuring BRAF V600 mutations, further research into the mechanism and optimal management of pyrexia syndrome is needed.
