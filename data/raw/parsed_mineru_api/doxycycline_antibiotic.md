# Beta-Lactam Plus Azithromycin or Doxycycline vs Fluoroquinolones in Adult Patients Hospitalized With Community-Acquired Pneumonia: A Systematic Review and Network Meta-analysis

Magdoleen H. Farah,1,2, Mohamed Abusalih,1,2, Wigdan Farah,3, Mohamad El Labban,4, Qusay Haydour,5 Neha Ramachandran,1,2, Zhen Wang,1,2   
Erin F. Barreto,6 Larry Prokop,7 M. Hassan Murad,1,2 and Yewande Odeyemi3

1 Evidence-Based Practice Research Program, Mayo Clinic, Rochester, Minnesota, USA, 2 Robert D. and Patricia E. Kern Center for the Science of Health Care Delivery, Mayo Clinic, Rochester, Minnesota, USA, 3 Division of Pulmonology and Critical Care Medicine, Mayo Clinic, Rochester, Minnesota, USA, 4 Department of Internal Medicine, Mayo Clinic Health System, Mankato, Minnesota, USA, 5 Division of Pulmonary, Critical Care, Sleep and Occupational Medicine, Indiana University, Indianapolis, Indiana, USA, 6 Department of Pharmacy, Mayo Clinic, Rochester, Minnesota, USA, and 7 Mayo Clinic Libraries, Mayo Clinic, Rochester, Minnesota, USA

Background. Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) is a significant public health concern associated with increased rates of hospital admissions and mortality. The purpose of the current study was to compare beta-lactam plus azithromycin or doxycycline vs fluoroquinolones in adults hospitalized with CAP.

Methods. We searched several databases from inception to 10 June 2024. Two reviewers independently screened, selected, and extracted data. Disagreements were resolved by consensus or a third reviewer. Study quality was assessed in duplicate with the Cochrane Risk of Bias 2 for randomized studies and the Newcastle-Ottawa Scale for nonrandomized studies. Meta-analysis was conducted by a random effect model when feasible. Network meta-analysis was performed to compare direct and indirect evidence. The GRADE approach (Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development, and Evaluation) was followed to rate the certainty of evidence.

Results. Twenty studies were included (20 beta-lactam + azithromycin, 7 beta-lactam + doxycycline, 12 levofloxacin, and 4 moxifloxacin): 6 were randomized, enrolling 861 participants (mean age, 61.8 years; 43.2% women), and 14 were nonrandomized, enrolling 185 928 participants (mean age, 65.2 years; 42.9% women). Direct comparison and network metaanalysis results showed no statistically significant differences and likely equal effectiveness between beta-lactam + azithromycin and beta-lactam + doxycycline in terms of in-hospital mortality, need for intensive care unit admission or invasive mechanical ventilation, or safety outcomes including Clostridioides difficile infection and QT prolongation.

Conclusions. The current evidence demonstrated no likely difference in outcomes between azithromycin and doxycycline in adults hospitalized with CAP receiving beta-lactam. Head-to-head randomized clinical trials are needed to validate these results.

Keywords. β-lactam antibiotics plus azithromycin or doxycycline; community-acquired pneumonia; fluoroquinolones; network meta-analysis; systematic review.

Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) is a major public health concern and the leading infectious disease cause of death worldwide, imposing a considerable burden on patients and health care systems [1–3], with mortality rates ranging from 4.1% to 9.6% in cases of hospitalization [4, 5].

Empiric treatment for individuals hospitalized with nonsevere CAP without risk factors for multidrug-resistant organisms involves either a combination of a beta-lactam antibiotic with a macrolide, such as azithromycin, or monotherapy with a respiratory fluoroquinolone, such as levofloxacin or moxifloxacin. A third option, combination therapy with a betalactam and doxycycline, remains an alternative treatment option for nonsevere CAP in the presence of a contraindication to macrolides and respiratory fluoroquinolones. These approaches are strongly supported by guidelines of the American Thoracic Society and the Infectious Diseases Society of America [6–8].

While several studies have examined the comparative effectiveness of antimicrobial regimens for CAP, some observational studies and meta-analyses have suggested that combinations of beta-lactam and macrolide may offer improved outcomes in severe cases, particularly in a hospitalized setting [9, 10]. However, other studies have found no difference in clinical or safety outcomes [11–14]. The evidence, particularly on doxycycline, remains limited by the lack of robust randomized controlled trials (RCTs). Hence, current recommendations rely heavily on observational data [1, 9, 10, 15].

This systematic review and network meta-analysis (NMA) synthesize the existing evidence by comparing the efficacy and safety of azithromycin and doxycycline, each combined with beta-lactam antibiotic, as well as respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy, in the treatment of adults hospitalized with CAP, to provide clinically relevant insights that support informed decision making.

## METHODS

This systematic review followed an a priori protocol on the management of CAP. This report complies with the PRISMA statement (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-analyses) [16] and Extension Statement for Reporting Network Meta-analyses [17].

## Eligibility Criteria

We included RCTs and comparative observational studies that met the following inclusion criteria:

• Participants were adults aged ≥18 years who were hospitalized with CAP (severe and nonsevere).

• Eligible comparison involved a combination of beta-lactam with azithromycin or doxycycline as compared with each other or respiratory fluoroquinolones.

• Outcomes were as follows: treatment failure, time to objective clinical stability (meeting criteria for clinical stability per the Infectious Diseases Society of America) [6], hospital-free days (HFDs; defined as the number of days alive outside a hospital or emergency department following the index admission), hospital length of stay (LOS), in-hospital mortality, 30-day mortality, need for invasive or noninvasive mechanical ventilation, and need for intensive care unit (ICU) admission.

• Safety outcomes included Clostridioides difficile infection and QT prolongation.

Respiratory fluoroquinolones (levofloxacin, moxifloxacin) were included to enable comprehensive indirect comparisons of common inpatient CAP regimens, given the limited availability of direct comparative studies. The studies were included if at least 85% of participants met the inclusion criteria (ie, individuals hospitalized with CAP receiving the relevant interventions). Studies were excluded if they were noncomparative, nonpublished abstracts, or non-English language; if they included participants with hospital-acquired pneumonia, health care–associated pneumonia, or ventilator-associated pneumonia; or if they were performed in pediatric participants or animals.

The question is presented per the PICO format (population, intervention, comparator, and outcomes) and accompanied by the eligibility criteria available in Supplementary Table 1 [18].

## Data Sources and Search Strategies

A comprehensive search of several databases was conducted from inception to 10 June 2024 and limited to studies published in English. The databases included Ovid MEDLINE(R) and Epub Ahead of Print, In-Process & Other Non-indexed Citations, and Daily), Ovid EMBASE, Ovid Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials, Ovid Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews, and Scopus.

The search strategy was designed and conducted by an experienced librarian with input from the study’s principal investigator. A controlled vocabulary supplemented with keywords was used to search for studies of azithromycin vs doxycycline in combination with beta-lactam in adults hospitalized with CAP. The actual strategy listing all search terms used and how they are combined is available in the supplementary file [18].

## Study Selection

Two reviewers (M. H. F., M. A., W. F., M. E. L., Q. H., N. R.) independently screened the titles and abstracts of candidate studies. If a study was deemed ineligible by a single reviewer, a second reviewer reassessed its eligibility. If a study was considered eligible, the full text was screened by 2 independent reviewers (M. H. F., M. A., W. F., M. E. L., Q. H., N. R.). Any disagreements were resolved by consensus or by a third reviewer.

## Data Extraction

Data from the studies were extracted by 1 reviewer and confirmed by an independent second reviewer (M. H. F., M. A., W. F., M. E. L., Q. H., N. R.) for completeness and accuracy using a standardized data extraction form. If a study compared different interventions, we considered the groups relevant to our PICO question. Elements of data extraction are presented in Supplementary Tables 2.1 and 2.2 [18].

## Data Synthesis and Analysis

Analysis was performed by statistical software STATA (version 17.0; StataCorp LLC). For pairwise comparisons, relative risk (RR) and 95% CIs were estimated and, when feasible, directly meta-analyzed by the DerSimonian-Liard random effects model with Hartung-Knapp-Sidik-Jonkman variance correction. Statistical heterogeneity was evaluated with the I 2 statistic, interpreted as 0%–30% (not important), 30%–60% (moderate), 60%–90% (substantial), and 90%–100% (considerable). Indirect comparisons in NMA were done with a frequentist approach, by use of a multivariate consistency model randomeffects meta-regression [19]. Network incoherence between direct and indirect comparisons was evaluated for local incoherence by the node-splitting method and global incoherence by a $\chi ^ { 2 }$ statistic test. Relative ranking of interventions for each outcome was expressed with the surface under the cumulative ranking (SUCRA), which represents the percentage of efficacy or safety achieved by an intervention as compared with a hypothetical comparator that is always the best without uncertainty (ie, SUCRA of 1.00). Higher SUCRA scores correspond to a higher ranking for efficacy outcomes and a higher ranking for safety (ie, lower risk of C difficile infection). Due to the limited number of eligible studies, we were unable to conduct subgroup analyses (eg, CAP severity, age, sex).

## Methodological Quality and Certainty of Evidence

Two independent reviewers (M. H. F., M. A., W. F., M. E. L., Q. H., N. R.) assessed the risk of bias using the Cochrane Risk of Bias assessment tool version 2 [20] for RCTs and the Newcastle-Ottawa Scale [21] for nonrandomized studies. The certainty of evidence was evaluated by GRADE criteria (Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development, and Evaluation) [22], with RCTs starting at high certainty and observational studies at low certainty. The certainty of evidence was downrated for risk of bias, indirectness, imprecision, inconsistency or heterogeneity, or publication bias. Indirect evidence starts at the lowest certainty rating of contributing pairwise comparisons and may be downgraded for imprecision or inconsistency with direct evidence. NMA assumptions of coherence (direct vs indirect estimates) and transivity (effect modifier comparability) were satisfied. Data are presented in Table 1 and Supplementary Tables 3.1 and 3.2 [18].

## RESULTS

## Study Selection

Electronic searches yielded 1180 citations (supplementary file) [18]. An additional 82 references were identified through other sources, including cross-referencing of studies and consultation with content experts. After exclusion of duplicates, 1238 unique titles and abstracts were identified and screened for eligibility, of which 106 citations underwent full-text review and were subsequently evaluated for inclusion. A total of 20 studies [1, 11, 12, 15, 23–38] were included. The study selection process is illustrated in Figure 1.

## Characteristics and Risk of Bias of Included Studies

The baseline characteristics, demographics, and intervention details of the studies in the NMA are provided in Supplementary Tables 2.1 and 2.2 [18]. Of the studies, 6 were RCTs [26, 27, 31, 32, 37, 38] enrolling 861 participants (mean age, 61.8 years; 43.2% women), and 14 were observational studies [1, 11, 12, 15,

23–25, 28–30, 33–36] enrolling 185 928 participants (mean age, 65.2 years; 42.9% women). Four interventions were identified and included in the NMA: beta-lactam + azithromycin, betalactam + doxycycline, levofloxacin, and moxifloxacin. Of the studies, 15 were single-center studies whereas 5 [11, 12, 15, 27, 38] were multicenter.

Overall, the majority of the studies were at low to moderate risk of bias. The risk-of-bias assessment is presented in Supplementary Tables 3.1 and 3.2 [18].

## Effectiveness Outcomes

There was no significant heterogeneity among studies contributing direct evidence, and no evidence of network incoherence was observed across any of the outcome estimates. Direct and NMA estimates along with the certainty of evidence are presented in Table 1. Figure 2 presents the map of the network for the 4 interventions. The network results are presented in Supplementary Tables 4.2.a and 4.2.b [18]. The summary of findings is presented in Table 1, Figures 2 and 3, Supplementary Table 4.1.a, and Supplementary Tables 4.2.a and 4.2.b [18].

## In-Hospital Mortality

Eleven studies [1, 11, 12, 15, 23, 27, 30, 31, 33, 34, 36] evaluated in-hospital mortality in individuals treated for CAP, comparing beta-lactam + azithromycin, beta-lactam + doxycycline, and fluoroquinolone (primarily levofloxacin, with some studies including moxifloxacin).

Direct meta-analysis showed no statistically significant difference between beta-lactam + doxycycline and beta-lactam + azithromycin for in-hospital mortality risk (RR, 1.12; 95% CI, .77–1.65; very low certainty).

The NMA showed that there was no significant difference between beta-lactam + doxycycline and beta-lactam + azithromycin (RR, 1.12; 95% CI, .85–1.49). Similar findings were observed when comparing levofloxacin vs beta-lactam + azithromycin (RR, 0.94; 95% CI, .71–1.24), moxifloxacin vs beta-lactam + azithromycin (RR, 1.63; 95% CI, .15–18.02), levofloxacin vs beta-lactam + doxycycline (RR, 0.84; 95% CI, .58–1.20), moxifloxacin vs beta-lactam + doxycycline (RR, 1.45; 95% CI, .13–16.29), and moxifloxacin vs levofloxacin (RR, 1.74; 95% CI, .15–19.46).

## 30-Day Mortality

Five studies [1, 15, 24, 33, 38] evaluated 30-day mortality in individuals treated for CAP, comparing beta-lactam + azithromycin, beta-lactam + doxycycline, and fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin). Direct meta-analysis showed that beta-lactam + doxycycline was associated with slightly increased risk of 30-day mortality when compared with beta-lactam + azithromycin (RR, 1.19; 95% CI, 1.00–1.43; low certainty). The NMA showed that beta-lactam + doxycycline was associated with increased risk of 30-day mortality as compared with betalactam + azithromycin (RR, 1.19; 95% CI, 1.04–1.37). However, no significant differences were found when levofloxacin was compared with beta-lactam + doxycycline (RR, 1.39; 95% CI, .80–2.42) or when levofloxacin was compared with betalactam + azithromycin (RR, 1.66; 95% CI, .97–2.83).

## Hospital-Free Days

One study [15] evaluated HFDs in individuals treated for CAP, comparing beta-lactam + azithromycin and beta-lactam + doxycycline. The HFDs were comparable between groups with a median of 23 days (range, 16.5–24.7) in the betalactam + azithromycin group and 22 days (range, 17.5–24) in the beta-lactam + doxycycline group, indicating no clinically meaningful difference between treatment regimens. Meta-analysis was not feasible for this outcome. No data were available regarding the HFDs for respiratory fluoroquinolones.

## Hospital LOS

Sixteen studies [1, 11, 12, 23–25, 28–33, 35–38] evaluated LOS in individuals treated for CAP, comparing beta-lactam + azithromycin, beta-lactam + doxycycline, and fluoroquinolone (primarily levofloxacin, with some studies including moxifloxacin). The median LOS was 5.8 days (range, 2.8–9.2) for the beta-lactam + azithromycin group, 6 days (range, 4–23.3) for the beta-lactam + doxycycline group, and 5 days (range, 2.9–8.4) for the fluoroquinolone group.

Overall, LOS appeared comparable across regimens, although variability, particularly in large datasets, reflected differences in patient characteristics, clinical severity, and care settings. Due to heterogeneity in outcome definitions, follow-up durations, and reporting formats, meta-analysis was not feasible.

## Need for ICU Admission

Four studies [1, 11, 12, 23] evaluated the need for ICU admission in individuals treated for CAP, comparing beta-lactam + azithromycin, beta-lactam + doxycycline, and fluoroquinolone (primarily levofloxacin, with some studies including moxifloxacin). Direct meta-analysis showed no statistically significant difference between beta-lactam + doxycycline and beta-lactam + azithromycin for the need for ICU admission (RR, 0.95; 95% CI, .46–1.93; very low certainty). The NMA suggested that, when compared with beta-lactam + azithromycin, levofloxacin may be associated with a lower risk of ICU admission (RR, 0.81, 95% CI, .67–.99; low certainty). However, no statistically significant difference was found when levofloxacin was compared with beta-lactam + doxycycline (RR, 0.85; 95% CI, .53–1.36).

## Need for Invasive Mechanical Ventilation

One study [12] evaluated the need for invasive mechanical ventilation in individuals treated for CAP, comparing beta-lactam + azithromycin and beta-lactam + doxycycline. Direct metaanalysis showed no statistically significant difference between beta-lactam + doxycycline and beta-lactam + azithromycin in reducing the need for invasive mechanical ventilation (RR, 0.43; 95% CI, .03–7.04; very low certainty). No data were available regarding the need for invasive mechanical ventilation for respiratory fluoroquinolones.

## Time to Clinical Stability

Two studies [23, 24] evaluated time to clinical stability, comparing beta-lactam + azithromycin with beta-lactam + doxycycline. The first study [23] showed that the mean time to clinical stability was 5 days (SD, 2.96) in the beta-lactam + azithromycin group and 4 days (SD, 2.22) in the beta-lactam + doxycycline group, suggesting a shorter time to clinical stability with the latter regimen. In contrast, the second study [24] showed similar results between the treatment groups, with a mean of 3.8 days (SD, 3.3) in the beta-lactam + azithromycin group and 3.9 days (SD, 2.7) in the beta-lactam + doxycycline group. Overall, the findings were inconsistent across studies, with one suggesting a modest benefit of the doxycycline combination, while the other showed no meaningful difference between the regimens. Meta-analysis was not feasible for this outcome. No data were available regarding the clinical stability for respiratory fluoroquinolones.

## Treatment Failure

Six studies [12, 26, 27, 31, 32, 38] evaluated treatment failure in individuals treated for CAP, comparing beta-lactam + azithromycin, beta-lactam + doxycycline, and fluoroquinolone (primarily levofloxacin, with some studies including moxifloxacin). Direct meta-analysis showed no statistically significant difference between beta-lactam + doxycycline and beta-lactam + azithromycin in the risk of treatment failure (RR, 1.42; 95% CI, .92–2.17; very low certainty). The NMA suggested that levofloxacin may be associated with lower risk of treatment failure as compared with beta-lactam + doxycycline (RR, 0.59, 95% CI, .38–.91; low certainty). However, there were no statistically significant differences between levofloxacin and beta-lactam + azithromycin (RR, 0.84; 95% CI, .69–1.02), moxifloxacin and beta-lactam + azithromycin (RR, 2.00; 95% CI, .20–20.33), moxifloxacin and betalactam + doxycycline (RR, 1.40; 95% CI, .13–14.76), and moxifloxacin and levofloxacin (RR, 2.39; 95% CI, .23–24.48).

## Safety Outcomes

No significant differences among the 4 interventions were observed for safety outcomes on direct meta-analysis or NMA. The summary of findings is presented in Table 1, Figure 3, and Supplementary Tables 4.1.b and 4.2.c [18].

## C difficile Infection

Six studies [1, 11, 12, 25, 27, 34] evaluated C difficile infection in individuals treated for CAP, comparing beta-lactam + azithromycin, beta-lactam + doxycycline, and fluoroquinolone (primarily levofloxacin, with some studies including moxifloxacin). Direct meta-analysis showed no statistically significant difference between beta-lactam + doxycycline and beta-lactam + azithromycin in increasing the risk of C difficile infection (RR, 0.91; 95% CI, .75–1.12; very low certainty). The NMA showed no significant differences between levofloxacin and betalactam + azithromycin (RR, 1.13; 95% CI, .48–2.67) or levofloxacin and beta-lactam + doxycycline (RR, 1.25; 95% CI, .42–3.76).

## QT Prolongation

One study [1] evaluated QT prolongation in individuals treated for CAP, comparing beta-lactam + azithromycin and betalactam + doxycycline. Direct meta-analysis showed no statistically significant difference between beta-lactam + doxycycline and beta-lactam + azithromycin in increasing the risk of QT prolongation (RR, 1.39; 95% CI, .70–2.78; very low certainty). No data were available regarding the QT prolongation for respiratory fluoroquinolones.

## DISCUSSION

This systematic review and NMA summarized the evidence from 20 studies, including RCTs and comparative observational studies, examining the efficacy and safety of azithromycin and doxycycline, each combined with beta-lactam antibiotic, as well as respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy, for the treatment of adults hospitalized with CAP.

Our findings showed no statistically significant differences among these treatment regimens across all evaluated outcomes, except for risk of ICU admission, treatment failure, and 30-day mortality, with NMA suggesting that levofloxacin may be associated with a lower risk of ICU admission and treatment failure when compared with beta-lactam + azithromycin and betalactam + doxycycline, respectively. Additionally, beta-lactam + doxycycline may be associated with increased risk of 30-day mortality as compared with beta-lactam + azithromycin. No significant differences were observed among the regimens in terms of safety outcomes, including C difficile infection and QT prolongation. Notably, no randomized trials directly compared beta-lactam + doxycycline vs beta-lactam + macrolide regimens, highlighting a persistent evidence gap.

The direct comparison displayed no significant difference in either in-hospital or 30-day mortality among the different treatment regimens, mirroring the results of several studies that showed similar mortality rates among individuals treated with beta-lactam + macrolide, beta-lactam + doxycycline, or respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy [1, 11, 12, 39, 40]. The NMA demonstrated similar results except for an increased risk of 30-day mortality with beta-lactam + doxycycline as compared with beta-lactam + azithromycin, aligning with the results of a recent large-matched cohort study [15], suggesting a possible advantage for macrolide-based regimens. However, the discrepancy between direct and indirect evidence and the absence of consistent mortality benefit among these studies should be interpreted with caution, as they highlight the multifactorial nature and drivers of CAP-related mortality and outcomes from system- or patient-level factors rather than treatment choice alone, thereby emphasizing the need for wellpowered RCTs that can address some of these gaps. Furthermore, the observed discordance between the increased 30-day mortality risk and shorter time to clinical stability with beta-lactam + doxycycline as compared with beta-lactam + azithromycin may reflect differences in the nature and robustness of these outcomes. Specifically, time to clinical stability was informed by only 2 studies with inconsistent findings: one suggesting a modest benefit with doxycycline and the other showing no meaningful difference, precluding meta-analysis and limiting confidence in this outcome. In contrast, mortality reflects a more distal and multifactorial endpoint, potentially influenced by factors beyond early clinical response, including complications and comorbid conditions.

Similarly, while the point estimate for 30-day mortality with levofloxacin vs beta-lactam + azithromycin was relatively large, the wide confidence interval and lack of statistical significance indicate substantial uncertainty and should be interpreted with caution, especially given the limited data regarding the levofloxacin arm within the network.

Although no significant differences were observed in the need for noninvasive ventilation across treatment regimens, our findings differ from prior meta-analyses and observational cohorts [41, 42] that reported a statistically significant reduction in ICU admissions with beta-lactam + azithromycin as compared with levofloxacin monotherapy. In contrast, the NMA demonstrated a modest but statistically significant reduction in the need for ICU admission associated with levofloxacin use as compared with beta lactam + azithromycin. While the previously reported benefit was not consistently observed across all studies, the majority of the impact appears most pronounced in individuals with severe pneumonia, delayed presentation, or high inflammatory markers. In these cases, the immunomodulatory properties of macrolide-containing regimens may reduce progression to critical illness and attenuate systemic inflammation, which could explain some of the difference noted between the NMA results and the previously reported results, especially given the limited data on pneumonia severity in many of the studies, as well as the exclusion of studies evaluating fluoroquinolones that lacked a direct comparison with azithromycin or doxycycline.

In general, no significant differences were reported in safety outcomes, including C difficile infection or QTC prolongation, among the different treatment regimens. This aligns with previously reported data, where no significant differences in serious adverse event rates were observed among these regimens in adults hospitalized with CAP. However, the regimen selection should still consider individual risk factors for specific adverse effects [12, 13, 15, 43, 44].

## Strengths and Limitations

This study is the most comprehensive and up-to-date systematic review and NMA comparing commonly used empiric antibiotic regimens for adults hospitalized with CAP. In the absence of head-to-head trials, the NMA approach incorporates randomized trials and comparative observational studies to capture a broad range of real-world clinical settings and patient populations. The use of a priori established protocol, a comprehensive search strategy across multiple databases, and input from content experts strengthened the methodological rigor of this review.

However, as with any systematic review, our study was limited by several factors, including the lack of microbiological confirmation across the studies, as most relied on clinical diagnosis for case ascertainment, which can increase the risk of ascertainment bias. Nonetheless, the use of comparable diagnostic criteria across studies resulted in largely consistent findings, mitigating concerns about substantial bias. Furthermore, the limited data available on safety outcomes and the lack of standardized definitions of clinical outcomes across studies resulted in wide confidence intervals and limited our ability to perform meaningful subgroup or sensitivity analyses. Additionally, as beta-lactam + doxycycline regimens are not recommended for empiric therapy in severe CAP and the number of eligible studies with severity-specific data was limited, severity-based subgroup analyses could not be performed. While our study provides robust insight into the major safety outcomes, the inconsistent reporting of mild adverse events limited our ability to comprehensively evaluate these outcomes and may have resulted in an underestimation of tolerability issues.

## CONCLUSION

The current evidence demonstrated no likely difference in outcomes between azithromycin and doxycycline in adults hospitalized with CAP receiving beta-lactam. This is the first NMA to shed light on these interventions, focusing on their efficacy and safety. Furthermore, the current analysis emphasizes the paucity and low certainty of evidence informing important aspects of the care of individuals with CAP. More RCTs are needed to help strengthen and validate our results.
