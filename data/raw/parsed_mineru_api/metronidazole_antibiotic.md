SYSTEMATIC REVIEW

# Treatment for Dientamoeba fragilis: A Systematic Review and Meta‑analysis

Nadav Baharav  · Michael Itzkovich · Eli Schwartz · Dafna Yahav

Received: February 7, 2026 / Accepted: March 10, 2026 / Published online: April 1, 2026   
© The Author(s) 2026

## ABSTRACT

Introduction: Dientamoeba fragilis (D. fragilis) is detected in stool tests of patients with gastrointestinal symptoms. Despite a debate over its pathogenicity, treatment is often prescribed, after excluding other reasons. The most commonly used therapy in practice is metronidazole; however, evidence to support this practice is limited.

Methods: We conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis compiling studies evaluating metronidazole vs any other regimen for the treatment of D.  fragilis. Studies reporting

treatment outcomes (clinical and/or microbiological) of D.  fragilis regimens diagnosed by standard stool microscopy and polymerase chain reaction. We searched PubMed, Cochrane library, and Web of Science up to March 2025 for relevant studies. The primary outcome was clinical response; microbiological response was a secondary outcome.

Results: We included 12 comparative studies in the quantitative meta-analysis and additional non-comparative studies in the qualitative systematic review. Two studies were randomized controlled trials (RCT), and others were observational studies. Nine studies were included in the analysis of the primary outcome. Signifcantly higher clinical response rates were demonstrated with paromomycin (odds ratio [OR] 2.31, 95% confdence interval [CI] 1.37–3.89, without heterogeneity) and clioquinol (odds ratio [OR] 2.52, 95% confdence interval [CI] 1.26–5.05, without heterogeneity) compared to metronidazole therapy. Signifcantly higher microbiological response was demonstrated with paromomycin (OR 3.94, 95% CI 2.75–5.65) and a trend for higher response for clioquinol (OR 1.41, 95% CI 0.68–2.92), compared with metronidazole.

Conclusions: Limited data compiled in this meta-analysis supports the use of paromomycin or clioquinol for treatment of D. fragilis symptomatic infection, in case a decision to treat this pathogen is taken. Future studies correlating

clinical effcacy with microbiological eradication are needed.

Keywords: Diarrhea; Dientamoeba fragilis; Metronidazole; Paromomycin; Therapy

## Key Summary Points

Dientamoeba fagilis is an intestinal organism commonly found in the stool of patients with gastrointestinal symptoms. Metronidazole is commonly prescribed for D. fagilis, with several other regimens reported in the literature. We conducted a systematic review and metaanalysis to compare the clinical and microbiological success of metronidazole with other reported regimens, namely paromomycin and clioquinol. Signifcantly higher clinical response rates were demonstrated with paromomycin and clioquinol, compared to metronidazole. Limited data compiled in this meta-analysis supports the use of paromomycin or clioquinol for treatment of D. fagilis symptomatic infection.

## INTRODUCTION

The intestinal protozoa Dientamoeba fragilis (D. fragilis) was frst isolated over a century ago. Ever since its discovery, it has been debated whether it contributes to intestinal disease or serves solely as a gut commensal [1]. First described as a non-pathogenic amoeba, prevalence studies demonstrated higher prevalence of D. fragilis in control-group patients than in patients with gastrointestinal symptoms [2–4]. A recent prospective cohort study in adults demonstrated that while antiparasitic therapy led to signifcantly higher rates of parasitological clearance, there was no signifcant difference in symptom improvement [5]. Similarly, a double-blind, placebo-controlled randomized trial in children found that metronidazole resulted in higher rates of D. fragilis eradication, but  did not improve gastrointestinal symptoms compared to placebo [6].

However, several case-series and observational studies found a correlation between infection and symptoms as well as between eradication and symptom relief after treatment [7–11]. Several studies have shown an association between higher parasite loads and gastrointestinal symptoms, suggesting a pathogenic role [12]. Moreover, animal models of infected mice showed colonic infammation and cyst infectivity, contributing to the perception of D. fragilis pathogenic nature [13].

Conventionally, D. fragilis was detected by microscopic examination of the stool. In order to achieve higher sensitivity, examination of the stool is performed immediately after defecation, which is logistically complex. Moreover, secretion is phasic and parasite load can change signifcantly even between consecutive days, making wet mount highly insensitive [14]. To overcome this obstacle, laboratories use the trichrome staining or the three feces test (TFT), combining multiple sampling and fxing over 3 days to achieve higher sensitivity [15]. However, microscopic methods are time-consuming, logistically complex, and still provide relatively low sensitivity of detection. Real-time polymerase chain reaction (PCR) has a superior sensitivity and specifcity, with favorable logistic and practical considerations [16]. Moreover, recent multiplex-PCR commercial kits are already validated and in use in clinical practice and provide diagnosis of several intestinal parasites, including D. fragilis. These commercial kits have shown more consistent accuracy compared to laboratory-developed PCR assay [17].

Despite the uncertainty regarding its pathogenicity, textbooks and clinical guidelines are recommending treatment in symptomatic patients with D. fragilis, when other pathogens are excluded [18, 19]. However, the treatment of choice is still under debate and metronidazole, which seems to be highly effective in vitro, did not show promising results in one randomized prospective trial in children [6]. Other treatment options reported in the literature include paromomycin, clioquinol, and ornidazole [1].

In this systematic review and meta-analysis, we aimed to compile the existing evidence on the clinical and microbiologic effectiveness of different treatment regimens for D. fragilis.

## METHODS

This systematic review and meta-analysis was performed in accordance with the PRISMA guidelines [20]. Studies eligible for inclusion were randomized controlled trials (RCTs), cohort studies, case–control studies, and case series of 20 patients or more. We included any of these studies reporting treatment outcomes (clinical and/or microbiological) of D. fragilis regimens. For the meta-analysis, only comparative studies were included; for the systematic review we included also non-comparative studies reporting treatment outcomes. The population for inclusion comprised patients of all ages, pediatric and adult, treated for D. fragilis. Studies were classifed as overall addressing children or adults according to the majority of participants (> 50%).

Diagnosis of D. fragilis by microscopy methods included only standard TFT and/or immediate fxation and staining. PCR methods were also included. Interventions for inclusion were any antiparasitic drug regimen compared with another regimen, placebo or no treatment for comparative studies and any treatment regimen in non-comparative studies. Forest plots were subcategorized by microbiological diagnostic method—PCR or microscopy.

Studies including non-pharmacological interventions were excluded. Studies designed as case reports or case series including fewer than 20 participants were also excluded.

We focused on the comparison of metronidazole, the most commonly used drug, versus any other regimen, subcategorized for paromomycin, clioquinol, or placebo.

We searched the PubMed, Web of Science, and Embase databases until March 31, 2025. The search string combined the terms “dientamoeba OR dientamoebiasis” with “Treatment

OR therapy OR eradication OR therapeutic OR therapeutics OR therapies OR clioquinol OR secnidazole OR metronidazole OR tinidazole OR paromomycin”. No restrictions on publication year, language, or publication status were implemented.

The primary outcome was clinical response to antiparasitic treatment, as defned in individual studies (see Supplemental Table 1 for defnitions of primary outcome in individual included studies). Secondary outcomes included microbiological response, defned as eradication of D. fragilis by stool follow-up microscopy or PCR (negative result) and adverse events. We searched for data on any adverse events, serious adverse events, adverse events requiring discontinuation of study drug, gastrointestinal adverse events (diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain), and neurological adverse events (peripheral neuropathy or others).

Two independent reviewers performed the search, retrieved relevant publications meeting inclusion criteria, and extracted the data from all included studies. Methodological quality was assessed using the Newcastle–Ottawa Scale (NCOS) for cohort studies [21]. Discrepancies between two reviewers were resolved after a discussion with a third reviewer. Data extracted included demographics and clinical data, diagnostic method, outcomes, and adverse events. The meta-analysis was conducted using the Cochrane Review Manager [22]. Heterogeneity was evaluated using the chi-square-based Q test and the I 2 measure of inconsistency [23]. A random-effect model was used and pooled odds ratios (ORs) with 95% confdence intervals (CIs) were calculated. For outcomes of non-randomized studies, we aimed to extract both unadjusted and adjusted effect estimates. Sensitivity analysis was planned according to methodological quality (NOCS 7–9 vs lower). A funnel plot to estimate potential small studies effect was not examined because of the small number of studies included.

This article is based on previously conducted studies; it does not contain new studies or participants. Ethical approval and informed consent were not required in accordance with the policies of our institution regarding meta-analysis of previously published studies.

## RESULTS

The literature search yielded overall 401 publications. After fltering of duplicates and applying inclusion and exclusion criteria, 16 studies were eligible for inclusion in the qualitative analysis and 12 studies in the quantitative analysis. The study fowchart is presented in Fig.  1. Characteristics of the included studies are detailed in Table 1. Treatment success rates (in qualitative and quantitative studies) are detailed in Table 2. Eight non-randomized studies compared metronidazole versus paromomycin [10, 24–29]; fve non-randomized studies compared metronidazole versus clioquinol [3, 5, 11, 30]; one study compared both paromomycin and clioquinol to metronidazole [31]; two RCTs compared metronidazole vs placebo and metronidazole vs ornidazole [6, 9]. None of the studies was conducted in low-middle income countries (15 studies— high-income countries and one upper-middleincome country, see Table 1) [32]. Five studies included only children [3, 6, 11, 30, 33], and the others included mostly adults. Ten studies used PCR as main diagnostic method [3, 5, 6, 24–27, 30, 33, 34] and six used microscopy methods [9–11, 28, 29, 31]. Most commonly reported symptoms were abdominal pain and diarrhea.

For the primary outcome of clinical response, nine studies (805 patients) were included, six compared metronidazole with paromomycin and three metronidazole with clioquinol (for individual studies defnition of clinical response, see Supplemental Table 1). Both comparators demonstrated signifcantly higher response rates compared to metronidazole (paromomycin, 6 studies, OR 2.31, 95% confdence interval [CI] 1.37–3.89, without heterogeneity; clioquinol, 3 studies, OR 2.52, 95% confdence interval [CI] 1.26–5.05, without heterogeneity), (Fig. 2). Roser el al. compared metronidazole with placebo and evaluated as their primary outcome the change in level of gastrointestinal symptoms, using a visual analog scale (VAS) score. Mean VAS change was not signifcantly different between metronidazole and placebo (p = 0.8) [6].

Microbiological response was reported from ten studies (1443 patients). Six studies compared metronidazole with paromomycin and five with clioquinol (one study included both comparisons). Paromomycin presented signifcantly higher microbiological response compared with metronidazole (OR 3.94, 95% CI 2.75–5.65). No signifcant difference was demonstrated between clioquinol and metronidazole (OR 1.41, 95% CI 0.68–2.92), (Fig. 3). Signifcant heterogenicity was demonstrated in microbiological outcome for studies with microscopy diagnosis. However, this was due to effect size rather than direction of the effect (studies favored comparators to metronidazole).

Adverse events were reported only from the RCT by Roser et al., reporting no serious adverse events among 54 children who received metronidazole versus 52 who received placebo. Rates of nausea and vomiting were higher with metronidazole (65% vs 42%; and 33% vs 10%, respectively) [6].

Methodological assessment of the included studies in presented in Supplemental Table 2. All included studies, besides the RCTs by Roser et al. and Kurt et al., were evaluated using NCOS for observational studies, with seven studies rated as NCOS of less than 7. Hence, sensitivity analysis according to methodological quality was performed. Including only studies rated as NCOS at least 7, the comparison of metronidazole vs paromomycin included only two studies for the outcome of clinical response and four studies for the outcome of microbiological response, with no change in results (signifcantly higher response with paromomycin). The comparison of metronidazole vs clioquinol included no studies for the outcome of clinical response and only two studies for the outcome of microbiological response, showing no difference between the drugs for the latter. (See Supplemental Figs. 1–4.)

## DISCUSSION

In this systematic review and meta-analysis, nine studies evaluating metronidazole vs other regimens for the treatment of D. fragilis were included. Metronidazole demonstrated signifcantly lower clinical response compared to paromomycin or clioquinol. Microbiological response was signifcantly higher for paromomycin and without signifcant difference compared to clioquinol.

Current recommendations for the management of D. fragilis include treatment for persistent symptoms in the absence of other pathogens [18, 19]. In practice, metronidazole is the most known and used drug in clinical practice, despite low treatment success rates described in the literature, and the results of an RCT showing no signifcant difference in clinical outcomes compared to placebo. The substantial number of patients in the placebo group achieving parasite eradication in this trial may suggest a self-limiting nature of this pathogen in some patients [6].

Previous experience suggests that treatment response of D. fragilis should be based on both clinical and microbiological response (i.e., eradication of parasite from stool, as demonstrated by negative microscopy or PCR); hence, we chose to focus on PCR and microscopy diagnosis and follow-up in addition to clinical outcomes, and included eradication in the list of outcomes [34, 35].

Real-time PCR has a superior sensitivity and specifcity, with favorable logistic and practical considerations [16]. In Israel, since the switch from stool microscopy test to multiplex PCR test, D. fragilis is the most common protozoa detected in stool in symptomatic returning travelers [36] and in the symptomatic general population as well [37] ranging from 19% to 29% of stool samples, respectively. Similar data from other regions are limited [38]. Nevertheless, since some cases are still treated, fnding the optimal treatment regimen is of importance.

One study by Pietilä [10] demonstrated lower clinical response of paromomycin (41%) compared with other studies (100%) and had signifcant weight (85%) in the analysis. We believe that this difference may lie with the fact that clinical cure was defned as complete symptom resolution, making this outcome difficult to achieve compared to clinical improvement used by other studies.

Metronidazole is well absorbed and treatment includes commonly reported systemic adverse reactions such as nausea and headache to rare but more serious neuropathy and encephalopathy [39]. Paromomycin is not absorbed in the gut with zero systemic absorption and effect. This characteristic allows high intestinal concentrations and suggested preferable effectivity in the gut lumen, and also its excellent reported safety and tolerability profle [8, 25]. These properties make paromomycin more appealing for use.

Our meta-analysis is limited by the small number of included studies, their design, and quality. Clinical response is a subjective outcome and its defnition varied between studies. Nevertheless, no heterogeneity was observed between studies reporting this outcome, supporting the fnding of improved clinical response with paromomycin or clioquinol compared to metronidazole. Considering the nature of the included studies, raw data were compiled in this metaanalysis, probably exaggerating the inherent bias in observational studies. In addition, most studies were small, retrospective, and with high risk of bias. Our analysis included both pediatric and adult population in the same analysis, disregarding the differences between these two groups of patients. However, subgroup analysis was not feasible as several studies included both groups without stratifed outcomes reported. Moreover, our analysis used both microscopy and PCR as the diagnostic method, with all the aforementioned disadvantages of traditional microscopy methods. It should also be stated that the implications of microbiological response measured by PCR, detecting genetic material rather than viable organism, is not well understood. Five studies included in the quantitative analysis did not report control sample timing, further compromising our results, since longer follow-up has been associated with positive post-treatment samples, possibly due to reinfection [6, 29]. None of the included studies were conducted in low-middle-income countries, limiting the generalizability of our results to such regions.

## CONCLUSIONS

Over a century after its discovery, D.  fragilis still remains elusive and controversial. The limited success of treatment of D. fragilis with metronidazole may have also contributed to approaching this organism as non-pathogenic. Nevertheless, reports with no signifcant beneft of metronidazole over placebo along with our results demonstrating signifcantly higher clinical response with paromomycin or clioquinol over metronidazole imply that if a decision is taken to treat D. fragilis infection, paromomycin or clioquinol should be the preferred therapy. RCTs comparing different regimens are needed.
