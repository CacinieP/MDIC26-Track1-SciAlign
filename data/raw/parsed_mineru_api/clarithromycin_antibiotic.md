# Pharmacokinetic Drug–Drug Interactions of DA-8010 with Clarithromycin or Rifampicin: Influence of CYP2D6 Phenotype on the Extent of These Interactions

Sujong Lee 1,2 , Heejae Won 1,2 , Kyung-Sang Yu 1,2 , Dae Young Lee 3 , SeungHwan Lee

1 Department of Clinical Pharmacology and Therapeutics, Seoul National University College of Medicine and Hospital, Seoul, Republic of Korea; 2 Department of Biomedical Sciences, Seoul National University College of Medicine, Seoul, Republic of Korea; 3 Dong-A ST Research Institute, Yongin, Republic of Korea

Correspondence: SeungHwan Lee, Department of Clinical Pharmacology and Therapeutics, Seoul National University College of Medicine and Hospital, Seoul, Republic of Korea, Email leejh413@snu.ac.kr

Purpose: This study aimed to evaluate the pharmacokinetic (PK) drug–drug interactions (DDIs) of DA-8010 with clarithromycin or rifampicin, and to assess the influence of cytochrome P450 (CYP) 2D6 phenotype on the extent of these interactions.

Methods: An open-label, fixed-sequence, three-period crossover study was conducted in healthy participants. Participants received a single dose of DA-8010 5 mg alone on Day 1 (Period 1); clarithromycin 500 mg twice daily from Day 6 to 11, with a concomitant single dose of DA-8010 5 mg on Day 10 (Period 2); and rifampicin 600 mg once daily from Day 16 to 25, with a concomitant single dose of DA-8010 5 mg on Day 25 (Period 3). Each treatment period was separated by a five-day washout. Serial blood samples for PK assessments of DA-8010 were collected up to 48 h post-dose. CYP2D6 genotyping was performed retrospectively, and phenotypes were assigned based on activity score.

Results: Sixteen participants completed the study: eleven were classified as CYP2D6 extensive metabolizers (EMs) and five as CYP2D6 intermediate metabolizers (IMs). Co-administration of DA-8010 with clarithromycin had no significant effect on the PKs of DA-8010. In contrast, co-administration with rifampicin reduced the systemic exposure of DA-8010 by approximately 50% compared with DA-8010 alone. Across all treatment conditions, DA-8010 exposure was approximately two-fold higher in IMs than in EMs.

Conclusion: DA-8010 showed no clinically meaningful PK interaction with clarithromycin, while rifampicin significantly reduced DA-8010 exposure. Although the CYP2D6 phenotype affected overall DA-8010 exposure, it did not alter the extent of the PK interaction observed with clarithromycin or rifampicin.

Keywords: muscarinic receptor antagonist, drug interactions, CYP3A4 modulation, CYP2D6 polymorphism, pharmacokinetics

## Introduction

Antimuscarinics are the first-line pharmacotherapy for overactive bladder (OAB), a condition characterized by urinary urgency, frequency, and incontinence.1 Although the exact etiology of OAB is not fully understood, prevailing theories suggest that detrusor overactivity alters the sensory mechanisms of the bladder, resulting in a sensation of urgency at lower bladder volumes.2 Bladder contraction is primarily regulated by the parasympathetic nervous system through stimulation of muscarinic receptor subtype-3 $\left( \mathbf { M } _ { 3 } \right)$ in the detrusor muscle of the urinary bladder.3 Antimuscarinics alleviate OAB symptoms by inhibiting muscarinic receptor activation, thereby promoting detrusor muscle relaxation.4

DA-8010 ([(3R)-1-Methylpyrrolidin-3-yl]methyl N-[2-(3-chloro-4-fluorophenyl)phenyl]carbamate) is a novel M3 muscarinic receptor antagonist under development for the treatment of OAB. In an in vitro muscarinic receptor binding assay, DA-8010 showed the highest binding affinity for the human M3 muscarinic receptor (Ki 1.55 nM), outperforming other antimuscarinics including solifenacin, tolterodine, oxybutynin, and darifenacin.5,6 Additionally, DA-8010 exhibited greater bladder selectivity by antagonizing muscarinic receptors in mouse bladder smooth muscle cells than in salivary gland cells, suggesting a lower risk of anticholinergic side effects such as dry mouth.6 In a first-in-human (FIH) study, DA-8010 was well tolerated and demonstrated dose-proportional systemic exposure across single and multiple dose ranges of 1–40 mg and 5–20 mg respectively.7 As only 0.115–0.216% of the administered dose was excreted unchanged in urine, hepatic metabolism is presumed to be the primary elimination route.7

In in vitro studies, DA-8010 did not significantly inhibit or induce microsomal cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzymes (unpublished data on file, Dong-A ST). Metabolic screening using human cDNA-expressed CYP enzymes identified CYP2D6 as the primary enzyme responsible for DA-8010 metabolism (fraction metabolized, $\mathrm { f _ { m } } = 8 4 \% )$ , with CYP3A4 serving as a minor contributing enzyme $( \mathrm { f _ { m } } = 7 \% )$ (unpublished data on file, Dong-A ST). Further metabolite profiling in the FIH study identified four notable metabolites: two cleavage products (M1 and M17) and two oxidized products (M13 and H10).7 Among these, M13 and H10 were predominantly formed by CYP2D6 and CYP3A4, supporting the role of these enzymes in DA-8010 metabolism.

CYP enzymes play a critical role in hepatic metabolism, with CYP2D6 and CYP3A4 serving as the primary metabolic pathways for many other antimuscarinics.8 Given the high prevalence of OAB in elderly populations, who are frequently exposed to polypharmacy, the likelihood of co-administration with CYP3A4 modulators, such as macro lide antibiotics or azole antifungals, is a clinically relevant concern.9,10 Accordingly, evaluating the pharmacokinetic (PK) interactions between DA-8010 and CYP3A4 modulators is essential to ensure the safe and effective use of DA-8010 in this population.

While DA-8010 is primarily metabolized by CYP2D6, the contribution of CYP3A4 may become more pronounced in individuals with reduced CYP2D6 activity. This has the potential to increase susceptibility to drug–drug interactions (DDIs) involving CYP3A4 inhibitors or inducers.11 In this context, CYP2D6 genotyping is informative not only for understanding interindividual variability in DA-8010 PKs but also for determining whether the extent of CYP3A4- mediated DDIs differs according to CYP2D6 phenotype.

According to guidance from the US Food and Drug Administration, clinical drug interaction studies should use highpotency inhibitors and inducers to achieve the most sensitive assessment of potential PK interactions.12 In this study, clarithromycin and rifampicin were selected as perpetrator drugs due to their well-established roles as potent CYP3A4 inhibitors and inducers, respectively.13 DA-8010 has demonstrated dose-dependent improvements in OAB symptoms at doses of 2.5 mg and 5 mg compared with placebo.14 To maximize the likelihood of detecting meaningful PK interactions, the 5 mg dose, representing the upper end of the clinical dosing range, was selected for evaluation.

This study aimed to evaluate the PK DDIs of DA-8010 with clarithromycin or rifampicin, and to assess the influence of CYP2D6 phenotype on the extent of these interactions.

## Materials and Methods

The study protocol was approved by the Korean Ministry of Food and Drug Safety and the Institutional Review Board of Seoul National University Hospital (Approval number: H-2307-087-1450, ClinicalTrials.gov identifier: NCT05991401). This study was conducted in compliance with the Declaration of Helsinki and the Korean Good Clinical Practice guidelines. Written informed consent was obtained from all participants prior to any procedure related to the study.

## Study Population

Healthy Korean volunteers aged 19–50 years with a body mass index (BMI) between 18.0 and 28.0 kg/m2 were eligible for enrolment if they were deemed healthy based on their medical history, physical examination, 12-lead electrocardio gram (ECG), vital signs, and clinical laboratory test results. Individuals with any medical history or condition that could potentially affect the PKs of the study drug, including clinically significant hepatic or renal impairment, were excluded. During the study, participants were instructed to abstain from consuming grapefruit juice and from taking any other medications or supplements known to modulate CYP enzyme activity. Detailed inclusion and exclusion criteria are provided in Supplementary Information 1.

## Study Design

An open-label, fixed-sequence, three-period, crossover study was conducted in healthy participants (Figure 1). Participants received a single oral dose of DA-8010 5 mg (Dong-A ST., Seoul, Korea) alone on Day 1 (Period 1); clarithromycin 500 mg (Klaricid® Film Coated Tab. 500 mg, Abbott Korea, Seoul, Korea) twice daily from Day 6 to 11 and once on the last day, with a concomitant single dose of DA-8010 5 mg on Day 10 (Period 2); and rifampicin 600 mg (Rifodex® Tab. 600 mg, Chong Kun Dang Pharmaceutical Corp., Seoul, Korea) once daily from Day 16 to 25, with a concomitant single oral dose of DA-8010 5 mg on Day 25 (Period 3). Each treatment period was separated by a fiveday washout from the last dosing of the preceding period, based on the turnover half-life of CYP3A4 and the terminal elimination half-life $\left( \mathrm { t } _ { 1 / 2 } \right)$ of the study drugs.12

Serial blood samples for PK assessments of DA-8010 were collected pre-dose and at 1, 2, 3, 3.5, 4, 4.5, 5, 5.5, 6, 6.5, 7, 8, 12, 24, and 48 h post-dose on Day 1, 10, and 25. At each blood sampling point, approximately 3 mL of blood was collected into EDTA K2 tubes and centrifuged at 3,000 rpm for 10 min at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . The plasma aliquots were stored at $- 7 0 \mathrm { { } ^ { \circ } C }$ until analysis.

## Bioanalytical Method

Plasma concentrations of DA-8010 were quantified using a validated liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (LC– MS/MS) method. Analyses were conducted using a Waters ACQUITY UPLC system (Waters, Milford, MA, USA) coupled with a Xevo TQ-XS triple quadrupole mass spectrometer (Waters, Wilmslow, UK) operated in positive electrospray ionization mode. Chromatographic separation was achieved using an ACQUITY UPLC BEH C18 column (2.1 × 50 mm, 1.7 μm particle size; Waters, Milford, MA, USA) maintained at $3 0 \pm 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ The mobile phase consisted of water containing 0.1% (w/v) ammonium formate and acetonitrile, delivered at a flow rate of 0.4 mL/min under gradient elution (Table S1). Multiple reaction monitoring transitions were set at m/z 363.30 → 98.20 for DA-8010 and m/z 366.30 → 101.22 for the internal standard, $\mathrm { D A } – 8 0 1 0 – \mathrm { d } _ { 3 } .$ Calibration curves were validated over a concentration range of 0.00500–5.00 ng/mL. The accuracy of quality control samples ranged from 95.3% to 97.2%, with imprecision below 5.7%.

## Pharmacokinetic Assessments

PK parameters were calculated using non-compartmental analysis with Phoenix WinNonlin 8.4 (Pharsight, Sunnyvale, CA, USA). The maximum observed plasma concentration $\mathrm { ( C _ { m a x } ) }$ and the time to reach $\mathrm { { C _ { \mathrm { { m a x } } } \left( \mathrm { { T _ { \mathrm { { m a x } } } } } \right) } }$ were determined directly from the observed plasma concentration–time profiles. Plasma concentrations below the lower limit of quantification (LLOQ) were treated as zero when occurring prior to $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ and as missing value when occurring after $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { m a x } } .$ The area under the concentration–time curve from time zero to the last measurable plasma concentration $\left( \mathrm { { A U C } _ { \mathrm { { l a s t } } } } \right)$ was calculated using the linear-up/log-down trapezoidal method. The terminal elimination rate constant $( \lambda _ { z } )$ was estimated by log-linear regression of the terminal phase concentrations that provided the best goodness-of-fit, as determined by the

## CYP2D6 Genotyping and Phenotyping

Blood samples for CYP2D6 genotyping were collected prior to the first administration of the study drug and analyzed retrospectively. Genomic DNA was extracted from whole blood and analyzed using the SPMED Genotyping Kit: CYP2D6 (SPMED, Busan, Korea). Genotyping was performed via multiplex polymerase chain reaction followed by multiplex allele-specific primer extension, using a Veriti 96-Well Thermal Cycler and a 3500 Dx Genetic Analyzer (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA, USA). The assay covered a comprehensive panel of CYP2D6 alleles, including \*2, \*3, \*4, \*5, \*6, \*9, \*10, \*14, \*17, \*18, \*21, \*29, \*41, \*49, \*52, and \*60, as well as CYP2D6 gene duplications.

Based on the genotyping results, participants were classified into one of four CYP2D6 metabolizer phenotypes: ultrarapid metabolizer (UM), extensive metabolizer (EM), intermediate metabolizer (IM), and poor metabolizer (PM), according to the Clinical Pharmacogenetics Implementation Consortium guidelines.15 Phenotypes were assigned using the CYP2D6 activity score system, where activity scores of >2.25 were classified as UM, scores between 1.25 and 2.25 as EM, scores from 0.25 to 1.0 as IM, and a score of 0 as PM.

## Safety Assessments

Safety was assessed throughout the study by monitoring adverse events (AEs), physical examinations, 12-lead ECGs, vital signs, and clinical laboratory tests, including hematology, blood chemistry, coagulation, and urinalysis. All AEs were recorded and followed until resolution, and AEs were summarized by treatment period and were categorized by severity (mild, moderate, or severe) and their relationship to the study drug. Abnormal values in 12-lead ECGs, vital signs and clinical laboratory tests were evaluated as clinically significant or not clinically significant, and adequate clinical explanation was established for each clinically significant abnormal value.

## Statistical Analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using SAS 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC, USA). Differences in demographic characteristics between CYP2D6 phenotypes were assessed using the Mann–Whitney U-test for continuous variables. To evaluate the effects of treatment and CYP2D6 phenotype on the PKs of DA-8010, analysis of variance (ANOVA) was performed on log-transformed PK parameters $( \mathrm { C } _ { \mathrm { m a x } } , \mathrm { A U C _ { \mathrm { l a s t } } }$ , and $\mathrm { A U C } _ { \mathrm { i n f } } )$ using a linear mixed-effects model. In each analysis, the model included treatment or phenotype as a fixed effect and participant as a random effect; period was not included because its effect was confounded with treatment due to the fixed-sequence design. Based on the ANOVA results, geometric mean ratios (GMRs) and corresponding 90% confidence intervals (CIs) were calculated to compare the PK parameters between the co-administration of DA-8010 with clarithromycin or rifampicin versus DA-8010 alone, as well as between phenotypes. Additionally, nonparametric comparisons of PK parameters between CYP2D6 phenotypes were conducted using the Mann–Whitney U-test. A P-value of <0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## Effect of CYP3A4 Inhibition or Induction on the Pharmacokinetics of DA-8010

Co-administration of DA-8010 with clarithromycin, a strong CYP3A4 inhibitor, had no significant effect on the PKs of DA-8010 (Figure 2 and Table 2). The median $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ was comparable between the two treatments, while the mean $\mathrm { t } _ { 1 / 2 }$ was prolonged from 5.58 h to 7.59 h in the presence of clarithromycin. The GMRs (90% CI) for co-administration with clarithromycin relative to DA-8010 alone were 0.77 (0.62–0.96) for $\mathrm { { C } _ { \mathrm { { m a x } } } }$ , 1.01 (0.88–1.16) for $\mathrm { \Delta A U C _ { \mathrm { l a s t } } }$ , and 1.04 (0.91–1.19) for $\mathrm { A U C } _ { \mathrm { i n f } } .$

In contrast, co-administration with rifampicin, a potent CYP3A4 inducer, significantly reduced the systemic exposure of DA-8010 by approximately 50% compared with DA-8010 administration alone (Figure 2 and Table 2). The median $\mathrm { T } _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ remained unchanged, while the mean $\mathrm { t } _ { 1 / 2 }$ increased slightly from 5.58 h to 6.45 h. The GMRs (90% CI) for coadministration with rifampicin relative to DA-8010 alone were 0.42 (0.34–0.53) for $\mathrm { C _ { m a x } } , 0 . 5 3 ( 0 . 4 6 { - 0 . 6 0 } )$ for $\mathrm { { A U C } _ { \mathrm { { l a s t } } } , }$ and 0.55 (0.48–0.62) for $\mathbf { A U C } _ { \mathrm { i n f } } .$

## Effect of CYP2D6 Phenotype on the Pharmacokinetics of DA-8010

Across all treatment conditions, the systemic exposure of DA-8010 was approximately two-fold higher in IMs than in EMs (Figure 3 and Table 3). This consistent increase in exposure indicates reduced metabolic clearance in IMs relative to EMs, highlighting the influence of CYP2D6 phenotype on the PKs of DA-8010 (Figure 4).

The extent of change in systemic exposure following CYP3A4 modulation was comparable between EMs and IMs, suggesting a similar contribution of CYP3A4 to DA-8010 metabolism across CYP2D6 phenotypes (Figure 4 and Table 4). After co-administration with clarithromycin, the GMRs (90% CI) for $\mathrm { \Delta A U C _ { \mathrm { l a s t } } }$ relative to DA-8010 alone were 1.00 (0.85–1.18) in EMs and 1.04 (0.76–1.41) in IMs, while the corresponding GMRs (90% CI) for $\mathrm { { C } _ { \mathrm { { m a x } } } }$ were 0.76 (0.61–0.95) and 0.79 (0.45–1.40), respectively. Similarly, rifampicin co-administration resulted in GMRs for $\mathrm { { A U C } _ { \mathrm { { l a s t } } } }$ of 0.52 (0.44–0.61) in EMs and 0.54 (0.40–0.74) in IMs, and corresponding GMRs for $\mathrm { { C } _ { \mathrm { { m a x } } } }$ of 0.43 (0.34–0.53) and 0.42 (0.24–0.75), respectively.

## Discussion

This study evaluated the PK DDIs of DA-8010 in the presence of clarithromycin, a strong CYP3A4 inhibitor, or rifampicin, a potent CYP3A4 inducer, and assessed the influence of CYP2D6 phenotype on the extent of these interactions. Although the maximum approved daily doses for clarithromycin and rifampicin are 500 mg three times daily and 600 mg twice daily, respectively, pretreatment regimens of clarithromycin 500 mg twice daily for four days and rifampicin 600 mg once daily for nine days were employed to ensure participant safety while achieving sufficient CYP3A4 inhibition and induction.17,18 These regimens are considered adequate to induce steady-state modulation of CYP3A4 activity, as supported by prior literature.19–22

An open-label, fixed-sequence study design was adopted to minimize potential carryover effects associated with CYP3A4 modulation. DA-8010 was first administered alone, followed by co-administration with clarithromycin and subsequently with rifampicin. This sequence was selected based on the differing recovery profiles of CYP3A4 modulation, as clarithromycin exerts transient, time-dependent inhibition, whereas rifampicin induces more sustained enzyme activity through transcriptional upregulation.23–25 The five-day washout interval, exceeding five $\mathrm { t } _ { 1 / 2 }$ of DA-8010 and clarithromycin, was considered sufficient to allow recovery of CYP3A4 activity.17,23 Although the open-label design may introduce potential bias, its impact is likely limited because PK outcomes were based on objectively quantified plasma concentration data obtained under standardized study procedures.

Co-administration of DA-8010 with clarithromycin had no clinically meaningful effect on the PKs of DA-8010. Although the $\mathrm { { C } _ { \mathrm { { m a x } } } }$ decreased by 23%, the $\mathrm { \Delta A U C _ { \mathrm { l a s t } } }$ and $\mathrm { \sf A U C } _ { \mathrm { i n f } }$ remained unchanged. Given clarithromycin’s potent inhibitory effect on CYP3A4 and the high variability in DA-8010 PKs, this reduction in $\mathrm { { C } _ { \mathrm { { m a x } } } }$ is more likely attributable to inter- and intra-individual variability rather than a true PK DDI.7,26 The increase in $\mathrm { t } _ { 1 / 2 }$ observed after coadministration with clarithromycin may reflect a modest reduction in clearance due to CYP3A4 inhibition; however, the magnitude of this change was limited and not accompanied by a corresponding increase in systemic exposure.

These findings raise the possibility that the reduction in DA-8010 exposure after co-administration with rifampicin may involve alternative mechanisms, such as induction of intestinal efflux transporters leading to reduced oral absorption. However, available in vitro data indicate that DA-8010 is not a substrate of major efflux transporters, including P-glycoprotein (P-gp) and breast cancer resistance protein (BCRP) (unpublished data on file, Dong-A ST). Bidirectional permeability studies conducted in Madin–Darby canine kidney II cells (MDCKII) transfected with human P-gp and MDCKII-BCRP cell monolayers demonstrated high passive permeability of DA-8010 with low efflux ratios and comparable transport in both directions, supporting the absence of active efflux mediated by these transporters under the investigated conditions. Accordingly, the mechanism underlying the rifampicin-mediated reduction in DA-8010 exposure remains incompletely understood, and further investigations are considered to clarify the potential roles of alternative metabolic enzymes, transporter systems, or other absorption-related processes.

Across all treatment conditions, the systemic exposure of DA-8010 was approximately two-fold higher in IMs than in EMs. These observations are consistent with preclinical findings that identified CYP2D6 as the primary enzyme responsible for DA-8010 metabolism. Notably, the GMRs of $\mathrm { C } _ { \mathrm { m a x } , \mathrm { A U C } _ { \mathrm { l a s t } } , }$ and $\mathbf { A U C } _ { \mathrm { i n f } }$ between EMs and IMs remained consistent regardless of co-administration with clarithromycin or rifampicin. These results support the assertion that CYP2D6 is non-inducible and suggest that the CYP2D6 phenotype does not influence the extent of CYP3A4-mediated interactions on DA-8010 PKs. Taken together, these findings confirm that DA-8010 metabolism is primarily mediated by CYP2D6 and that alterations in CYP3A4 activity have a limited effect on its overall metabolic profile.

This study has several limitations. First, CYP2D6 genotyping was conducted retrospectively, and the PK and safety profiles of DA-8010 were not assessed in PMs due to their low prevalence in the Korean population which predominantly consists of EMs.31 Larger prospective studies that include PMs may improve the generalizability of these findings. Second, this study evaluated the PKs of DA-8010 after single-dose administration only. Although previous studies have reported that the overall PK profile of DA-8010 is comparable between single-dose and multiple-dose administrations, the extent of DDIs, particularly in the presence of CYP enzyme inhibitors or inducers, may differ under steady-state conditions. Therefore, further investigation may be considered to evaluate the DDI potential of DA-8010 at steady state.

## Conclusion

DA-8010 showed no clinically meaningful PK interaction with clarithromycin, while rifampicin significantly reduced DA-8010 exposure. Although the CYP2D6 phenotype affected overall DA-8010 exposure, it did not alter the extent of the PK interaction observed with clarithromycin or rifampicin.
