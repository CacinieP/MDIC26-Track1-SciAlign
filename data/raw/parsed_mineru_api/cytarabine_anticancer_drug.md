# Fixed-duration cytarabine consolidation improves relapse-free survival over indefinite AZA–VEN in younger AML

© The Author(s) 2026

Blood Cancer Journal (2026) 16:61 ; https://doi.org/

10.1038/s41408-026-01492-0

## To the Editor,

The treatment of newly diagnosed Acute Myeloid Leukaemia (ND-AML) requires a personalised approach based on age, comorbidities, and functional status of a patient. The azacitidine–venetoclax (Aza-Ven) regimen, though initially approved for elderly or unfit patients, is now increasingly used in younger adults (<65 years) who are not suitable for intensive chemotherapy. Real-world evidence from the UK NHS reports that 28–35% of patients receiving Aza-Ven were under 65 years old [1], while other studies [2] report similar trends, with median ages between 45 and 55 years [3–5]. Although Aza-Ven achieves remission rates of 60–70%, long-term follow-up shows that the median relapse-free survival(RFS) remains only 10–12 months [6]. Long-term treatment also poses several challenges, like severe (grade 3–4) myelosuppression, frequent dose interruptions, treatment discontinuation, and the development of drug resistance with continuous exposure [7–9]. Given these limitations, fixed-duration consolidation with high-dose or intermediate-dose cytarabine (HiDAC/IDAC) may offer a safer and more durable alternative for younger AML patients achieving remission with Aza-Ven. This study compares post-remission outcomes of timelimited HiDAC/IDAC consolidation versus continuous Aza-Ven in patients <65 years using real-world data.

This was a single-centre medical record review that included patients younger than 65 years with ND-AML. Patients who achieved complete remission (CR) or CR with incomplete haematologic recovery (CRi) after induction with Aza-Ven between June 2022 and June 2025 were included. Patients with secondary AML, mixed-phenotype leukaemia, or those who proceeded directly to allogeneic stem cell transplantation (allo-HSCT) after remission induction were excluded. Among patients in remission, those who did not undergo immediate allo-HSCT or those with favourable risk cytogenetics based on the ELN 2022 classification received one of two post-remission strategies [1] continuation of Aza-Ven until disease progression/intolerance, or [2] three cycles of intermediate/high-dose cytarabine (IDAC/HiDAC; cumulative dose 12–18 g/m²) with or without venetoclax (100 mg for 7 days) followed by 12 months of oral azacitidine maintenance (300 mg once daily for 14 days of each 28-day cycle). The decision between Aza-Ven continuation and cytarabine-based consolidation was individualised, considering patient preferences (continuous therapy versus fixed-duration treatment) and the financial feasibility of prolonged venetoclax use. During the study period, 24 patients received cytarabine-based consolidation. Of these, the first 13 received 3 cycles of HiDAC/IDAC alone, while the subsequent 11 patients also received venetoclax (100 mg daily for 7 days with

concurrent posaconazolealong with each HiDAC/IDAC cycle) following a departmental protocol amendment. Patients with FLT3 mutations were also treated with concomitant midostaurin in both groups (Aza-Ven continuation: 7/29 [24%] vs. cytarabine consolidation: 6/24 [25%]). Patients who later proceeded to allo-HSCT were censored at HSCT. The primary endpoint was RFS, defined as the time from first CR/CRi to relapse; secondary endpoints included minimal residual disease (MRD) negativity rate, treatment-related mortality (TRM), and overall survival (OS). MRD was assessed by multiparameter flow cytometry on bone marrow aspirates at 3-monthly intervals for each patient., MRD value ≥0.1% was taken as positive. Survival outcomes were estimated using the Kaplan–Meier survival analysis, and prognostic factors were analysed using Cox proportional hazards regression, with p < 0.05 considered statistically significant.

Among 82 patients with ND-AML treated with Aza-Ven induction, 58(70%) patients achieved CR/CRi. Five patients who underwent immediate Allo-HSCT were excluded, rest 53 patients were included for analysis. Post-remission therapy was either continuation of Aza-Ven (n = 29) or time-limited HiDAC/IDAC consolidation (n = 24). The median age was 45 years (12–64) in the Aza-Ven group and 37 years (14–61) in the HiDAC/IDAC group (p = 0.938). A higher proportion of ELN 2022 favourable-risk patients was observed in the HiDAC/IDAC arm (58% vs. 28%, p = 0.072), while ELN 2024 and Mayo classifications were comparable (p = 0.489 and 0.569) (Table 1). Mutational and cytogenetic profiles, including NPM1, FLT3, CEBPA, RUNX1, RAS, and MR-associated mutations, were similar between groups (Table 1). Prior to starting consolidation, flow-cytometric MRD negativity was 50% (12/24) in the HiDAC/IDAC group. Following consolidation, MRD negativity in the HiDAC/IDAC arm increased to 75% (18/24), while 8% (2/24) of initially MRD-negative patients converted to MRD-positive. In comparison, the MRD negativity rate in the cohort on continuation of Aza-ven was only 51.7% (15/29)(p = 0.04). (Table 1). Median follow-up of the patients was 310 days (range 62–1100).

No treatment-related mortality occurred in either group. Grade 3 infections were observed in 12.5% of patients receiving HiDAC/ IDAC and in 7% of those on continued Aza-Ven therapy (p = 0.49). Within the cytarabine arm, infection rates were similar between patients who received 7 days of venetoclax and those who did not. Four patients (16.6%) in the HiDAC/IDAC arm and four (14%) in the Aza-Ven arm underwent allo-HSCT. The median time from diagnosis to transplant was 204 days and 115 days, respectively. In the HiDAC/IDAC group, two transplants were for MRD recurrence and two were elective post-consolidation. For RFS and OS analyses, all eight patients who underwent allo-HSCT were censored at HSCT. In the overall cohort of 53 patients who achieved CR/CRi following Aza-Ven induction, the estimated median RFS was 522 days (95%CI: 248–795) (Fig. 1A). Median OS of the overall cohort was 649 days (95%CI: 565–909) (Fig. 1B).

Patients receiving 3 cycles of HiDAC/IDAC consolidation had significantly better RFS than those continuing Aza-Ven (median not reached vs. 309 days, CI 192-425; p = 0.023) (Fig. 2). Cytarabine consolidation reduced relapse risk by 66% (HR 0.34; 95% CI 0.13–0.90 $p = 0 . 0 2 3 )$ . Overall survival was numerically longer with HiDAC/IDAC (median 649 days: CI 544–1004, vs. 470 days; CI 421–710, $p = 0 . 6 1 1 )$ (Fig. 2). The cumulative death rate was comparable (21.7% vs. 21.4% $p = 0 . 9 6 )$ between the group.

Incidence of non-relapse mortality was 1 (4%) in the cytarabine arm (due to myocardial ischaemia after 1year of therapy) and none in the Aza-Ven continuation arm. When stratified by the ELN 2022 risk model, no significant difference in RFS was observed in the favourable-risk subgroup $( n = 2 2 ) ,$ , with median RFS not reached in either arm $( p = 0 . 4 8 )$ or in the intermediate-risk subgroup $( n = 1 4 ) _ { i }$ , where median RFS was 290 days with Aza-Ven continuation and median survival not reached with cytarabine consolidation $( p = 0 . 1 5 )$ . However, when favourable and intermediate risk groups were combined $( n = 3 6 ) ,$ cytarabine consolidation was associated with a statistically significant improvement in RFS compared with Aza-Ven continuation $( p = 0 . 0 5 )$ , whereas in the adverse-risk subgroup $( n = 1 7 )$ , median RFS was 304 versus 522 days with no differential benefit between strategies $( p = 0 . 7 8 )$ (Supplementary Fig. 1). Survival analyses stratified by MRD status within each treatment group showed that, in the Aza-Ven subgroup, patients who achieved MRD negativity had a significantly longer RFS than those who remained MRD positive (median RFS 597 vs. 221 days; $p { = } 0 . 0 3 1 )$ . Likewise, in the cytarabine-based consolidation group, MRD negativity was associated with superior RFS, with the median not reached compared with 312 days (95% CI 182–441) among MRD-positive patients $( p = 0 . 0 1 4 )$ Overall, across both treatment strategies, attainment of MRD negativity consistently conferred improved RFS (Fig. 2C, D). In a multivariable Cox regression model adjusting for age and risk models, cytarabine-based consolidation remained independently associated with improved progression-free survival (HR: 0.21; 95% CI: 0.06–0.75, $p = 0 . 0 1 8 )$ In subgroup-specific multivariable Cox regression models, distinct predictors of RFS emerged for each consolidation strategy. Among patients receiving cytarabine-based consolidation, younger age (HR 0.89; 95% CI 0.81–0.99; $p = 0 . 0 3 )$ , intermediate/favourable ELN 2022 risk (HR 0.01; 95% CI 0.00–0.71; $p = 0 . 0 5 )$ , and achievement of MRD negativity at any time during treatment (HR 0.09; 95% CI $0 . 0 1 { - } 0 . 8 5 ; p = 0 . 0 4 )$ were independently associated with improved RFS. In contrast, in the Aza-Ven continuation group, MRD negativity showed a trend toward improved RFS compared with MRD-positive disease (HR 0.27; 95% CI 0.07–1.08; p = 0.06), whereas age (HR 0.97; 95% CI 0.92–1.01; p = 0.08) and favourable ELN 2022 risk (HR 0.53; 95% CI 0.11–2.68; $p = 0 . 3 0 )$ were not significant predictors.

However, in younger AML patients who achieve complete remission after Aza-Ven-based induction therapy, selecting the optimal post-remission approach often poses a clinical dilemma. Our approach of fixedduration HiDAC/IDAC consolidation provides an effective alternative, offering superior survival and nearly 50% higher MRD negativity rates without added toxicity compared with continued Aza-Ven therapy.

Cytarabine-based consolidation after Aza-Ven has a strong biological rationale. It eradicates residual leukaemic clones persisting after HMA–BCL2 inhibition, potentially delaying relapse and mitigating resistance driven by adaptive MCL-1 or BCL-XL upregulation during prolonged venetoclax exposure [14]. In a recent study, comparing venetoclax–decitabine induction with intensive chemotherapy among patients aged 18-59 years, all patients in CR/CRi received consolidation with either allo-HSCT or HiDAC/IDAC [15]. Notably, 46% of patients in the VEN-DEC arm completed four cycles of cytarabine-based consolidation without major safety concerns. This study supports the feasibility of a timelimited consolidation approach in younger AML patients. However, the trial was not designed to compare outcomes between continued VEN-DEC therapy vs cytarabine-based consolidation. Several studies also report high response rates and longer RFS in younger AML patients when venetoclax is combined with intensive chemotherapy during or immediately after induction. The CAVEAT study combining venetoclax with standard 5 + 2 (cytarabine–idarubicin) in fit older adults showed high tolerability and ORR of 97%, with median OS > 30 months in ND-AML [16]. Use of alternative Aza-Ven and cytarabine-cladribine cycles therapy achieved CR/CRi rates around 88% and one-year OS of \~80% in younger patients [17].

In our time-limited strategy, we employed a sequential approach in the HiDAC/IDAC arm(Aza-ven followed by consolidation), effectively addressing key real-world limitations of continuous Aza-Ven therapy. Prolonged venetoclax exposure produces cumulative toxicity and challenges in clinical practice. Grade 3–4 cytopenias in 60–70% of patients, with prolonged neutropenia (>28 days) in nearly half, frequently necessitating venetoclax duration reductions [1]. In the Mayo Clinic and Italian series, over two-thirds of patients needed dose adjustments, and up to 80% received shortened venetoclax exposure (7–21 days per cycle) to manage cytopenia and infections. Many of these patients required repeated transfusion support beyond six months, reflecting progressive marrow exhaustion [7, 9]. In contrast, in our cohort, cytarabine-based consolidation was superior in terms of RFS, and provides a time-limited treatment and a treatment-free remission that overcomes the major limitations of indefinite Aza-Ven continuation while increasing a deeper level of response (higher MRD negativity) and durability of remission. Achievement of MRD negativity was associated with better RFS in both treatment strategies, supporting the emerging role of MRD in AML. In the subgroup analysis stratified by ELN 2022 classification, although limited by small sample size, the benefit of cytarabine-based consolidation was most pronounced among younger patients and those with favourable or intermediate cytogenetic profiles as per ELN 2022 classification. In the cytarabine consolidation arm, oral azacitidine was delivered as a predefined, fixed-duration (1-year) maintenance rather than as an indefinite strategy as in the QUAZAR AML-001 trial [18]. While this approach aligns with the overall time-limited treatment philosophy, its independent contribution to RFS cannot be fully delineated in the current analysis and should be explored with longer follow-up and in larger prospective studies.

This study has several limitations. First, the absence of an OS benefit despite improved RFS may be explained by competing risks from NRM associated with cytarabine-based consolidation. Additionally, Small sample size, Short follow-up (median <1 year). Potential crossover and effective salvage therapy post-relapse lead to an insignificant difference in OS. There were more patients with an ELN2022 favourable risk cytogenetics in the HiDAC/IDAC group, reflecting the preference of treating physicians for HiDAC/IDAC consolidation among this cytogenetic group. Moreover, as a retrospective, single-centre analysis, potential selection bias cannot be excluded.

In conclusion, this real-world study demonstrates that a timelimited cytarabine-based consolidation followed by 1-year of oral azacytidine maintenance After Aza-Ven induced remission provided a safe and more effective alternative to indefinite Aza-Ven continuation in younger adults with AML. This approach achieves deeper remissions and significantly prolongs RFS, while reducing the cumulative myelosuppression, resistance, and financial burden linked to prolonged venetoclax therapy.
