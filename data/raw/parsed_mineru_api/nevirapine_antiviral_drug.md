## A B S T R A C T

A term male infant developed a blistering muco-cutaneous rash in the second week of life following postnatal HIV prophylaxis with nevirapine (NVP). Clinical findings were consistent with Stevens-Johnson syndrome. Following NVP discontinuation, the infant made a full recovery. This first reported neonatal case of nevirapineinduced Stevens-Johnson syndrome highlights the need for vigilance, early recognition, and prompt drug discontinuation to enable resolution and prevent a severe outcome.

## Introduction

Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) and its severe variant, toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN, defined as involvement of greater than 30 % body surface area), are rare, potentially life-threatening mucocutaneous hypersensitivity reactions characterised by epidermal detachment, erosion of the skin and mucous membranes, and systemic symptoms such as fever and malaise. SJS/TEN are observed in all age groups including neonates, who account for 10–20 % of reported paediatric cases [1]. Mortality rates range from 5 % for SJS and up to 30 % for TEN [2]. Triggers include a wide variety of infectious agents and medications which can lead to difficulty establishing the cause with certainty.

The mechanism of drug related SJS/TEN is thought to involve immune-mediated cytotoxic responses triggered by drug metabolites leading to apoptosis of keratinocytes and eventual epidermal detachment in genetically susceptible individuals [2]. The typical time between exposure and onset of rash is 4–28 days [3].

Nevirapine, a potent non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor, is a well-established cause of hypersensitivity reactions, including SJS/ TEN, with an estimated incidence of 6–10 % in exposed adults [4,5]. Identified risk factors in adults for NVP-induced SJS include pregnancy [6], higher CD4+ count at NVP commencement [7], and presence of specific alleles [7]. Fewer cases of NVP-induced SJS/TEN in children are reported. Two relatively small case series in children have estimated the incidence at 1.4–7.1 % [8,9]. However, despite its widespread use in neonatal HIV prophylaxis there are no documented neonatal cases of NVP-induced SJS/TEN and only two cases in those aged less than one year reported [8]. We present the first reported instance of neonatal NVP-induced SJS to highlight the importance of early recognition as early drug withdrawal promotes a favourable outcome.

## Patient information

A term male infant, born by spontaneous vaginal delivery, with a birthweight of 4.3 kg, presented to a tertiary paediatric centre in Malawi with a four-day history of worsening rash on day of life (DOL) 15. His mother had been diagnosed with HIV in the second trimester of this pregnancy and was commenced on antiretrovirals. The infant was well at birth and commenced on NVP prophylaxis on DOL one (Table 1). This was dispensed in tablet form as the liquid formulation was unavailable. The mother was advised to halve 50 mg tablets, crush and mix with water for administration. The dose was 25 mg once daily (5.8 mg/kg/ day), in line with World Health Organisation and Medecins Sans Frontieres simplified age-based dosing for tablet formulation [10].

## Clinical findings and diagnostic assessment

At presentation the infant was afebrile and alert. Examination revealed presence of a maculopapular rash affecting neck creases, antecubital fossae, axillae, buttocks, hands, and feet with relative truncal sparing. Atopic dermatitis was initially considered the most likely diagnosis, and the infant was admitted to the paediatric nursery for observation and topical treatments. However, by DOL 17 the rash had quickly progressed and dermatology opinion was sought. There was now bullous eruption of the limbs, particularly the hands and feet (Figs. 1, 2), and stripping of the flexural areas and buttocks was noted. Oral mucosa showed ulceration with crusting of the lips, and purulent bilateral conjunctivitis was evident. It was estimated that 10 % of body surface was involved.

The infant remained systemically well, was not jaundiced and had no signs of sepsis. Full blood count was normal. Liver function testing was unavailable. HIV-1 DNA PCR was negative. The differential diagnoses considered included impetigo, staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome and neonatal HSV infection. However, the distribution, mucosal involvement, and temporal association with NVP prophylaxis supported a clinical diagnosis of NVP-induced SJS.

## Follow-up and outcomes

By DOL 23, significant re-epithelialisation was observed with improvement of ocular and oral lesions, with some remaining areas of post inflammatory erythema and hypopigmentation (Fig. 3). The mother received counselling regarding the adverse drug reaction. The child was discharged home with planned outpatient follow up. The weight on discharge was 4.5 kg.

## Discussion

This case documents the first known neonatal occurrence of NVPinduced SJS. NVP is integral to prevention of mother-to-child transmission (PMTCT) programmes across sub-Saharan Africa. Six weeks NVP remains a standard of care for postnatal prophylaxis, given as monotherapy for lower-risk infants or combined with zidovudine for high-risk infants [11].

It is unclear why the reported incidence of NVP-induced SJS/TEN appears lower in infants than in older age groups. Greater immune tolerance in neonates has been proposed as a possible factor contributing to this reduced incidence [3].

Clinical recognition of SJS in neonates is challenging due to overlap with infectious dermatoses. Key distinguishing features include mucosal involvement, development of large flaccid bullae and a positive Nikolsky sign (lateral pressure on the skin results in shedding of the epidermis) [2]. Skin biopsy can support the diagnosis of SJS; however, as in many resource limited settings, this was not available. The diagnosis in this infant was made on clinical grounds. Although there is no universally accepted diagnostic criteria for SJS, this case fulfilled sufficient clinical features to support the diagnosis, including an identified trigger which is known to be associated with SJS/TEN, resolution with removal of the triggering agent, presence of blistering, mucosal involvement, and absence of an alternative explanation for the presentation [12].

The Naranjo scale [13], a causality assessment tool for adverse drug reactions can also be used as an adjunct to assess the likelihood of adverse drug reactions. However, caution is advised in interpretation in paediatrics as it has not been thoroughly validated in this population [14]. Our patient scored 6 on this scale, resulting in a probable adverse drug reaction, however several questions on the scale were unanswered, in part due to the patient’s age.

Once SJS/TEN is suspected, the most important step is prompt withdrawal of the offending drug. Early multidisciplinary management, including ophthalmology and dermatology input, together with meticulous supportive care, is essential. Admission to a critical-care ward or specialised burns unit should be considered where available. Attention must be given to fluid balance, nutritional support, and wound care. Specific therapies such as intravenous immunoglobulins or corticosteroids have been trialled, but evidence for their efficacy remains limited [15]. Systemic antibiotics should be reserved for cases with clinical or laboratory evidence of secondary infection.

This case also highlights the importance of pharmacovigilance within PMTCT programmes. Where liquid NVP formulations are unavailable, manual tablet division may lead to dosing inaccuracies, while simplified age-based dosing may lead to over-or under-dosing. Health systems should prioritise the supply of age-appropriate formulations and ensure caregiver education on correct administration.

## Conclusion

Nevirapine-induced SJS can occur at any age. Awareness among prescribers, timely drug cessation, and supportive management are key to favourable outcomes. This case contributes to the limited literature on neonatal adverse reactions to antiretrovirals and highlights the need for safer drug formulations and strengthened pharmacovigilance systems in

PMTCT care.
