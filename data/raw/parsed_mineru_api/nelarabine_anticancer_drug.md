# Correspondance: Venetoclax combined with FLAG-based chemotherapy induces rapid and deep responses in early T-cell precursor lymphoblastic leukemia/lymphoma. Report of two cases and review of the literature

Julien Dereme1  · Amandine Segot1  · Mitja Nabergoj1  · Natacha Dewarrat1  · Valentin Basset1  · Sabine Blum1  · Gerasimos Tsilimidos1

## Dear editor,

Early T-cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia/ lymphoma (ETP-ALL) is a rare and aggressive subtype of ALL, associated with an unfavorable prognosis in adults [1]. Unfavorable outcomes in ETP-ALL are primarily due to the lower responses after induction chemotherapy [1]. Consolidation with allogeneic hematopoietic cell transplantation (allo-HCT) is recommended, to overcome the chemoresistance, significantly improving overall survival (OS) and aligning the prognosis with that of non-ETP-ALL [2]. Measurable residual disease (MRD) status prior to transplantation and performing allo-HCT in complete remission 1 (CR) or CR2 are critical factors impacting OS and event free survival (EFS) [3]. This underscores the need for alternative, more effective induction and consolidation regimens to enhance CR rates in ETP-ALL. Novel therapies with BCL2 inhibitors, such as venetoclax, have demonstrated promising results in combination with conventional therapy (Table 1). We report two cases successfully treated with an association of fludarabine, cytarabine, G-CSF and idarubine (FLAG-IDA) and the BCL2 inhibitor venetoclax (thereafter, FLAG-IDA-VEN).

## Case 1

A 36-year-old male patient presented with respiratory symptoms due to SARS-CoV-2 infection and blood tests revealed leucocytosis at 73G/L with 95% circulating blast cells, anemia, thrombocytopenia, and massive bone marrow infiltration. Initial assessment showed no extramedullary involvement of the disease, and the cerebrospinal fluid analysis was negative (CNS 1). Immunophenotyping with multiparameter flow cytometry (MFC) demonstrated a population expressing CD1a-/CD2/cCD3 + /CD5dim/ CD7 + /CD8- /CD34 + /CD38 + /CD117 + dim/CD33 + / CD56 + /CD123 + dim/cMPO-/TdT + dim (Fig. 1A), suggesting the diagnosis of ETP-ALL as per WHO 2022 [4]. Cytoplasmic BCL2 was strongly positive. Next-generation sequencing (NGS) found a PHF6 mutation with a variant allele frequency (VAF) of 87% and a U2AF1 mutation with 46%. Induction with FLAG-IDA-VEN regimen led to CR with negative MRD by MFC (10e-4 sensitivity). Both PHF6 and U2AF1 mutations were undetectable by non-error-corrected NGS within the sensitivity level of the assay (1%), and IgH/TCR rearrangement was still detectable at a level of $1 0 ^ { - 6 }$ by quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR). The patient then received one cycle of decitabine and venetoclax, achieving undetectable disease on MRD2 as confirmed by qPCR, NGS, and MFC. Notable adverse events during induction included hypoxemic respiratory failure due to SARS-CoV-2 infection, grade IV febrile neutropenia of unknown origin, disseminated intravascular coagulation, and retinal hemorrhage secondary to grade IV thrombocytopenia. Hematologic recovery occurred by day twenty. As the patient denied allo-HCT, treatment was followed by consolidation, late intensification (with asparaginase containing regimen)

## Case 2

A 30-year-old male patient presented with generalized asthenia, night sweats, and an involuntary weight loss of 15 kg within a week. Blood tests revealed 1.4G/L of leuco - cytes with 12.5% circulating blast cells, and bone marrow evaluation showed 80% medullary blast infiltration. MFC identified two distinct cell populations: the first (pop1) was CD1a-/CD2 + + /cCD3 + /CD5-/CD7 + + /CD8-/CD34 + + /CD117 + + /CD38 + /CD13 + + /MPO-, and the second (pop2) was CD5 ± /CD7 + + /cCD3 + + /CD34 ± (Fig. 1B). cBCL2 was bright. The WHO 2022 classification identifies this leukemia as ETP-ALL: cCD3 + , absent MPO, absent CD1a and CD8, expression of at least one stem cell or myeloid markers (CD34 ± in pop1 but + + in pop2, CD117 + + , CD13 +), and negative CD5 in pop1 and dim in pop2 [ 5]. Cytogenetic analysis revealed a complex karyotype, and NGS identified mutations in DNMT3A (21% VAF), FLT3- TKD (11% and 2.5%), GATA2 (15%), RUNX1 (4.6%), and WT1 (20%). No CNS involvement was found (CNS 1), nor any extramedullary disease. Following initial induc - tion therapy with FLAG-IDA-VEN, the patient achieved CR with MRD negativity by MFC (10e-4 sensitivity), and undetectable levels of DNMT3A and GATA2 by non-errorcorrected NGS. However, low VAFs for FLT3, RUNX1, and WT1 persisted at 0.05%, 0.05%, and 0.19%, respectively. No IgH/TCR sequencing was performed at diagnosis for MRD follow-up. Notable adverse events included pneumo - nia related to a probable fungal infection, likely aspergil - losis, with lesions that were probably already present prior to treatment initiation, as well as a meningitis of unknown origin occurring between the two induction cycles. Hema - tologic recovery was observed by day 29. A second induc - tion was initiated with FLAG-Venetoclax associating the FLT3 inhibitor midostaurin, achieving MRD negativity by both MFC and oncogenomic analysis (MRD2). CNS pro - phylaxis was refused by the patient after only 3 intrathe - cal chemotherapies (after the meningitis, although a causal relationship with intrathecal therapy remains unproven). Treatment was consolidated with allo-HCT. Two years after HCT, the patient remains in CR with negative MRD and 100% donor chimerism.

## Discussion

The most immature forms of T-ALL are subdivided into ETP-ALL, near-ETP-ALL, and pro-T ALL. Sometimes the overlap between those different entities, and even between ETP-ALL and biphenotypic T/M acute leukemias, makes diagnosis challenging [6]. Immunophenotypic-only diagnostic approach can be challenging as ETP cells are highly immature, and recent classification systems show some discrepancies and do not fully align [5]. Our second case illustrates this diagnostic ambiguity and the difficulty in distinguishing ETP-ALL from acute leukemia from ambiguous lineage (ALAL), given immunophenotypic and lineage overlaps (Fig. 1B). Nowadays transcriptome profiling can be helpful and allows differentiation between T-cell, myeloid, and dendritic cell lineages, although genomic alteration can be similar [7].These subtypes exhibit slightly different patterns of antigen expression, but it is unclear whether these differences reflect truly distinct clinical and prognostic subsets. Entities closely related to these immature forms tend to have worse outcomes as compared to other forms of ALL.

With these two cases, we suggest that venetoclax may have contributed to a meaningful therapeutic effect when combined with FLAG-based chemotherapy. Notably, one patient remained in sustained remission without allo-HCT, although additional subsequent treatments were administered and must be considered as confounding factors. It should be noted that neither patient presented with CNS involvement at diagnosis, nor with extramedullary disease. As mentioned above, MRD status prior to transplantation and performing allo-HCT in CR1 or CR2 are critical factors impacting OS and EFS [3]. Early deep responses, with negative MRD after one or two FLAG-IDA-VEN inductions is possibly one of the factors that could explain the persistent CR and allowing for a better prognosis in the described cases. No unexpected safety concerns were observed in these patients beyond the complications typically associated with induction chemotherapy, with the exception of the meningitis reported in patient 2. Hematologic recovery times were not considered unusual. It is also important to note that in the second patient, the persistence of molecular CR cannot be conclusively attributed to venetoclax exposure rather than to the effect of allo-HCT.

The rationale of the use of venetoclax in ETP-ALL is that BCL2 is highly expressed in early T cell progenitor in the double negative stage and seems to be specifically upregulated in ETP-ALL leading to his sensitivity [8]. Our two cases indeed presented a strong cBCL2 expression, justifying the rationale use of venetoclax. Our first patient presents also a PHF6 mutation, which has been associated with increased in vivo sensitivity to the demethylating agent 5-azacitidine alone or combined with the BCL2 inhibitor venetoclax in T-ALL patients [9]. This molecular context constitutes an additional confounding factor supporting treatment response independently of venetoclax. The addition of a tyrosine kinase inhibitor in case two is based partly on data regarding TKIs during induction therapy for FLT3- mutated AML, but also on favorable findings in patients with ETP-ALL, in whom the rate of FLT3 mutations reaches 35% [10], with safety regarding this combination with others regimen.

Ongoing and past studies examined the use of venetoclax in relapsed/refractory (R/R) B or T-ALL. Results indicated that venetoclax-based chemotherapy achieved comparable outcomes non-venetoclax cohorts in T-ALL [11]. However, ETP-ALL are underrepresented in those cohorts because of their rarity It is important to mention that these studies lack of reporting of MRD status following induction therapy.

Reported CR rates for venetoclax combinations in R/R setting range from 60 to 100%, with overall response rate (ORR) from 67.70 to 100% according to the studies (Table 1), considering mixed T-ALL populations for some. In all publications, regimen toxicity profiles are reported as acceptable with relatively easily manageable adverse reactions, demonstrating venetoclax combined regimens as a safe and efficacious option.

As first line treatment, only one prospective phase 2 study is published, including fifteen patients with ETP-ALL, in which venetoclax was used as frontline therapy combined with the HAG regimen, showing a CR of 100% and an OS that had not yet been reached with a follow up of almost eight months [12]. It is worth noting that in this study, the homoharringtonine based regimen used (HAG – homoharringtonine with low dose cytarabine and G-CSF) is not available in many countries. A phase 1b study investigated venetoclax combined with attenuated chemotherapy in both newly diagnosed and relapsed/refractory ALL [13]. Four of the nineteen enrolled patients had ETP-ALL, and three of them achieved a CR. All remaining publication adress the use of venetoclax in relapsed and/or refractory disease. Another prospective study (phase 1/2) evaluated the safety and efficacy in R/R ALL with mini-Hyper-CVD with CR/Cri rate of 57%, but only one patient presented an ETP-ALL phenotype [14]. Kong et al. reported a retrospective study with fifteen patients with venetoclax in combination with different multidrug therapies (hypomethylating therapy, HAAG therapy (homoharringtonine, cytarabine, aclarubicine, G-CSF), CAG regimen (cytarabine, aclarubicine, G-CSF)), with a CR in 60%, and a median OS of 17.7 months [15]. Richard-Carpentier et al. also explored the use of venetoclax combined with chemotherapy in thirteen T-ALL patients [16]: amongst them, there were five ETP-ALL with two of the five (40%) achieving a CR, one with negative MRD, allowing for allo-HCT (after one cycle of nelarabine and asparaginase). In another similar retrospective study, four of five patients obtained CR and of those two of the four with a negative MRD, highlighting the efficacy and depth of response in those highly refractory patients. [17]. In these cases, patients were treated with CV(A)D/MA (cyclophosphamide, vincristine, doxorubicin, dexamethasone, methotrexate, cytarabine) or a nelarabine-based regimen. Moreover, this approach enabled 3/5 patients to proceed to allo-HCT. A phase I study established the efficacy of Venetoclax with Navitoclax in combination with chemotherapy, showing good response rates (8/12 patients with ETP-ALL), highlighting the appeal of BCL-2 inhibition [18]. Concerning the individual case reports, and case series, summarized in Table 1, they report a total of five patients treated with CAG or CVAD (cyclophosphamide, vincristine, doxorubicine, dexamethasone) regimens, all achieving complete remission [19–21]. Other case reports and case series describe the successful use of venetoclax in combination with decitabine, CAG, HAG, nelarabine, homoharringtonine and 5-Azacitidine respectively, each resulting in a CR [22–29].

Targeting CD38 in ETP-ALL is another attractive option as more than 90% of cases express CD38 [30], as was the case in our patients. Venetoclax in combination with CD38 antibody (Daratumumab) and CAGE regimen (cytarabine, aclarubicine, G-CSF, etoposide) were explored in a phase I trial [31]. In this study, seven of the fifteen patients presented an ETP-ALL and showed a better CR rate than other ALL (71.4%). La Starza and colleagues investigated the use of Bortezomib in combination with venetoclax in three patients guided by a drug response profiling (DRP)—based approach [32]. More studies are exploring the strategy of tailoring therapeutic choices according to the mutational landscape, alterations in intracellular signaling pathways, and ex vivo drug sensitivity. In a similar precision-guided paradigm, the ALL-TARGET Observatory, in patients with R/R T-ALL (including 36 ETP-ALL), showed a 3-month cumulative incidence of response of 70% with this approach, including venetoclax based regimens [33]. The GIMEMA ALL2720 trial (NCT04582487) evaluated the feasibility of a chemogenomic-driven approach for the clinical management of early T-cell precursor (ETP)- ALL and T-ALL by integrating DRP and genomic data into therapeutic decisions. Among the 29 patients, 14 had refractory ETP-ALL and 6 had ETP-ALL at diagnosis. The composite complete response rate (CR + CRi) was 60%, with 44% (4/9) being bridged to allo-HSCT, all with an ETP phenotype [34].

In conclusion, venetoclax appears to be highly effective in ETP-ALL when combined with various treatment regimens, as supported by existing case reports, series and phase 2 studies. Its combination with FLAG-Idarubicin is a promising and safe option with acceptable toxicity. However, due to confounding variables, including TKI addition, allo-HCT, the association with intensive chemotherapy backbones such as FLAG-Ida, and prolonged hypomethylating-agent exposure in a PHF6-mutated case, our report cannot demonstrate with certainty that durable MRD negativity and long-term remission result specifically from venetoclax rather than from other therapeutic components. Although ETP-ALL has a higher rate of induction resistance, approximately 70% (68% complete response) of patients still respond to frontline intensive chemotherapy without venetoclax, which highlights that the added benefit of venetoclax must be interpreted cautiously [1]. Moreover, as this work is based on only two case reports, inherent limitations such as selection bias and reporting bias must be acknowledged. Future prospective studies with controlled treatment variables and standardized MRD reporting will be critical to more clearly delineate the true contribution of BCL2 inhibition in ETP-ALL. Prospective studies are needed to further evaluate the integration of venetoclax into standard treatment protocols, with a particular focus on the depth of response by different MRD measures.
