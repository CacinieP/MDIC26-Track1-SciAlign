# Novel Non-Invasive Treatment for Canine Corneal Ulcers: Application of New Extracellular Matrix-Based Sticky Sealants

## ABSTRACT

Objective: To evaluate the clinical efficacy of a newly developed adhesive tissue sealant, gelatinized cornea-derived decellularized extracellular matrix (Cod-ECM) for the treatment of stromal ulcers in dogs.

Animal Studied: This study included 60 eyes of 56 dogs diagnosed with stromal ulcerative keratitis, limited to corneal ulcers involving less than 50% of corneal thickness.

Procedures: Thirty eyes were treated with gelatinized Cod-ECM in combination with standard medical therapy, while the remaining eyes received conventional therapy alone. Assessments included the corneal opacity score (assessed using the semiquantitative preclinical ocular toxicology scoring system), degree of corneal neovascularization, and time taken for complete epithelialization.

Results: Complete re-epithelialization was achieved in both the gelatinized Cod-ECM group and the Control group. The gelatinized Cod-ECM group exhibited a significant improvement in corneal opacity compared with that in the Control group (p < 0.01). Complete epithelialization occurred significantly faster in the GelCODE group (3.9 ± 0.7 days) than in the Control group (5.3 ± 1.0 days) (p < 0.01). The use of gelatinized Cod-ECM was not associated with a significant improvement in corneal vascularization compared to the Control group (p = 0.3).

Conclusions: Gelatinized Cod-ECM is a simple, non-invasive, and cost-effective treatment option for stromal corneal ulcers. Compared to conventional therapy, it provides superior corneal clarity and significantly accelerates epithelial healing. Although it showed no significant effect on corneal neovascularization, its ease of application and therapeutic benefits make it a promising and widely applicable approach for the management of stromal corneal ulcers in veterinary practice.

## 1 | Introduction

Corneal ulceration is one of the most common ocular diseases in dogs. Treatment strategies depend on the depth and severity of the ulcer and may involve either medical management or surgical intervention [1]. In addition to these conventional methods, corneal tissue adhesives have emerged as a non-invasive treatment option for canine corneal ulcers [2–8].

Ocular adhesives are synthetic or naturally derived compounds that aid in wound repair [3]. Natural biopolymers, present in fibrin or collagen-based adhesives, exhibit biocompatibility and complete biodegradability, resulting in minimal stromal inflammation, but they often have low mechanical stability and adhesion [9]. In contrast, synthetic adhesives offer instant adhesion upon contact with a wet surface and are able to offer mechanical support, but have low biocompatibility and insufficient recovery of tissue, which can lead to inflammation [4]. With an aim to restore the function of damaged tissues, new strategies have continuously emerged to consider both adhesion capacity and tissue repair.

Tissue Engineering and Regenerative Medicine (TERM) is an emerging interdisciplinary field focused on developing therapies that enable damaged tissues to regenerate and regain normal function [10]. In veterinary ophthalmology, TERM approaches are attracting increasing interest as potential alternatives or adjuncts to conventional treatments for corneal injuries. One such innovation is a bio-adhesive derived from decellularized corneal tissue: a gelatinized cornea-derived extracellular matrix (Cod-ECM) sealant activated by visible light [11]. This ECM-based sealant exemplifies the TERM strategy by providing a scaffold rich in native corneal matrix components to support cell attachment and tissue remodeling, while its light-triggered crosslinking mechanism securely bonds the material to the stroma in  situ [11]. Early studies have indicated that using Cod-ECM as a corneal patch can promote de novo tissue regeneration with minimal scarring, effectively integrating into the host tissue in a rabbit model [11].

Therefore, the aim of the present study was to evaluate whether the combination of a Cod-ECM tissue sealant with conventional therapy (e.g., topical antibiotics) accelerates corneal healing compared to conventional therapy alone in dogs with stromal corneal ulcers.

## 2 | Materials and Methods

## 2.1 | Animals and Case Selection

Sixty eyes from 56 dogs aged 1–17 years, diagnosed with corneal ulcers at Konkuk University Veterinary Medical Teaching Hospital between January 2021 and February 2025, were retrospectively included. The inclusive criteria were as follows: diagnosis of stromal ulcerative keratitis with corneal ulcer depth less than 50% of normal corneal thickness, confirmed through a comprehensive ophthalmic examination, including fluorescein dye staining and spectral-domain anterior segment optical coherence tomography (AS-OCT, RS-3000, Nidek). This 50% threshold was set to ensure clinical stability and to minimize confounding factors associated with advanced cases that require immediate surgical intervention. To minimize institutional and inter-observer variability, all clinical cases were managed by the same board-certified ophthalmologist according to a standardized medical protocol. Furthermore, the diagnostic and monitoring procedures were performed using the same specialized equipment (e.g., AS-OCT) throughout the study period.

To standardize treatment initiation, only cases that presented within 48 h after the owners first recognized acute clinical signs (e.g., blepharospasm, epiphora, or overt discomfort) were included. At the time of enrollment, although all cases were primarily characterized by stromal loss, they often exhibited concurrent clinical findings such as corneal neovascularization, edema, and fibrosis.

Exclusion criteria included dogs with a history of ocular surgery within the past month or concurrent ocular diseases such as severe keratoconjunctivitis sicca or other tear film abnormalities.

Additionally, dogs with an immunocompromised status due to systemic illness or immunosuppressive therapy, or systemic diseases known to affect corneal healing, such as Cushing's disease or diabetes mellitus requiring ongoing management, were excluded from the study.

Patients were divided into two treatment groups. Thirty eyes from 28 dogs treated with gelatinized Cod-ECM were assigned to the “GelCODE group,” while the remaining 30 eyes from 28 dogs receiving conventional therapy served as the Control group.

Informed consent was obtained from all dog owners prior to initiating treatment. Patient information collected included breed, age, sex, reproductive status, eye involved, slit-lamp biomicroscopic images, AS-OCT images, adjunct medications, duration until treatment completion, and any complications encountered. This study was approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Konkuk University (approval number: KU 24035). The authors have not used AI to generate any part of the manuscript.

## 2.2 | Treatment

All ulcers received topical antibiotics either prophylactically or therapeutically. Infected lesions were initially managed with empirical topical antibiotics, which were subsequently adjusted based on bacterial susceptibility results when indicated. The antibiotics used included chloramphenicol (Chloramphenicol Ophthalmic Solution Unimed, Unimed Pharm Inc., Korea), ofloxacin (Tarivid Eye Drops, Santen Pharmaceutical Co. Ltd., Japan), levofloxacin (Cravit Ophthalmic Solution, Santen Pharmaceutical Co. Ltd., Japan), and tobramycin (Tobra Eye Drops, Dae Woong, Korea). For all cases, topical antibiotics were administered 2 to 4 times daily, depending on the severity of the lesion and the presence of infection. Carprofen (Rimadyl, Zoetis Inc., USA; 2.2 mg/kg, PO, BID) was administered for its antiinflammatory effects. Topical atropine sulfate hydrate (Alcon Atropine Sulfate Eye Drops 1%, Alcon-Couvreur N.V., Belgium) was instilled for cycloplegic purposes.

The gelatinized Cod-ECM sealant used in this study was provided by Biobricks Inc. (Pohang, Republic of Korea) for research purposes. The sealant was synthesized and standardized according to the decellularization and gelatinization protocols established in a previous study [11]. No financial support or research grants were received from the provider for this study.

Gelatinized Cod-ECM therapy was initiated immediately upon diagnosis of the corneal ulcer, following this protocol: Initially, topical anesthesia was achieved using 0.5% proparacaine hydrochloride (Alcaine Eye Drops 0.5%, Alcon Inc., USA). Before applying gelatinized Cod-ECM, eyes were thoroughly cleaned with Hartmann's solution (Daihan Hartmann's Sol., Dai Han Pharm. Co. Ltd.), and prophylactic ophthalmic antibiotic solution (ofloxacin) was administered. The affected corneal region was gently debrided with sterile cotton swabs to remove devitalized and loosely adherent epithelial tissues. The ulcer bed was then meticulously rinsed again with Hartmann's solution and carefully dried using sterile PVA eye spears (Tecfen Medical, USA) (Figure 1A). Gelatinized Cod-ECM was stored as a solid gel under refrigeration and liquefied into gelatin by heating in a water bath at $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } \mathrm { - } 4 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min prior to use. The liquefied gelatinized Cod-ECM was drawn into a 1-mL syringe and then delivered via a 27-gauge irrigation cannula. The patient's head and eyelids were gently restrained for approximately 150 s to ensure immobility during application. Gelatinized Cod-ECM was then carefully applied to the corneal ulceration site (Figure 1B). For photocrosslinking, the gel was exposed to visible blue light for 2–3 min using ophthalmologic diagnostic equipment (BPI-50, RetinoGraphics) (Figure 1C).

To ensure consistent management of the corneal surface, a bandage contact lens (Purevision 2 HD, 0 Diopter, Bausch & Lomb Incorporated) was placed on the cornea in all cases across both GelCODE and control groups immediately after the initial treatment. In the GelCODE group, the lens served the dual purpose of providing general mechanical protection and specifically stabilizing the applied Cod-ECM sealant by shielding it from eyelid movement and friction. The use of bandage contact lenses was standardized for all study participants to eliminate any potential bias in re-epithelialization times arising from the lens itself.

## 2.3 | Clinical Outcome Evaluation

## 2.3.1 | Ophthalmic Examination

Clinical assessments included evaluation of the menace response and dazzle reflex using a direct ophthalmoscope, as well as detailed ophthalmic examinations performed with a slit-lamp biomicroscope (BQ 900, Haag-Streit, Switzerland). All procedures were conducted by a single investigator, and images from both the GelCODE and Control groups were analyzed by the same observer (Appendix A).

## 2.3.2 | Corneal Opacity Score

Corneal opacity score was assessed using the semi-quantitative preclinical ocular toxicology scoring (SPOTS) system [12]. The opacity was graded by evaluating both severity and affected area, and then summing these individual scores. Severity of corneal opacity was scored as follows: 0, normal; 1, minimal loss of corneal transparency; 2, a decrease in the detail of the underlying anterior segment structures; 3, moderate loss of corneal transparency with visible anterior chamber flare and pupillary response; and 4, severe loss of corneal transparency for which anterior segment evaluation was not possible (Figure 2). The affected corneal area was scored as follows: 0, normal; 1, ≤ 25% affected area; $2 , \leq 5 0 \% ; 3 , \leq 7 5 \% ;$ and 4, ≤ 100% of the corneal surface.

## 2.3.3 | Corneal Vascularization Grade

Corneal vascularization severity was graded using modified criteria adapted from previously established scoring systems [13, 14]. To assess the baseline condition and subsequent therapeutic response, evaluations were conducted at two time points: at enrollment (Day 0) and at the completion of treatment. Grading was based on the clinical appearance of the cornea at the time of treatment completion. The cornea was divided into four quadrants, each receiving a score of 1–4 points based on the extent of neovascularization. Superficial vessels were scored as 1 point and deep vessels as 2 points. Vessel location scoring was 1 point for peripheral areas, 2 points for mid-peripheral areas, and 3 points for central areas (Figure 3). Thus, the maximum score per quadrant was 6, and the total possible score for the entire cornea was 24. ## 2.3.4 | Area of Corneal Ulcer

Ulcer areas were quantified using images taken at the onset of ulceration and analyzed using ImageJ software version 2.14.0 (National Institutes of Health, USA) [15]. The method was based on previously described technique [15, 16]. The area of the fluorescein-stained lesion was manually outlined using the polygon selection tool in the ImageJ software. Two slit-lamp magnifications (10× and 16×) were used. Images were saved as uncompressed RGB TIFF files (3008 × 1960 pixels), captured with the IM 910 Imaging Module integrated into the slit-lamp biomicroscope.

## 2.3.5 | Microbiological Evaluation

Bacterial culture and antimicrobial susceptibility testing were performed in 33 of the 60 eyes (55%). The samples were submitted to Green Vet Laboratories (Yongin-Si, Republic of Korea) for pathogen identification and susceptibility testing. For the remaining 27 eyes, while bacterial culture was not pursued due to financial constraints and owner preference, cytological examination via corneal swabs was consistently performed for all cases at the time of enrollment to confirm the presence and morphology of microbial pathogens.

## 2.3.6 | Treatment Endpoint and Follow-Up

The end of treatment was determined when corneal reepithelialization was complete, as confirmed by the restoration of epithelial integrity on AS-OCT images and negative fluorescein staining. Frequent follow-up examinations were conducted until consistent improvement in corneal wound healing was noted, after which evaluations were performed at longer intervals (Figure  1D). Follow-up periods ranged from 32 to 60 days, including an additional monitoring period of approximately 4 weeks after treatment completion to evaluate for ulcer recurrence. All evaluations were consistently performed by the same observer until treatment completion.

## 2.4 | Statistical Analysis

Normality was assessed using the Shapiro–Wilk test, and data were analyzed using an independent t-test if normally distributed or the Mann–Whitney U test if not. The independent t-test was used to compare the age of the dogs and the time required for complete corneal epithelialization between the GelCODE and control groups; data are presented as means ± standard deviations (SD). The Mann–Whitney U test was used to compare the area of corneal ulcers, the degree of corneal opacity improvement, and corneal vascularization grades between groups; data are presented as medians and interquartile ranges (IQR). Fisher's exact test was used to compare the proportions of eyes exhibiting improvement in corneal opacity and to compare the distribution of bacterial species between the groups to assess the influence of microbial factors. Additionally, a two-way ANOVA was employed to evaluate potential interaction effects between the treatment group and the isolated pathogen types, ensuring that the therapeutic efficacy was independent of the microbial environment. Statistical analyses were performed using SPSS software (Version 28, IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA).

## 3 | Results

The clinical characteristics of dogs in each group are presented in Table 1. The mean ages were 11.1 ± 4.7 years in the GelCODE group and 9.32 ± 2.6 years in the control group. No significant difference in age was observed between the groups (t(45.74) = 1.816, $p { = } 0 . 0 7 6 )$ , indicating that age-related bias in clinical outcomes was minimized. Maltese (n = 10) and Shih Tzus (n = 8) were the most common breeds (Table 1).

Bacterial culture and antimicrobial susceptibility testing were performed in 33 of the 60 eyes. Fisher's exact test showed no significant difference in the distribution of bacterial species between the GelCODE and control groups $\left( p = 0 . 6 3 4 \right)$ , ensuring baseline comparability regarding microbial load. Additionally, a two-way ANOVA revealed no significant interaction between the treatment group and the pathogen type (F(3, 52)=1.26, p=0.296). This suggests that the re-epithelialization time was independent of the specific microbial environment, with the sealant's effect remaining consistent across different isolated pathogens.

Both groups either maintained or improved their menace response and dazzle reflexes. The median area of corneal ulcers was 11.3 mm2 (IQR: 4.9–33.8) in the GelCODE group and 18.9 mm2 (IQR: 11.3–24.3) in the control group. No statistically significant difference in ulcer area was detected between the groups (U = 368, Z = −1.212, p = 0.225; Table 2).

Complete re-epithelialization was achieved in both groups; however, the GelCODE group healed significantly faster (3.9 ± 0.7 days) compared to the control group (5.3 ± 1.0 days; t(58) = −2.491, p = 0.016; Figure  4). All eyes in the GelCODE group became fluorescein-negative within 2–14 days, whereas the control group required a longer period, ranging from 2 to 21 days. The total follow-up period ranged from 32 to 60 days.

When comparing the proportion of eyes with improved corneal opacity scores (pre- vs. post-treatment), the GelCODE group (n = 22, 73.3%) demonstrated significantly greater improvement compared to the control group (n = 13, 43.3%, p = 0.035; Figure 4). Additionally, the degree of corneal opacity improvement was significantly greater in the GelCODE group (median: 2.5, IQR: 2–4) than in the control group (median: 1, IQR: 1–2; U = 315, Z = −2.337, p = 0.019; Figures 4 and 5; Table 2).

Regarding corneal vascularization, initial assessments at enrollment (Day 0) confirmed baseline comparability between the two study groups. The median vascularization scores at Day 0 were 1 (IQR: 0–2) for the GelCODE group and 1 (IQR: 0–2) for the Control group, showing no statistically significant difference (U=395, Z=−0.849, p=0.396). This indicates that both groups exhibited similar levels of corneal neovascularization prior to the initiation of treatment. At the conclusion of treatment, although there was a numerical difference in median corneal vascularization grades between the GelCODE group (median: 1, IQR: 0–3) and the control group (median: 0, IQR: 0–2), the difference was statistically nonsignificant (U=392, Z=−0.895, p=0.371; Figure 4).

## 4 | Discussion

The present study demonstrates that topical gelatinized Cod-ECM markedly accelerates corneal epithelial wound closure and improves corneal clarity in canine ulcers. Treated eyes reepithelialized faster and exhibited less stromal haze, indicating more transparent healing compared to standard care. However, Cod-ECM therapy had no significant impact on corneal neovascularization; the extent of new blood vessel ingrowth was similar between treated and control groups. This suggests that the benefits of Cod-ECM are primarily in promoting epithelial and stromal repair rather than altering angiogenic responses in the acute healing phase.

The therapeutic effects of Cod-ECM are likely attributable to its bioactive extracellular matrix components that favor regenerative healing [11]. In particular, the proteoglycan decorin, abundant in corneal stroma, is a known modulator of transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β) activity  [17, 18]. By sequestering TGF-β, decorin can attenuate fibrotic signaling pathways that lead to scar formation [17, 19, 20]. In the context of corneal healing, this modulation helps limit the differentiation of keratocytes into scar-forming myofibroblasts and reduces disorganized collagen deposition. The result is a clearer, more organized stromal repair, aligning with the observed improvement in corneal transparency. Additionally, the Cod-ECM likely serves as a scaffold that supports cellular migration and adhesion [11, 21]. The gelatinized matrix conforms to the ulcer bed and provides a provisional extracellular framework for epithelial cells to migrate across the defect more efficiently, thereby explaining the accelerated epithelialization seen in treated ulcers.

From a clinical standpoint, Cod-ECM offers significant practical advantages. Its application is simple and minimally invasive, involving only the topical placement of the gel onto the cornea with subsequent light-induced crosslinking without the need for sutures or donor grafts. This clinical convenience contrasts with more invasive interventions, such as conjunctival flaps or corneal transplants, potentially reducing the need for general anesthesia and extensive postoperative care. The ability to treat corneal ulcers topically with a biomaterial is particularly valuable in veterinary ophthalmology, where patient compliance and repeated anesthesia pose significant challenges. Gelatinized Cod-ECM therapy can be administered on an outpatient basis and repeated as necessary, making it a practical adjunct to conventional medical management and a promising initial therapeutic option before considering more invasive surgical procedures. By minimizing surgical manipulation of the cornea, this approach may help preserve native corneal architecture and maintain corneal clarity.

Despite these encouraging results, several limitations must be acknowledged. Primarily, the retrospective design of this study and its reliance on owner-reported clinical histories may introduce a degree of subjectivity regarding the onset of disease. While a 48-h inclusion window was strictly applied based on the initial recognition of acute discomfort, the presence of branching neovascularization in some cases at enrollment suggests that subclinical pathological progression may have preceded the owners' observations. This discrepancy reflects a common challenge in veterinary clinical practice, where the biological initiation of a corneal lesion often precedes overt clinical symptoms. Furthermore, the inclusion of ulcers with diverse underlying etiologies—ranging from traumatic injury to chronic degenerative changes—may have influenced individual healing dynamics, necessitating future prospective trials with more etiologically uniform cohorts. Another important consideration is that bacterial culture and antimicrobial susceptibility testing were performed in only 55% (33 of 60) of the eyes, primarily due to owner financial constraints or preferences. Although cytology was consistently performed for all cases to confirm the presence of microbial pathogens, the lack of complete culture data for all eyes may limit a comprehensive characterization of the microbial landscape. Additionally, the sample size was relatively small, potentially limiting the statistical power and generalizability of the findings. A larger cohort would likely provide more robust data on both efficacy and safety. Moreover, administering the matrix as a topical ocular therapy presents inherent challenges, such as dilution by tears and clearance by blinking, that could reduce its pre-corneal residence time. As a result, the matrix's true therapeutic potential might have been underestimated if a substantial portion was lost due to tear turnover. Lastly, the follow-up period was short, and longer observation is needed to ensure that corneal clarity persists and that no late-onset complications, including corneal neovascularization or scarring, develop [22]. Overall, acknowledging these limitations allows for proper interpretation of the results and informs the design of future investigations.

Future research should build on the current findings by conducting comparative studies to determine the relative efficacy of Cod-ECM therapy compared to established tissue sealants such as cyanoacrylate tissue adhesive (CTA). Additionally, investigating the therapeutic potential of Cod-ECM in managing spontaneous chronic corneal epithelial defects (SCCEDs) represents a valuable direction, as these refractory ulcers may particularly benefit from ECM-based regenerative approaches. Results from these studies would further clarify the clinical scope of Cod-ECM, potentially establishing it as a preferred treatment option for challenging corneal ulcers.

In conclusion, gelatinized Cod-ECM represents a promising novel therapeutic option for managing canine corneal ulcers, facilitating rapid epithelialization and improved corneal transparency, likely via ECM-mediated cellular support and modulation of TGF-β- driven fibrotic pathways. Given its minimally invasive nature and straightforward application, Cod-ECM treatment offers significant clinical utility in veterinary ophthalmology. Further research will clarify its therapeutic efficacy and potential role in routine clinical practice. Ultimately, this ECM-based biomaterial approach may enhance clinical outcomes by enabling accelerated, scar-free corneal healing with minimal intervention.
