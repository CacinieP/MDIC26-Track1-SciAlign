# Comparative effects of thiocolchicoside and etodolac on fracture healing in a rat femur model

<!-- image-->

Yunus Emre Bulum1,2\* , Sönmez Sağlam2 , Mücahid Osman Yücel2 , Raşit Emin Dalaslan2 , Mehmet Arıcan2 , Zekeriya Okan Karaduman2 and Ayşe Nur Toksöz Yıldırım3

## Abstract

Background Fracture healing may be influenced by pharmacological agents commonly prescribed for pain control and muscle spasm; while the effects of non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) on bone healing have been widely discussed, data regarding centrally acting muscle relaxants such as thiocolchicoside remain limited. This study aimed to evaluate the effects of thiocolchicoside and etodolac, administered alone and in combination, on fracture healing in a rat femur fracture model.

Methods Seventy-two male Wistar albino rats were randomly assigned to four groups (n = 18): thiocolchicoside, etodolac, thiocolchicoside + etodolac, and control. A standardized mid-diaphyseal femoral osteotomy was performed and stabilized with intramedullary fixation. Treatments were administered orally for 15 days. Animals were sacrificed on postoperative days 15, 30, and 45. Fracture healing was assessed using radiological, histopathological, and biomechanical methods.

Results Radiological healing scores differed significantly among groups at all time points (p < 0.05), with the control group showing consistently higher Lane–Sandhu scores than the etodolac and combined thiocolchicoside + etodolac groups, particularly at postoperative days 15 and 30. Histopathological evaluation revealed significant intergroup differences on postoperative days 15 and 30 (p < 0.001) and day 45 $( p = 0 . 0 0 2 )$ , with the lowest Huo scores observed in the combined treatment group and the highest scores in controls. Biomechanical testing showed significant differences in maximum load to failure among groups at all evaluated time points $( p < 0 . 0 1 )$ , where femurs from the etodolac and combined treatment groups exhibited lower mechanical strength compared with controls. Withingroup analyses demonstrated significant improvements in histopathological and biomechanical parameters over time in all groups (p < 0.001).

Conclusions Thiocolchicoside and etodolac were associated with reduced radiological, histopathological, and biomechanical fracture healing parameters compared with untreated controls, particularly during the early healing phase. The combined thiocolchicoside + etodolac regimen demonstrated the most pronounced delay in healing metrics. Although causality cannot be established, these results indicate that commonly used analgesic and

muscle-relaxant agents may transiently modulate early fracture repair and warrant further experimental and clinical investigation.

Keywords Fracture healing, Thiocolchicoside, Etodolac, Experimental model, Rat

## Introduction

Fracture healing is a dynamic and highly regulated biological process resulting from the complex interaction of mechanical stability, cellular activity, and molecular signaling pathways. This process progresses through overlapping phases, including inflammation, soft callus formation, hard callus development, and bone remodeling. Although the majority of fractures heal uneventfully, complications such as delayed union and nonunion remain clinically significant problems, contributing to increased patient morbidity and substantial healthcare costs [1–3]. Consequently, identifying factors that influence fracture healing continues to be a major focus of research in orthopedic and trauma surgery [4].

Numerous patient-related and injury-related factors have been shown to adversely affect fracture healing, including advanced age, smoking, soft tissue injury, infection, inadequate fracture stabilization, and nutritional deficiencies [5–7]. In addition to these factors, the use of pharmacological agents in the peri- and postoperative period has gained increasing attention due to their potential impact on bone biology. Among these agents, non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are particularly controversial because of their widespread use for pain control and their possible inhibitory effects on bone repair [8, 9].

The effects of NSAIDs on fracture healing are primarily attributed to suppression of the inflammatory phase and inhibition of prostaglandin synthesis through cyclooxygenase (COX) pathways [10]. Experimental animal studies and clinical observational data have suggested that prolonged or high-dose NSAID use may impair callus formation and increase the risk of delayed union or nonunion. However, the literature remains inconsistent regarding the effects of short-term or selective NSAID use [11, 12]. Etodolac, a preferential COX-2 inhibitor frequently prescribed in orthopedic practice, has not been sufficiently evaluated in experimental fracture models, and its influence on bone healing remains incompletely understood [13, 14].

Fractures caused by traumatic injury are commonly accompanied by muscle spasm, soft tissue damage, and significant pain. In this clinical context, treatment strategies often extend beyond NSAIDs to include centrally acting muscle relaxants [15, 16]. Thiocolchicoside is widely used in orthopedic and traumatologic practice for the management of muscle spasm–related pain. While its muscle-relaxant properties are well recognized, thiocolchicoside also exhibits γ-aminobutyric acid - mimetic and glycine receptor–mediated neuromodulatory activity, and has been reported to exert indirect antiinflammatory and antinociceptive effects that may influence neurogenic inflammation, muscle tone, and local mechanical loading at the fracture site [17]. Through these neurogenic and indirect anti-inflammatory mechanisms, thiocolchicoside could theoretically modulate the biological and mechanical microenvironment of fracture healing, although direct effects on osteogenesis have not been systematically investigated.

Recent translational research has expanded the understanding of fracture healing beyond traditional inflammatory and mechanical paradigms, highlighting the role of systemic metabolic responses and regulatory non-coding RNA networks in modulating osteogenic differentiation and repair processes. Clinical and experimental studies have identified stress-induced metabolic disturbances, including hyperglycemic responses following trauma, as potential predictors of delayed fracture healing. In parallel, emerging molecular evidence suggests that long noncoding RNAs and microRNAs may influence osteoblast activity, angiogenesis, and callus maturation through complex gene-regulatory pathways. These findings indicate that fracture repair is highly sensitive to biological modulation and may be influenced by peri-injury pharmacological exposure [18–22].

Furthermore, a broad spectrum of interventional strategies has been investigated to enhance fracture healing, including pharmacological agents evaluated in recent clinical trials, adjunct biophysical approaches such as intermittent pneumatic compression and low-intensity pulsed ultrasound, and experimental biologics tested in standardized animal fracture models. While many of these interventions aim to accelerate bone formation and improve structural integrity, the possibility that commonly co-prescribed analgesic and muscle-relaxant medications may negatively modulate early fracture repair remains clinically relevant and insufficiently explored. Placing the present study within this evolving interventional landscape may therefore contribute to a more balanced understanding of pharmacological influences on fracture healing dynamics [23–26].

Most existing studies have focused on the isolated effects of NSAIDs on bone healing, largely overlooking the role of muscle relaxants in this process [9, 11, 12]. However, in routine clinical practice, NSAIDs and muscle relaxants are frequently prescribed concomitantly during the early post-injury or postoperative period. This combined use is intended to provide both analgesia and reduction of muscle spasm, potentially altering local mechanical loading, inflammation, and functional use of the injured limb. Despite this common combined use, the effects of such pharmacological combinations on fracture healing have not been clearly elucidated. Whether such combinations exert additive, synergistic, or merely cumulative biological effects on bone repair remains unknown. This gap in the literature highlights the need for controlled experimental studies evaluating both single-agent and combined pharmacological interventions in standardized fracture models [2, 3].

The aim of the present study was to evaluate the effects of thiocolchicoside and etodolac, administered individually and in combination, on fracture healing in a rat femur fracture model. Radiological, histopathological, and biomechanical outcomes were assessed to provide a comprehensive evaluation of fracture repair. By examining both biological and functional parameters, this study seeks to explore whether these commonly co-prescribed agents are associated with altered fracture healing dynamics during the early and intermediate phases of bone repair, thereby informing safer pharmacological strategies in orthopedic practice.

## Materials and methods

## Study design and ethical approval

This study was designed as a randomized, controlled experimental animal study investigating the effects of thiocolchicoside and etodolac on fracture healing using a standardized rat femur fracture model. All experimental procedures were conducted in accordance with the ARRIVE 2.0 guidelines for reporting animal research. Ethical approval was obtained from the Düzce University Animal Experiments Local Ethics Committee on 12 June 2024 (decision no: 2024-05-06). All animal handling and experimental procedures complied with national and international regulations for the care and use of laboratory animals.

## Animals and housing conditions

A total of 72 male Wistar albino rats, aged 6–8 weeks and weighing 230 ± 30 g, were included in the study. Male rats were selected to minimize potential hormonal variability in fracture healing. All animals were obtained from the Düzce University Experimental Animal Research and Application Center (DUDAM). Rats were housed in standard cages under controlled environmental conditions, including a temperature of 23–25 °C, relative humidity of 60 ± 5%, and a 12-hour light/dark cycle. Standard laboratory chow and water were provided ad libitum. Animals were allowed a one-week acclimatization period before the initiation of experimental procedures.

## Randomization and group allocation

Following acclimatization, animals were randomly allocated into four equal groups (n = 18 per group) using a computer-generated randomization sequence created with SPSS random number functions. The experimental groups consisted of a thiocolchicoside group, an etodolac group, a combined thiocolchicoside and etodolac group, and a control group. Each group was further subdivided according to the predetermined evaluation time points, with six animals assigned to postoperative days 15, 30, and 45. Group allocation was concealed in sealed opaque envelopes and revealed only at the time of drug administration. The surgeon performing the procedures was not blinded to group allocation due to the nature of the interventions, whereas all radiological, histopathological, and biomechanical outcome assessments were conducted by investigators blinded to treatment groups.

## Anesthesia, analgesia, and perioperative care

General anesthesia was induced by intraperitoneal administration of ketamine hydrochloride (90  mg/kg) combined with xylazine hydrochloride (10 mg/kg). Adequate depth of anesthesia was confirmed by the absence of pedal withdrawal and tail pinch reflexes prior to surgical intervention. Postoperative analgesia was provided using fentanyl citrate (0.02  mg/kg, subcutaneous) once daily for three consecutive days, in accordance with institutional veterinary recommendations. Because opioid analgesics may modulate inflammatory and neurogenic pathways, fentanyl was administered uniformly across all groups to minimize confounding effects on fracture healing. Animals were monitored daily throughout the study period for signs of pain, distress, impaired mobility, infection, or wound complications.

## Surgical procedure and femur fracture model

All surgical procedures were performed under sterile conditions by the same experienced orthopedic surgeon to minimize inter-operator variability. The right femur was used in all animals. Following shaving and antiseptic preparation of the surgical field with povidone–iodine solution, a longitudinal lateral skin incision of approximately 2.5 cm was made along the mid-thigh. The femur was carefully exposed through blunt dissection of the surrounding soft tissues, with particular attention to preserving the periosteum.

A standardized mid-diaphyseal femoral osteotomy was then created using a Gigli wire saw, allowing for a reproducible transverse fracture line (Fig.  1A). After completion of the osteotomy, fracture stabilization was achieved via intramedullary fixation by inserting a 1.2-mm Kirschner wire retrogradely through the intercondylar notch and advancing it proximally across the fracture site (Fig.  1B). Proper alignment and stability of the fracture fragments were visually confirmed before closure.

Following fixation, the surgical site was irrigated with sterile saline and closed in layers. Animals were allowed unrestricted weight-bearing and free cage activity after recovery from anesthesia, without external immobilization. This unrestricted activity was applied equally to all groups; however, altered limb loading related to analgesic or muscle-relaxant effects is acknowledged as a potential confounder.

## Pharmacological interventions

Following surgical recovery, pharmacological treatments were administered via oral gavage for 15 consecutive days. Animals in the thiocolchicoside group received thiocolchicoside at a dose of 0.1 mg/kg/day, while those in the etodolac group received etodolac at a dose of 10  mg/kg/day. The combined treatment group received both agents at the same respective doses. Animals in the control group did not receive any pharmacological intervention. All medications were prepared in distilled water and administered at equal volumes in two divided doses at 12-hour intervals.

The selected doses of thiocolchicoside (0.1 mg/kg/day) and etodolac (10 mg/kg/day) were determined based on previously published rodent pharmacological studies demonstrating effective muscle-relaxant and anti-inflammatory activity without evidence of systemic toxicity [17, 23, 24]. These doses fall within the therapeutic range reported in experimental models and were selected to approximate clinically relevant exposure when adjusted for interspecies differences in body surface area. Furthermore, the chosen 15-day administration period was intended to encompass the early inflammatory and soft callus phases of fracture healing, during which pharmacological modulation of inflammatory and neuromuscular pathways may exert the most pronounced biological effects.

## Euthanasia and sample collection

At postoperative days 15, 30, and 45, six animals from each group were randomly selected and euthanized under deep anesthesia by cervical dislocation. Following euthanasia, the operated femurs were carefully dissected, preserving the periosteum and fracture callus. Specimens were processed for radiological, histopathological, and biomechanical analyses. Each femur was used sequentially for radiological imaging, followed by biomechanical testing, and finally processed for histopathological analysis, ensuring that all three outcome measures were obtained from the same specimen.

## Radiological assessment

Anteroposterior and lateral radiographs of all harvested femurs were obtained at each predefined evaluation time point to assess fracture healing. Radiological evaluation was performed using the Lane and Sandhu scoring system, which assesses callus formation, fracture line visibility, and cortical continuity. All radiographs were independently evaluated by two observers blinded to group allocation, and the mean of the two scores was used for statistical analysis [27].

Interobserver agreement was assessed using intraclass correlation coefficients, demonstrating acceptable reliability between the two evaluators.

Representative radiographic findings for each group and time point are presented in the Results section (Fig. 2).

## Histopathological assessment

After removal of the surrounding soft tissues without disrupting the periosteum, the Kirschner wires were carefully extracted. Specimens were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde at 4 °C for 48 h, followed by decalcification in 7% formic acid. Tissues were subsequently embedded in paraffin, sectioned at a thickness of 7 μm, and stained with hematoxylin and eosin. Additional histochemical evaluation was performed using Masson’s trichrome staining to further assess cartilage and bone tissue organization. Histopathological evaluation of fracture healing was conducted according to the scoring system described by Huo et al. by a pathologist blinded to the experimental groups [28].

Representative histological features of fracture healing at each time point and in each treatment group are described in the Results section and illustrated in Fig. 3.

## Biomechanical testing

Biomechanical testing was conducted using a material testing system (Elista TST 2500, Elista, Türkiye). Femurs were mounted using custom clamps applied to the proximal and distal metaphyseal regions. A longitudinal distraction force was applied at a constant rate of 3 mm/min until mechanical failure occurred. The maximum load to failure, expressed in Newtons (N), was recorded for each specimen.

All biomechanical measurements were performed by an investigator blinded to group allocation.

Although three-point bending and torsional testing may more closely reflect physiological loading, tensile testing was selected to allow standardized and reproducible intergroup comparison of callus strength.

## Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using IBM SPSS Statistics version 26.0 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA). The normality of continuous variables was assessed using the Shapiro–Wilk test. Continuous data were presented as mean ± standard deviation for normally distributed variables and as median (interquartile range) [minimum– maximum] for non-normally distributed variables.

Intergroup comparisons of radiological fracture healing scores and histopathological healing scores at each postoperative time point were conducted using the Kruskal–Wallis test, as these variables did not meet normal distribution assumptions. When a statistically significant difference was identified, post-hoc pairwise comparisons were performed using Dunn’s multiple comparison test.

## Discussion

In the present study, the effects of thiocolchicoside and etodolac, administered individually and in combination, on fracture healing were evaluated using a standardized rat femur fracture model. Radiological, histopathological, and biomechanical assessments revealed statistically significant differences among the treatment groups at various postoperative time points. Collectively, the results indicate a graded pattern of fracture healing, with the control group showing the most advanced repair, followed by thiocolchicoside, etodolac, and the combined thiocolchicoside + etodolac group. These findings suggest that commonly used pharmacological agents for pain and muscle spasm management may modulate fracture healing dynamics, particularly during the early and intermediate phases of repair.

Radiological assessment remains one of the most widely used methods for evaluating fracture healing in both experimental and clinical settings. In this study, radiological healing scores increased over time in all groups, reflecting the expected progression of fracture repair. However, consistent intergroup differences were observed, with the lowest scores in the combined treatment group and the highest in controls, indicating delayed callus formation in pharmacologically treated animals.

Lower radiological scores observed in the etodolac and combined treatment groups during the early postoperative period may be related to the inhibitory effects of NSAIDs on the inflammatory phase of bone healing [29]. Since early inflammation plays a critical role in initiating callus formation, suppression of this phase could potentially delay radiographically detectable healing [30]. In contrast, the thiocolchicoside group exhibited relatively higher radiological scores compared to the etodolac group, suggesting that thiocolchicoside may have a less pronounced effect on early radiographic consolidation. The consistently lower scores observed in the combined treatment group raise the possibility that concurrent administration of these agents may result in additive or cumulative biological effects, although this observation should be interpreted with caution [31].

Histopathological evaluation provides a detailed insight into the biological processes underlying fracture healing and was considered the primary outcome of this study. Differences in histological healing scores among groups were evident at all evaluated time points, with the control group generally demonstrating more advanced stages of tissue maturation.

Reduced histopathological scores in the etodolac group, particularly during the early and mid-healing phases, may reflect the known effects of COX inhibition on prostaglandin synthesis, osteoblast activity, and angiogenesis. These mechanisms have been proposed in previous experimental studies as potential contributors to delayed or impaired fracture repair associated with NSAID use [11, 12]. The thiocolchicoside group exhibited comparatively higher histological scores, which may suggest a more limited interference with the biological cascade of fracture healing. However, in the absence of molecular or biochemical analyses, these interpretations remain hypothesis-generating rather than mechanistically proven [32].

Notably, the combined thiocolchicoside and etodolac group demonstrated the lowest histopathological scores across several time points. This consistent pattern across radiological, histological, and biomechanical domains suggests a cumulative negative effect on fracture healing rather than a beneficial synergistic interaction. While this finding may indicate a more pronounced alteration in the healing process when both agents are used together, the present study was not designed to elucidate pharmacokinetic or molecular interactions between these drugs [33]. Therefore, this observation should be interpreted as exploratory and associative rather than confirmatory.

Biomechanical testing provides functional information regarding the structural integrity of the healing bone. In the current study, maximum load to failure increased progressively over time in all groups, consistent with ongoing fracture consolidation. Nevertheless, the control group consistently exhibited the highest mechanical strength, while the combined treatment group showed the lowest values at all time points, supporting the histopathological findings of delayed maturation.

Lower biomechanical strength values in the etodolac and combined treatment groups paralleled the histopathological findings, suggesting a potential association between delayed biological healing and reduced mechanical competence. The thiocolchicoside group demonstrated intermediate biomechanical properties, which may indicate that reduced muscle spasm and altered functional loading could indirectly influence mechanical recovery. However, because limb loading and muscle activity were not quantified, this remains speculative [31].

Within-group analyses revealed significant improvements in histopathological and biomechanical parameters over time in all groups, supporting the validity and reproducibility of the experimental fracture model. The fact that intergroup differences were most pronounced during the early and intermediate phases supports the concept that pharmacological modulation primarily affects the initial biological cascade of fracture repair [34, 35].

The impact of NSAIDs on fracture healing has been extensively studied, yet remains a subject of debate. While several experimental and clinical studies have reported an increased risk of delayed union or nonunion associated with prolonged NSAID use, others have found minimal or no clinically relevant effects, particularly with short-term administration. The findings of the present study are generally consistent with reports suggesting that NSAID exposure during the early healing phase may influence biological and mechanical outcomes [10, 11].

In contrast, data regarding the effects of thiocolchicoside on fracture healing are scarce. Most available studies focus on its muscle-relaxant and analgesic properties, with limited investigation into its potential effects on bone biology. Our findings suggest that thiocolchicoside alone may exert a milder influence on fracture healing compared with etodolac; however, its biological effects on bone remain largely unexplored and should be interpreted cautiously.

This study has several notable strengths that should be acknowledged. First, a standardized and reproducible rat femur fracture model was employed, allowing for controlled assessment of fracture healing under experimental conditions. The use of a consistent surgical technique performed by a single experienced surgeon minimized inter-operator variability and enhanced procedural uniformity. In addition, animals were randomly allocated to treatment groups, reducing the risk of selection bias and strengthening the internal validity of the study.

Another important strength of this study is the comprehensive evaluation of fracture healing using multiple complementary outcome measures. Radiological, histopathological, and biomechanical assessments were performed at predefined time points, enabling evaluation of fracture repair from both structural and functional perspectives. The inclusion of serial time points (postoperative days 15, 30, and 45) allowed for the assessment of temporal changes during early, intermediate, and later phases of fracture healing. Furthermore, the use of established and validated scoring systems, such as the Lane and Sandhu radiological score and the Huo histopathological scoring system, increased the reliability and comparability of the findings with existing literature.

Despite these strengths, several limitations of the present study should be considered when interpreting the results. The study was conducted exclusively in male rats to reduce hormonal variability; however, this design precludes evaluation of potential sex-related differences in fracture healing. Given the known influence of sex hormones on bone metabolism, the findings may not fully reflect fracture healing dynamics in female subjects.

Importantly, no a priori power analysis was performed to determine the sample size required to detect predefined effect sizes. Although statistically significant intergroup differences were observed, the relatively small number of animals per subgroup (n = 6 at each time point) may limit statistical robustness and increase the risk of both type I and type II errors. Therefore, the findings should be interpreted as exploratory and hypothesisgenerating rather than definitively confirmatory.

In addition, only a single dosage and a fixed duration of thiocolchicoside and etodolac administration were examined. Different dosing regimens, treatment durations, or timing of drug exposure relative to the fracture event may yield different effects on bone healing. The study also focused on morphological and mechanical outcomes, while molecular and biochemical markers of bone formation, inflammation, and remodeling were not assessed. Consequently, the underlying cellular and molecular mechanisms through which these pharmacological agents may influence fracture healing could not be elucidated. Furthermore, radiological assessment relied on conventional two-dimensional X-ray imaging and the semi-quantitative Lane–Sandhu scoring system, which is inherently observer-dependent and less sensitive than computed tomography–based volumetric or densitometric analyses of callus formation. The absence of CT-based imaging may have limited the ability to detect subtle differences in callus architecture and mineralization between groups.

Biomechanical evaluation was performed exclusively using tensile testing. While this method provides standardized and reproducible intergroup comparison of callus strength, it does not fully replicate the complex physiological loading conditions experienced by the femur in vivo, where bending and torsional forces play a substantial role. The absence of complementary biomechanical modalities, such as three-point bending or torsional testing, may therefore limit the comprehensive functional characterization of fracture healing.

Moreover, unrestricted cage activity and possible druginduced alterations in limb loading represent additional potential confounders that could have influenced callus formation and mechanical recovery.

Finally, as with all experimental animal studies, caution is warranted when extrapolating these findings to clinical practice. Differences in bone biology, fracture patterns, comorbid conditions, and pharmacokinetics between animal models and humans may limit direct clinical translation. Therefore, while the present findings provide experimental insight into the potential effects of commonly used analgesic and muscle-relaxant agents on fracture healing, further studies—including mechanistic investigations and well-designed clinical trials—are required to clarify the translational relevance of these observations in human fracture management.

## Conclusions

In conclusion, the findings of this experimental study suggest that thiocolchicoside and etodolac, administered individually or in combination, were associated with altered fracture healing parameters in a rat femur fracture model. The observed effects were most evident during the early phases of repair and varied according to the pharmacological regimen used. While these results do not establish causality, they indicate that commonly prescribed analgesic and muscle-relaxant agents may modulate the biological and mechanical progression of fracture healing, highlighting the need for further experimental and clinical investigations to clarify their relevance in human fracture management.
