# ROS-Mediated Apoptosis-Like Death Induced by Genistein in Candida albicans

Min Seok Kwun1  · Dong Gun Lee1,2

Received: 23 November 2025 / Accepted: 14 April 2026

© The Author(s) 2026

## Abstract

Candida albicans is an opportunistic fungal pathogen responsible for a wide spectrum of infections, and increasing antifungal resistance has intensified the need for new therapeutic strategies that operate through mechanisms distinct from membrane-targeting agents. In this study, we investigated the antifungal mechanism of genistein, a naturally occurring isoflavonoid, with particular focus on oxidative stress-mediated apoptosis-like cell death. Genistein treatment markedly elevated intracellular and mitochondrial reactive oxygen species (ROS) levels, while pretreatment with the ROS scavenger Tiron effectively suppressed ROS accumulation, indicating a ROS-dependent process. Excessive ROS generation disrupted intracellular redox balance by reducing the GSH/GSSG ratio and triggered apoptosis-associated events, including DNA fragmentation, activation of metacaspase-like proteases and phosphatidylserine externalization. These sequential responses demonstrate that oxidative stress acts as an upstream regulator initiating apoptosis-like programmed cell death in C. albicans. Collectively, our findings reveal a mechanistic model in which genistein induces controlled cell death through ROS-driven mitochondrial dysfunction and downstream apoptotic signaling, suggesting that genistein and related redox-modulating compounds may serve as promising candidates for the development of alternative antifungal therapies targeting oxidative vulnerabilities.

## Introduction

Genistein is a soy-derived isoflavonoid broadly distributed in leguminous plants and is recognized for its wide range of biological activities, including antioxidant, anti-inflammatory and anti-proliferative effects [1, 2]. Although its therapeutic potential has been extensively characterized in mammalian systems, recent attention has shifted toward the impact of genistein on fungal pathogens [3, 4]. Among these, Candida albicans remains a clinically significant opportunistic yeast capable of causing infections that range from superficial mucosal diseases to severe systemic candidiasis, particularly in immunocompromised individuals [5]. The rising prevalence of antifungal resistance underscores the urgent need for alternative approaches that act through mechanisms distinct from conventional membrane-targeting fungicides [6]. One promising direction is the induction of apoptosis-like programmed cell death in pathogenic fungi, a process that offers an advantage over necrotic cell death by minimizing inflammatory damage and enabling controlled elimination of fungal cells [7]. In fungi, apoptosis-like phenomena are characterized by hallmark events such as mitochondrial depolarization, chromatin condensation, DNA fragmentation and—critically—accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) [8, 9]. Given that genistein has been reported to disrupt mitochondrial stability and enhance ROS production in various biological systems, it raises the possibility that genistein may exert antifungal activity through oxidative stress–mediated apoptotic pathways.

Although genistein possesses antioxidant properties in certain contexts, it can also function as a pro-oxidant under specific cellular conditions, particularly in environments where redox balance is already compromised. This dual behavior has been observed in mammalian cells and bacteria, where genistein has been shown to elevate ROS levels, promote oxidative DNA damage and trigger apoptotic signaling cascades through mitochondrial disruption. Similar mechanisms have been proposed in microbial studies, yet the precise intracellular responses elicited by genistein in C. albicans remain insufficiently defined [10]. In fungi, ROS act as both signaling molecules and inducers of cellular damage, and excessive ROS can initiate metacaspase activation and mitochondrial permeability transitions that drive apoptosis-like cell death [11]. If genistein perturbs these ROS-dependent regulatory systems in C. albicans, it may offer a mechanistic explanation for its antifungal effects beyond simple growth inhibition. However, direct evidence linking genistein to ROS-driven apoptotic pathways in yeast is still limited. Therefore, the present study aims to elucidate the molecular basis of genistein-induced apoptosis in C. albicans, with particular emphasis on understanding how genistein influences ROS generation, mitochondrial function and downstream cell death signaling. By defining these mechanisms, this research seeks to contribute to the development of alternative antifungal strategies that exploit redox imbalance as a targetable vulnerability in pathogenic fungi.

## Materials & Methods

## Material Preparation

Genistein (Sigma Chemical Co., St. Louis, MO, USA) was dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), which served as a universal solvent, and used to examine its antifungal activity against Candida albicans (ATCC 90028). Candida albicans cells were routinely cultivated in YPD medium at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . Hydrogen peroxide (Sigma) was employed as a positive control. To assess the involvement of reactive oxygen species (ROS), the ROS scavenger Tiron (Sigma) was additionally applied.

## Measurement of Cell Viability

The antifungal activity of hesperetin was evaluated by treating Saccharomyces cerevisiae cells with various concentrations of hesperetin and norfloxacin, followed by incubation at 28 °C for 12 h. The cells were then washed with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), serially diluted, and plated onto YPD agar. Plates were incubated overnight at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ Cell viability was determined at 600 nm absorbance using a microtiter ELISA reader (BioTek Instruments, USA).

## Intracellular ROS Detection

The intracellular generation of ROS was quantified using the fluorescent probes 3′-(p-hydroxyphenyl) fluorescein (HPF) and dihydroethidium (DHE) (Molecular Probes). C. albicans cells $( 1 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 }$ cells/mL) were exposed to genistein (32 µg/mL) or H₂O₂ (2.5 mM). In some samples, cells were pretreated with 0.3 mg/mL Tiron prior to genistein exposure. Following 4 h of incubation, cells were collected via centrifugation (12,000 rpm, 5 min), washed, and resuspended in PBS (pH 7). HPF (5 µM) and DHE $( 1 0 \mu \mathrm { M } )$ were added, and the mixtures were incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min. Fluorescence signals were subsequently quantified using a FACSVerse flow cytometer (BD Biosciences, NJ, USA).

## Analysis of Mitochondrial Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) Accumulation

Mitochondrial ROS levels were determined with the MitoSOX Red indicator (Molecular Probes, Eugene, OR, USA). C. albicans cells were treated with genistein (32 µg/ mL) or H₂O₂ (2.5 mM) for 4 h at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ For Tiron pretreatment groups, 0.3 mg/mL Tiron was applied prior to genistein exposure. Cells were harvested by centrifugation (12,000 rpm, 5 min), resuspended in PBS (pH 7), and incubated with 5 µM MitoSOX Red at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min. After three PBS washes, mitochondrial ROS fluorescence was evaluated using the FACSVerse system.

## Mitochondrial Membrane Depolarization

The depolarization of mitochondrial membranes after treatment with genistein was examined using the JC-1 dye (Molecular Probes, Eugene, OR, USA). Cells were incubated with genistein, $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } ,$ or Tiron pretreatment with H₂O₂ at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4 h. After incubation, the cells were washed with warm PBS, and 0.5 mg/ml JC-1 dye was added. The mean JC-1 monomer fluorescence intensities (green fluorescence, 520 nm) and J aggregate fluorescence intensities (red fluorescence, 596 nm) were measured using a flow cytometer.

## Glutathione Assay

To determine glutathione levels, C. albicans was incubated with genistein (32 µg/mL) or H₂O₂ (2.5 mM) for 4 h at 28 ℃. Where indicated, Tiron (0.3 mg/mL) was added before genistein treatment. Cell pellets were suspended in 5% 5-sulfosalicylic acid (SSA) to precipitate proteins, followed by repeated freeze–thaw cycles for lysis. Lysates were incubated on ice for 5 min and then centrifuged (12,000 rpm, 10 min). Supernatants were collected for total glutathione analysis. Oxidized glutathione (GSSG) was quantified after derivatization with 2-vinylpyridine at room temperature for 1 h. Measurements of reduced (GSH) and oxidized glutathione were performed using the glutathione reductase recycling method described by Bergmeyer.

## Terminal Deoxynucleotidyl Transferase dUTP Nick End Labeling (TUNEL) Assay

Cells treated with genistein (32 µg/mL) or H₂O₂ (2.5 mM) were incubated for 4 h at 28 ℃. For designated groups, 0.3 mg/mL Tiron was added prior to genistein. Cells were fixed in 2% paraformaldehyde for 1 h, then permeabilized with 0.1% sodium citrate and 0.1% Triton X-100 for 2 min on ice. DNA fragmentation was evaluated using a commercial TUNEL reaction kit (Sigma-Aldrich), with incubation at 37 ℃ for 1 h. After washing, fluorescence was recorded using a FACSVerse flow cytometer.

## Measurement of Metacaspase Activation

Metacaspase activity was examined using FITC-VAD-FMK staining, a fluorogenic inhibitor of caspase-like proteins. C. albicans cells were exposed to genistein (32 µg/mL) or H₂O₂ (2.5 mM) for 4 h at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ , with some groups receiving 0.3 mg/mL Tiron pretreatment. After washing, cells were incubated with FITC-VAD-FMK for 20 min. Fluorescence intensities were measured using the FACSVerse flow cytometer.

## Analysis of Phosphatidylserine Exposure

For Annexin V staining, C. albicans cells were washed with 0.1 M potassium phosphate buffer (PPB) and incubated in PPB containing lysing enzyme (20 mg/mL) and 1 M sorbitol under shaking (70 rpm) at 28 ℃ for 4 h to generate protoplasts. Cells were centrifuged (1,500 rpm, 10 min) and subsequently treated with genistein (32 µg/mL) or H₂O₂ (2.5 mM) for 2 h at 28 ℃. When required, Tiron (0.5 mM) was applied before genistein. Cells were washed with 1 M sorbitol prepared in Annexin binding buffer, followed by staining with Annexin V-FITC (5 µg/mL) and propidium iodide (5 µg/mL) for 15 min at 28 ℃. Fluorescence profiles were acquired using a FACSVerse flow cytometer.

## Statistical Analysis

All experiments were performed in triplicate, and results are expressed as mean ± standard deviation. Statistical differences among three or more groups were tested using one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s post hoc test (SPSS version 25, IBM/SPSS). Significance levels were defined as P<0.1, P<0.05, and P<0.01.

## Results

## Detection of Cell Viability

To evaluate the antifungal potency of genistein and to define working concentrations for subsequent mechanistic assays, MIC assays were performed using genistein and H₂O₂. Compared with untreated cells, genistein -treated samples at 8, 16, and 32 µg/mL showed a dose-dependent reduction in absorbance, with a clear decrease observed starting at 32 µg/mL. These results indicate that the antifungal effect of genistein becomes apparent at concentrations of 32 µg/ mL (Fig. S1).

## Intracellular ROS Accumulation

Intracellular ROS levels were quantified using HPF fluorescence (Fig. 1a). Untreated cells exhibited a baseline value of 9.86, whereas treatment resulted in a significant increase to 66.00, indicating a rapid elevation of oxidative pressure within the cells. Co-treatment with the ROS scavenger Tiron reduced fluorescence to 10.00, comparable to the untreated control, confirming that the response is specifically dependent on ROS rather than nonspecific stress. Hydrogen peroxide treatment produced the highest elevation (97.78), validating the experimental system’s sensitivity.

Superoxide production measured using DHE (Fig. 1b) showed a similar pattern, increasing from 10.24 in untreated cells to 61.06, while Tiron reduced the value to 10.94, demonstrating substantial inhibition of ROS accumulation. Hydrogen peroxide treatment further increased fluorescence to 83.51, suggesting that intracellular ROS elevation is a major early event in the cell death response.

## Mitochondrial Oxidative Stress

Mitochondrial ROS generation was assessed using Mito-SOX Red (Fig. 2a). Fluorescence intensity increased markedly from 10.66 in untreated cells to 56.72, indicating that mitochondrial ROS production contributes significantly to the observed oxidative shift. Tiron pretreatment reduced the value to 17.71, implying that mitochondria are a primary target of ROS modulation. Hydrogen peroxide exposure resulted in the most pronounced increase (123.76), reinforcing the role of mitochondrial disruption in oxidative damage progression.

Collectively, these findings suggest that mitochondrial dysfunction accompanies intracellular ROS accumulation and may represent a key upstream event in the induction of cell death. JC-1 staining was used to assess mitochondrial membrane potential (ΔΨm). In healthy cells with high ΔΨm, JC-1 accumulates in the mitochondrial inner membrane and forms J-aggregates, emitting red fluorescence. In contrast, when the mitochondrial membrane is damaged, the decreased ΔΨm prevents JC-1 accumulation and the dye remains in its monomer form emitting green fluorescence. Therefore, the red/green fluorescence ratio serves as an indicator of mitochondria dysfunction. According to the FL-1/ FL-2 ratio analysis, the untreated cells showed a ratio of 1.47 whereas cells treated with genistein and Hydrogen peroxide exhibited significantly reduced ratios of 0.87 and 0.88 respectively indicating mitochondrial membrane depolarization. In contrast, cells treated with Tiron showed an increased ratio of 1.46 suggesting a partial restoration of membrane potential (Fig. 2b).

## Disruption of Intracellular Redox Balance

The GSH/GSSG ratio was measured to evaluate antioxidant disruption (Fig. 3). Compared with the control value of 2.13, treated cells showed a substantial reduction to 1.24, indicating a shift toward an oxidized intracellular environment associated with severe oxidative stress. Tiron pretreatment increased the ratio to 1.97, recovering much of the redox imbalance, whereas hydrogen peroxide lowered it further to 1.03, demonstrating the strongest oxidative pressure. These results confirm that antioxidant defenses are compromised under elevated ROS conditions and that glutathione depletion is tightly linked to oxidative stress–induced cellular damage.

## Detection of DNA Fragmentation and Metacaspase Activation

TUNEL staining revealed marked DNA fragmentation following treatment (Fig. 4a). The percentage of TUNELpositive cells increased from 7.31% in untreated controls to 41.39%, indicating extensive DNA breakage induced by oxidative stress. Tiron reduced this value to 11.73%, demonstrating efficient suppression of ROS-dependent DNA damage. Hydrogen peroxide treatment resulted in 47.32% TUNEL-positive cells, consistent with defined cell death signaling. These observations suggest that oxidative damage progresses to irreversible genetic injury and represents a major turning point in the commitment to programmed cell death.

FITC-VAD-FMK staining demonstrated strong activation of caspase-like proteases (Fig. 4b). Positive staining increased substantially from 5.43% in untreated cells to 95.42%, suggesting that protease activation is a central step in execution of apoptosis-like pathways. Tiron decreased the response to 40.39%, further supporting that caspase activation is directly dependent on ROS accumulation. Hydrogen peroxide treatment resulted in 98.64% positive cells, consistent with severe apoptotic progression. Thus, the activation of proteolytic enzymes appears to coordinate downstream apoptotic events in response to oxidative injury.

## Exposure of Phosphatidylserine

Annexin V/PI staining was used to analyze phosphatidylserine externalization (Fig. 5). Early apoptotic cell populations increased from 4.36% in untreated samples to 20.55%, indicating membrane flipping and early apoptosis activation. Tiron pretreatment reduced this proportion to 9.55%, confirming a ROS-dependent mechanism. Hydrogen peroxide treatment demonstrated 43.56% Annexin V-positive cells, the strongest apoptotic response among the tested groups. Together, these results indicate that oxidative stress initiates sequential events that culminate in apoptosis-like programmed cell death characterized by DNA damage, caspase activation, and membrane remodeling.

## Discussion

This study demonstrates that genistein induces apoptosislike programmed cell death in Candida albicans through excessive accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and subsequent oxidative damage [12]. Flow cytometric analyses using HPF and DHE clearly showed a substantial increase in intracellular ROS and superoxide levels following treatment, while pretreatment with the ROS scavenger Tiron effectively suppressed ROS elevation [13]. These findings suggest that oxidative stress plays a primary and necessary role in genistein-induced cytotoxicity, rather than occurring as a secondary consequence of cell injury. The ROS-dependent nature of the response establishes oxidative imbalance as an early and essential event in the mechanism of action [14]. Similar observations have been reported in mammalian cells, where genistein acts as a pro-oxidant under stress-prone cellular environments, shifting redox homeostasis [15].

Mitochondrial ROS detection via MitoSOX Red further revealed that mitochondria serve as a central origin and amplification site of ROS production [16]. The pronounced increase in mitochondrial superoxide suggests that mitochondrial electron transport chain disruption may contribute to the oxidative imbalance observed following treatment. In fungi, mitochondria are recognized as key determinants of apoptosis-like cell death, regulating events such as mitochondrial membrane potential collapse, cytochrome c release and metacaspase activation [17, 18]. The substantial restoration of mitochondrial ROS levels by Tiron suggests that downstream apoptotic events are initiated by ROS-mediated mitochondrial dysfunction rather than independent pathways. This aligns with previous findings in Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Cryptococcus neoformans, where mitochondrial impairment is tightly linked to ROSdriven programmed cell death.

The disruption of intracellular redox status observed in this study supports the notion that oxidative stress overwhelms antioxidant buffering capacity. A significant reduction in the GSH/GSSG ratio indicates depletion of reduced glutathione, a critical component of the cellular antioxidant system [19]. Glutathione imbalance is widely considered a key molecular switch for apoptosis initiation, as excessive ROS may inactivate glutathione-dependent enzymes, inhibit thiol-based redox regulation and increase vulnerability to oxidative DNA damage [20, 21]. The partial recovery of the GSH/GSSG ratio following Tiron treatment reinforces the mechanistic association between ROS accumulation and antioxidant collapse. This suggests that targeting glutathione metabolism may enhance susceptibility of C. albicans to genistein-induced cell death.

Markers of apoptosis-like death, including DNA fragmentation, caspase-like activation and phosphatidylserine (PS) externalization, further demonstrate that genistein triggers a controlled form of cell elimination rather than necrotic destruction [22]. TUNEL analysis revealed pronounced DNA fragmentation, indicating irreversible genomic damage as a consequence of oxidative stress [23]. The significant reduction in TUNEL-positive cells following ROS scavenging emphasizes that DNA fragmentation is driven directly by oxidative injury. Similarly, the strong activation of metacaspase-like proteases suggests that proteolytic signaling cascades serve as essential mediators of execution-stage apoptosis [7, 24]. These results align with fungal apoptosis studies where metacaspase activation follows mitochondrial dysfunction and precedes membrane remodeling.

The increase in PS externalization detected through Annexin V staining indicates translocation of membrane phospholipids, a hallmark of early apoptosis commonly observed in both yeast and mammalian systems [25]. This event is generally associated with cytoskeletal rearrangement and membrane asymmetry loss, facilitated by calcium-dependent scramblase activity under ROS-enriched conditions [8]. Given that pretreatment with Tiron significantly reduced PS exposure, the current findings reinforce the concept of ROS acting as a master regulator coordinating successive death phases.

Collectively, these results support a mechanistic model in which genistein induces excessive ROS accumulation, disrupts antioxidant balance, elevates mitochondrial oxidative stress and activates downstream apoptosis-like signaling pathways that culminate in controlled cell death. This redox-driven mechanism provides a novel explanation for the antifungal potential of genistein and may help expand therapeutic options for fungal infections. Importantly, apoptosis-like death offers advantages over necrotic cell death by limiting inflammatory responses and reducing release of toxic cellular contents. Because C. albicans increasingly demonstrates resistance to existing antifungal drugs targeting cell membranes or ergosterol biosynthesis, agents capable of exploiting intrinsic oxidative vulnerabilities may provide an attractive alternative.

Future investigations should aim to determine the specific upstream source of ROS generation in response to genistein. Potential candidates include mitochondrial respiratory complexes, NADPH oxidase systems, metal ion–dependent Fenton reactions and endoplasmic reticulum–associated oxidative folding pathways [26]. In addition, examining whether genistein synergizes with standard antifungal drugs such as amphotericin B or azoles could provide insight into combination therapy strategies that minimize drug resistance. Identification of genistein-responsive transcriptional changes and survival signaling pathways may also reveal molecular biomarkers of oxidative susceptibility and offer opportunities for clinical translation [27].

In conclusion, this study highlights the potential of targeting ROS-mediated apoptosis-like death as a strategic approach to controlling C. albicans. Elucidation of genistein’s pro-oxidative and mitochondrial-disruptive actions deepens understanding of redox-dependent antifungal mechanisms and establishes a foundation for developing new therapies that exploit oxidative stress as a therapeutic vulnerability.
