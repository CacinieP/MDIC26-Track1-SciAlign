## ABSTRACT

Sodium glucose cotransporter-2 (SGLT2) inhibitors are a class of antidiabetics with benefits including HbA1c-lowering, weight loss, cardiovascular and renal protection in addition to adverse effects including genitourinary tract infections, ketoacidosis, and bone loss. Here, we describe the rationale and design of the Genetics of Response to Canagliflozin (GRC) study and report on the pharmacodynamic findings. This study, for which enrollment has been completed, was conducted in healthy Amish individuals with a goal of identifying genetic predictors of variability in safety and efficacy of the SGLT2 inhibitor canagliflozin. Participants took canagliflozin 300 mg/day for 5 days, completed a 24-h urine collection, and had blood drawn at baseline, Day 3, and Day 6. The primary endpoint is glucosuria and the secondary endpoints assess additional biomarkers for safety and efficacy related to bone, cardiovascular disease, and ketosis. 402 participants completed the study. We identified significant variability in the degree of canagliflozin-induced glucosuria with a heritability of 34% (p = 8.69e−03). Canagliflozin induced significant increases in serum phosphorous (Day 3: +5.0%, p = 5.83e−21) and fibroblast growth factor-23 (Day 3: +20.2%, p = 1.58e−18), decrease in 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (Day 3: −25.0%, p = 5.71e−54), and increase in parathyroid hormone: (Day 3: +20.8%, p = 1.98e−33). Canagliflozin also significantly decreased serum uric acid (Day 6: −33.2%, p = 1.01e−65) and increased beta-hydroxybutyrate (Day 3: +71.9%, p = 8.11e−28). The most significant baseline characteristic associated with glucosuria was eGFR (p = 8.10e−10). This study will provide the opportunity to identify genetic and other 'omic predictors of safety and efficacy biomarkers for SGLT2 inhibitor response that could help guide diabetes treatment in the future.

The Genetics of Response to Canagliflozin (GRC) study seeks to identify genetic variants associated with SGLT2 inhibitorinduced glucosuria and safety-related pharmacodynamic responses to SGLT2 inhibitors, which include changes in the ketone body β-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) for ketoacidosis. For bone health, these include changes in serum phosphorous (P), fibroblast growth factor 23 (FGF23), 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (1,25(OH) D), intact parathyroid hormone (PTH), β-Cterminal telopeptide (β-CTX), procollagen type 1N-terminal intact propeptide (P1NP), and calcium (Ca). β-CTX is a marker of bone resorption, and P1NP is a marker of bone formation. ## 2 | Methods

## 2.1 | Rationale for the GRC Study Design

The GRC study was funded by the National Institute of Diabetes, Digestive, and Kidney Diseases (R01DK118942) to investigate potential genetic contributions to safety and efficacy responses to canagliflozin. The study will use genome-wide approaches coupled with assessment of relevant candidate genes. The widespread use of SGLT2 inhibitors provides a strong rationale to invest in building a scientific foundation to help guide physicians to prescribe SGLT2 inhibitors to patients most likely to derive clinical benefit while avoiding patients most likely to be harmed.

## 2.1.1 | Clinical Endpoints

The pharmacology of SGLT2 inhibitors has been extensively investigated. It is well established that SGLT2 inhibitors promote glucosuria, which mediates weight loss and HbA1c-lowering [12–14]. Given that glucosuria represents a direct measure of the physiological mechanism mediating the metabolic actions of SGLT2 inhibitors, it was chosen as the primary endpoint of the study (Table 1). Randomized, controlled studies provide strong evidence for additional effects of SGLT2 inhibitors: for example, decreasing serum uric acid and increasing serum phosphorous levels [12, 15]. Recent publications provide convincing evidence for SGLT2 inhibitor-induced increases in glucagon and ketone body levels [16–18]. There is also evidence for adverse effects of canagliflozin—including bone loss determined by dual energy X-ray absorptiometry (DXA) [19], increased risk of fractures [20], albeit conflicting [21], and increased risk of ketoacidosis in patients with type 1 diabetes (T1D) [22–24]. The FDA has also issued warnings that SGLT2 inhibitors increase the risk of ketoacidosis in T2D patients [25]. While there are also reports of adverse effects on bone health for dapagliflozin [26] and empagliflozin [27], this is less well established than for canagliflozin [19, 20]. Based on these additional pharmacologic effects of SGLT2 inhibitors related to bone health, cardiovascular disease, and ketoacidosis, our secondary endpoints were chosen to assess short-term changes related to these effects (Table 1). The choice for the Day 3 and Day 6 timing of the secondary endpoints was based on a preliminary study that included time series measurements over 120 h of P, FGF23, PTH, and 1,25(OH) D, demonstrating the early increase in serum P and subsequent downstream changes in FGF23, PTH, and 1,25(OH) D [28].

## 2.1.2 | Choice of SGLT2 Inhibitor

We chose to study canagliflozin not only because it was the most widely used SGLT2 inhibitor at the time we proposed our study, but also because canagliflozin has the strongest evidence base with respect to clinical adverse effects and mechanistically relevant biomarkers. Although the totality of evidence suggests that the various side effects are class effects, there may be compoundspecific differences with respect to the magnitude of the risks [e.g., related to differences in approved doses in relation to the dose– response curve [29]]. Furthermore, it is highly likely that data obtained with canagliflozin will be generalizable to other SGLT2 inhibitors. Pharmacogenetically-relevant genetic variants can be divided into two classes: (a) variants related to pharmacodynamics—the molecular mechanism of action; and (b) variants related to pharmacokinetics—drug absorption, distribution, metabolism or excretion. Inasmuch as all members of the SGLT2 inhibitor class share the same mechanism of action, we expect they would all be affected similarly by pharmacodynamically-relevant genetic variants. Furthermore, all approved SGLT2 inhibitors have very similar structures based on the C-glucoside structure of dapagliflozin introduced by Bristol-Myers Squibb medicinal chemists [30]. Glucuronidation is the principal route of metabolism for canagliflozin, dapagliflozin, and empagliflozin. While all three drugs are reported to be glucuronidated by UGT1A9, additional glucuronosyltransferases also contribute to metabolism of canagliflozin (UGT2B4) and empagliflozin (UGT2B7, UGT1A3, and UGT1A8) [31]. Follow up studies would be required to determine whether individual SGLT2 inhibitors are differentially affected by genetic variants in glucuronosyltransferases.

## 2.1.3 | Study Population

Our study was conducted in healthy volunteers for three primary reasons. The first reason for studying healthy volunteers relates to the challenges of interpreting glucosuria in patients with diabetes. While plasma glucose levels are relatively constant day-to-day in healthy individuals, they can vary dramatically hour-to-hour and day-to-day in patients with diabetes. Plasma glucose levels are critical determinants of the magnitude of filtered glucose and quantity of glucose excreted in the urine. Thus, this high degree of variability and subsequent statistical noise would make it very challenging to detect genetic variants associated with canagliflozin-induced glucosuria in patients with diabetes. The second rationale for studying healthy volunteers relates to the number of confounding factors present in patients with diabetes including multiple comorbid conditions (e.g., renal impairment which decreases the magnitude of glucosuric response to SGLT2 inhibitors), concomitant medications (and changes in medications), changes in body weight, acute disease, and progression of disease status. Finally, studying healthy volunteers allows us to conduct a short-term intervention in which we have greater control over factors influencing response to canagliflozin, thus higher signal-to-noise ratio to detect genetic associations in this first genome-wide association study (GWAS) of response to canagliflozin that we are aware of.

The rationale for conducting our study in the Old Order Amish is several-fold. Founder populations such as the Old Order Amish provide excellent opportunities to learn from experiments of nature. Because of genetic drift, some genetic variants become enriched while others become less prevalent. Our group has a long-standing history of community-engaged research in the Amish. Amish community members serve on our clinic board and work as Amish liaisons. The Amish have a relatively homogenous lifestyle and take few prescription medications leading to an increased signal-to-noise ratio as described above. Another advantage of conducting our study in the Old Order Amish is we can leverage the detailed knowledge of the genetic architecture of complex traits and disease in this population gained from over 30 years of NIH-funded research. Moreover, the Anabaptist Genealogy Database, which contains the genealogical records of the Old Order Amish community dating back to the 18th century, enables us to connect participants into a 14-generation pedigree for analysis [32, 33]. In addition, we have access to extensive whole-genome DNA sequencing data (in 1123 Amish), which facilitates our ability to perform imputation on our genotyping arrays' data. Finally, major discoveries we have made in the Amish have been replicated in non-Amish populations and even led to new drugs being developed, lending support for the generalizability of our findings [34, 35].

## 2.2 | GRC Study Design

## 2.2.1 | Eligibility Criteria

Males or females of Amish descent 18 years of age or older were recruited to participate. Our originally proposed sample size of N = 500 was decreased to N = 402 due to delays secondary to the COVID-19 pandemic and budget cuts from NIDDK. The R package pwr was used to conduct a statistical power analysis to determine the SNP effect size detectable in a regression model given our proposed and current sample sizes, 500 and 402, respectively. At an alpha of 5e−08 and power 80%, we can detect a beta effect size of 0.27 SDs per allele in 500 subjects and beta of 0.30 SDs in 402 subjects. The eligibility criteria for the study are shown in Table  S1. Individuals with 25-hydroxyvitamin D (25(OH)D) < 20 ng/mL or out of range thyroid stimulating hormone (TSH) could have their levels corrected and then be re-screened. Participants were recruited through our Amish Research Clinic in Lancaster, PA.

## 2.2.2 | Study Protocol

The study protocol was initiated in volunteers who met all the eligibility criteria for this 6-day outpatient study. Research team members from the Amish Research Clinic (a research nurse and an Amish liaison) made 4 home visits (Figure 1). Pre-dose blood samples were obtained on Day 1 and post-dose blood samples were obtained on Days 3 and 6. Participants took canagliflozin 300 mg/day on Days 1–5. Research participants collected a 24-h urine sample on Day 2 through 3 (24–48 h after initiating canagliflozin). We conducted a pilot study confirming that at baseline before canagliflozin treatment, glucosuria is essentially zero in healthy volunteers [36], thus we only measured 24-h glucosuria on canagliflozin. A prior study also demonstrated that the magnitude of glucosuria was stable from 24 through 120 h post-dose [28]. Participants were instructed to take canagliflozin at the same time each day and log the time in an instruction sheet provided to them. Blood pressure was measured in duplicate at each home visit.

## 2.2.3 | Phenotyping and Statistical Analyses

Safety-related biomarkers for bone health, ketoacidosis and CV risk factors were quantified using standard lab procedures. Labs were measured by Quest Diagnostics, with the exception of FGF23 and urinary glucose. FGF23 was measured in plasma using ELISA (QuidelOrtho, catalog# 60–6600, San Diego, CA). Urinary glucose was measured by LabCorp until they discontinued the test in April 2022. After that time, urinary glucose was measured in an internal clinical laboratory using an adaptation of the hexokinase-glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase method (Dimension Vista, Newark, DE). Due to this need to switch laboratories mid-study, a dichotomized variable named “Lab” was included in the analyses such that LabCorp was assigned 0 and the internal lab was assigned 1. Regardless of when participants completed the study, estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) was calculated using the 2021 CKD-EPI equation [37]. Many of the secondary endpoints of interest are subject to diurnal variation (e.g., P, FGF23, 1,25(OH) D, and PTH) [28], thus all labs were drawn at approximately the same time of day (approximately 0700). The primary endpoint, glucosuria, was calculated by dividing 24-h urinary glucose by 24-h urinary creatinine $\mathrm { ( g _ { g l u c } / g _ { c r } ) }$ to adjust for body size. We evaluated other ways of normalizing drug-induced glucosuria, for example, with body surface area or body weight, but these methods yielded results that were dependent on sex [36]. Thus, our future GWAS analyses will utilize glucosuria adjusted for 24-h creatinine which was independent of sex.

Two-sided one sample Wilcoxon signed rank test (as the distributions for the percent changes of secondary endpoints (SEPs) were not normally distributed) was used to identify SEP percent changes that had a median significantly different from zero. Linear mixed model as implemented in Mixed Model Analysis for Pedigrees and Populations (MMAP) [38] was employed to identify the baseline correlates of glucosuria, assess its heritability, and test its association with significantly changed SEP percent changes as identified by the Wilcoxon signed rank test. The heritability of glucosuria was calculated from the base model as the proportion of the additive polygenic genetic variance of the total phenotypic variance. The association between glucosuria and each SEP percent change was tested adjusting for age, age2, sex, body mass index (BMI), fasting plasma glucose (FPG), eGFR [37], lab for glucosuria, and familial correlation. The association analysis was run once with raw glucosuria to obtain the effect sizes and then with log transformed glucosuria (as glucosuria distribution was skewed) to obtain the p-values. The percent change of each SEP was calculated as the difference between Day 3/6 and Day 1 divided by Day 1 value and multiplied by 100 (([day 3−day 1]/day 1) × 100, ([day 6−day 1]/day 1) × 100). False discovery rate (FDR) [39] was used to adjust for multiple testing in Wilcoxon signed rank test and the association analysis results. P-Values reported in the results section for Wilcoxon signed rank test and glucosuria association with SEP percent changes are FDR adjusted p-values. Adjusted p-value < 0.05 was considered significant. Both marginal and adjusted p-values are included in Tables S2 and S3.

## 2.3 | Genotyping and Imputation

Isolated DNA was genotyped using the Global Diversity Array and called using GenomeStudio version 2011.1, Genotyping Module version 1.9.4, and GenTrain Version 1.0. Standard quality control was performed using the R packages GWASTools [40], GENESIS [41] and SNPRelate [42] and then data was uploaded to the Trans-Omics for Precision Medicine (TOPMed) Imputation Server [43] where the pre-phasing was performed using Eagle v2.4 [44], and then imputation to the TOPMed global reference panel [45], which includes whole genome sequence data for 1025 Amish participants, was performed using Minimac4 [46]. Imputed data was filtered to remove monomorphic variants, variants with imputation quality score < 0.1, and duplicates.

## 2.4 | Planned Analyses

## 2.4.1 | Single Variant Association Analysis

Genetic association will be analyzed with variance components analysis as implemented in MMAP  [38]. The dose of each variant will be tested for association with glucosuria and significantly changed SEPs adjusting for the same covariates along with baseline value of each SEP and family structure. Pre-specified candidate genes will be assessed (e.g., SLC2A2, SLC5A2, UGT1A9, UGT2B4, CASR, FGF23, SLC34A1, SLC2A9, ABCG2, GCGR, and GCKR) (Table S4) with substantially less stringent p-values based on the number of independent variants in each gene. Formal conditional analysis will be performed for significant variants within 1 Mb from each other to identify independent signals by adding the most significant variant at each signal as a covariate to the basic model. Sequential adjustment will be performed until the original signal is no longer significant $\left( p > 0 . 0 1 \right)$ . When several variants show similar statistical evidence of association, priority will be assigned to lower frequency variants with better functional prediction. Priority will also be assigned to: exonic variants (vs. intronic or intergenic variants), loss of function variants (stop, frame shift, splice site) vs. non-synonymous variants; and variants that can affect 3′UTR regulatory regions or disturb a binding site for a transcription factor or microRNA.

## 2.4.2 | Metabolomic Profiling and Analyses

Assessment of the metabolome provides the opportunity to evaluate interactions between genetics, lifestyle, diet, medications, environmental exposures, and the gut microflora as another source of interindividual variability in drug response. As a means for obtaining ketone bodies as well as additional metabolites that might influence response to canagliflozin, metabolomic profiling was performed in a subset of 216 participants using targeted high-throughput nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) (Nightingale Health Ltd., Helsinki, Finland) that quantified 249 metabolites, including ketone bodies, lipids and lipoprotein sub-fractions, fatty acids, amino acids, glycolysisrelated metabolites, and others. Plasma samples from the three time points were profiled and will be compared to identify the metabolic signature of drug exposure, namely, the significantly changed metabolites at Day 3 and/or Day 6 compared to baseline at Day 1.

## 3.2 | Baseline Characteristics Associated With Canagliflozin-Induced Glucosuria

The baseline characteristic most strongly associated with canagliflozin-induced glucosuria was eGFR (p = 8.10e−10) (Table 3). Upon dividing eGFR into tertiles, there was still a fiveto-six-fold variation in glucosuria within each tertile (Table 4). Sex (p = 8.65e−03), fasting plasma glucose (p = 3.67e−03), and lab (p = 6.31e−08) were also significantly associated with canagliflozin-induced glucosuric response. However, age, age2, and BMI were not significantly associated with canagliflozin-induced glucosuria (Table  3). The heritability of canagliflozin-induced glucosuria as calculated from this base model was 34% $\left( p = 8 . 6 9 \mathrm { e } - 0 3 \right)$ .

## 3.3 | Association Between Canagliflozin-Induced Glucosuria and Secondary Endpoints

Ideally, genetic variants associated with drug response (i.e., glucosuria) would be independent of those associated with adverse events. Therefore, we assessed the association between our primary endpoint, glucosuria, and our significantly changed secondary endpoint percent changes. Glucosuria was significantly associated with change in BHB at Day $\mathrm { \ ; ~ } ( p = 2 . 0 4 \mathrm { e } - 0 2 )$ . In addition, glucosuria was nominally associated with change in uric acid at Day 6, but this association was not significant after adjustment for multiple comparisons at an FDR < 0.05 (Table S3).

## 4 | Discussion

SGLT2 inhibitors represent an important class of oral antidiabetics with significant cardiovascular benefit that promote glucosuria, thereby resulting in loss of calories in the urine. Using a short-term canagliflozin intervention in healthy volunteers, we can identify clinically significant changes in biomarkers related to the efficacy and safety of SGLT2 inhibitors. Numerous findings from Amish volunteers have replicated in outbred populations. One example is the APOC3 R19X variant first identified in the Amish as associated with lower triglycerides and subsequently replicated in other populations and was found to offer cardioprotection, ultimately leading to the development of two

FDA-approved products targeting APOC3 [34, 48]. This missense variant is rare in the general population but is enriched in Amish. Such rare variants enriched in Amish are relevant to the general population for the following reasons: (1) clinical characteristics of complex traits/diseases impacted by these variants are not different between the Amish and general population and (2) variants for these complex traits/diseases in the Amish represent a subset of those found in the general European population since the Amish gene pool is derived from Central Europe.

The degree of variation in the magnitude of glucosuria we observed would potentially contribute to the substantial variation in glucose-lowering efficacy observed in patients with T2D. Importantly, canagliflozin-induced glucosuria was heritable, with a heritability estimate of 34%. A heritability of 34% is similar to other chronic diseases/traits. For example, SBP, fasting glucose, and fasting insulin have heritabilities of 24%, 30%, and 39%, respectively, based on analysis of approximately 8000 individuals from the ARIC cohort [49]. Among clinical factors evaluated, eGFR was the strongest predictor of canagliflozininduced glucosuria. However, there was still a substantial variability in glucosuria within each tertile of eGFR, suggesting there are additional factors that remain to be identified that underlie variation in canagliflozin-induced glucosuria. Consistent with known pharmacology of SGLT2 inhibitors, we observed a significant decrease in serum uric acid levels and increase in BHB [1] at Days 3 and 6. With regard to bone safety, we observed significantly increased levels of PTH and β-CTX, both of which cause bone resorption, at Day 3 and 6 of canagliflozin administration. We also observed a significant decrease in P1NP at Day 6, suggesting decreased bone formation. These changes in PTH, β-CTX, and P1NP represent biomarkers that would suggest a negative impact on bone health consistent with the decreased bone mineral density and increased fracture risk observed with canagliflozin [8] and is consistent with similar effects seen with dapagliflozin and empagliflozin on the FGF23/1,25(OH) D/ PTH axis [50, 51]. This early increase in PTH is consistent with our previously published data demonstrating an increase at 36 h [28, 47, 52].

Serum phosphorous and FGF23 were significantly increased at Day 3 and then significantly decreased at Day 6. Serum phosphorous and FGF23 are two elements in a negative feedback loop. When serum phosphorous is increased, it promotes secretion of FGF23, which triggers an increase in urinary phosphate which tends to bring the serum phosphorous back toward baseline.

Additionally, FGF23 decreases 1,25(OH) D. Given 1,25(OH) D exerts negative feedback on PTH secretion, there is, ultimately, a rise in PTH [53]. By evaluating these early time points, e.g., Day 3, we were able to observe acute effects, including the compensatory homeostatic mechanisms described herein.

At Day 3, 1,25(OH) D was significantly decreased, which we hypothesize is driven by the Day 3 increases in serum phosphorous and FGF23 and then over-corrected on Day 6. These changes in bone mineral biomarkers of serum phosphorous, FGF23, 1,25(OH) D, and PTH are consistent with published reports of SGLT2 inhibitors in patients with diabetes with and without kidney disease [50, 51, 54]. For reasons that are not completely understood, the PTH remains elevated at Day 6 despite the FGF23, serum phosphorous, and 1,25(OH) D levels returning to baseline or even overshooting baseline levels.

Taken together, these changes in serum phosphorous, FGF23, 1,25(OH) D, PTH, β-CTX, and P1NP are consistent with a negative impact on bone health. These changes also highlight the importance of studying early time points in order to gain deeper understanding of the impact of SGLT2 inhibitors on hormones regulating bone and mineral metabolism prior to and throughout the compensatory homeostatic mechanisms so that we can unveil insights into the potential underlying mechanisms of long-term side effects of these medications. The long-term goal of this research is to incorporate genetic variations and identify biomarkers that could help distinguish individuals at highest risk for various adverse effects versus those who may tolerate these medications well.

When thinking about how to implement precision medicinebased treatment decisions in diabetes, it will be important to understand the relationship between biomarkers for efficacy and safety. For example, are genetic variants associated with SGLT2 inhibitor efficacy independent of those associated with safety issues? Thus, we conducted preliminary phenotypic analyses assessing the association between canagliflozin-induced glucosuria and changes in our secondary endpoints that significantly changed in response to canagliflozin (i.e., BHB, P, FGF23, 1,25(OH) D, PTH, β-CTX, P1NP, Ca, UA, and SBP). We found the secondary endpoints to be largely independent of glucosuria, with the exception of BHB.

In summary, the GRC study will serve as a unique SGLT2 inhibitor pharmacogenomic study due to its focus not only on glucosuria, but on safety biomarkers as well. The study provides the opportunity to assess both genetic and other -omic biomarkers for SGLT2 inhibitor response in order to gain insights into mechanisms of benefit and adverse effects with the ultimate goal of providing precision-based therapeutics in the treatment of diabetes.
