# Adding Bortezomib and Bendamustine to High-Dose Melphalan in Autologous Haematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation for Relapsed Multiple Myeloma—A Single Centre Retrospective Study

## ABSTRACT

Introduction: High-dose melphalan (HDM) followed by autologous haematopoietic stem cell transplantation (ASCT) remains the standard of care for eligible patients with multiple myeloma (MM). Our objective was to evaluate the effectiveness and safety of adding bortezomib and bendamustine to melphalan (BBM) compared with HDM for relapsed MM, and investigate if BBM could offset the anticipated reduction in remission time observed after second ASCT (ASCT2) relative to first ASCT (ASCT1).

Method: We conducted a retrospective analysis of medical records of 43 patients with relapsed MM who received BBM following first-line HDM from November 2011 to October 2018 at Uppsala University Hospital, and compared them with a cohort of 43 patients receiving HDM immediately before and after the BBM era.

Results: The Kaplan–Meier estimated reduction in median time to next treatment was 26% for BBM- and 39% for HDM-treated patients in ASCT2 versus ASCT1 (p = 0.198), and 15% versus 39% in median progression-free survival (p = 0.0122). The estimated median overall survival after ASCT2 was 72.2 months versus 51.5 months (p = 0.14). Severe adverse events with BBM were consistent with those from HDM alone, although a trend toward increased risk of febrile neutropenia was observed.

Conclusion: Compared with standard HDM, the BBM-protocol in ASCT2 was associated with a smaller decline in progressionfree survival relative to the ASCT1, and a consistent trend toward higher effectiveness across other measures. These findings suggest that BBM may help preserve efficacy in the setting of repeated transplantation and underscore the need for larger prospective studies to confirm these results.

## 1 Introduction

Multiple myeloma (MM) is a chronic haematological malignancy that accounts for about 10% of all haematological cancers and 1% of all cancers in general [1]. High-dose melphalan (HDM)

followed by autologous haematopoietic stem cell transplantation (ASCT) has been a cornerstone in the treatment of MM for fit patients for almost 30 years [2–4], and is considered standard of care for eligible patients [2, 5, 6]. Although many new therapeutic agents have been introduced, the role of consolidation with

HDM and ASCT remains intact [7–10]. The UK Myeloma X study found that treating patients with a second ASCT (ASCT2) after relapse following ASCT in frontline therapy delayed disease progression and improved overall survival (OS) [11, 12]. Many different variations of conditioning regimens at ASCT have been unsuccessfully investigated to challenge HDM as the gold standard [13].

Bortezomib is a first-in-class proteasome inhibitor that has been used in many induction combinations for MM and has shown beneficial effects when combined with alkylating agents [14–16]. Adding bortezomib to HDM has been investigated by the Intergroupe Francophone du Myélome (IFM) study group that reported no increase in toxicity, but no statistical superiority in response rates, PFS or OS [17, 18]. A Danish–Swedish Phase II trial interestingly reported no difference in time to next treatment (TNT) and PFS between first ASTC (ASCT1) and ASCT2 when bortezomib was added to HDM in first relapse after conventional HDM [19].

Bendamustine hydrochloride is an alkylating agent with antimetabolite properties and manageable toxicity that is effective in MM [20, 21]. Bendamustine has been reported to have activity in p53-deficient cells resistant to standard therapy in vitro [22] and can overcome melphalan resistance in myeloma cell lines by inducing cell death through mitotic catastrophe [23]. Adding bendamustine to HDM has been investigated in a few Phase I and II studies, reporting acceptable toxicity and efficacy comparable to HDM [24–27].

Neither bortezomib nor bendamustine has shown any significant increase in toxicity when combined with HDM. To mitigate the anticipated reduction in TNT and PFS at ASCT2 compared to ASCT1, the conditioning regimen for patients eligible for ASCT2 at Uppsala University Hospital (UUH) was changed to bortezomib-bendamustine-melphalan (BBM) from 1 November 2011 to 31 October 2018. The objective of this study was to evaluate the effectiveness and safety of the conditioning regimen BBM and compare it to HDM in the setting of previously transplanted relapsed MM.

## 2 Materials and Methods

## 2.1 Data Collection

This retrospective single-centre cohort study compares the conditioning regimen BBM to standard HDM for relapsed MM after HDM in first-line treatment. Patients were identified through the local European Society for Blood and Marrow Transplantation (EBMT) registry at UUH. Patients from three regions referring patients to UUH for ASCT were included in the study; Dalarna, Gävleborg and Uppsala with a total population of 968,326 on 31 December 2022, accounting for 9.2% of the Swedish population [28].

The data sources were the local EBMT registry and electronic medical records. We used the International Myeloma Working Group’s definitions of diagnosis and response [29, 30]. All severe adverse events (AEs) were collected from the start of conditioning until Day +100 after ASCT2 according to the National Cancer

Institute’s Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE) version 5.0 [31].

## 2.2 Inclusion Criteria

The inclusion criteria were a diagnosis of MM, and first relapse after previous ASCT according to the International Myeloma Working Group, treatment with ASCT2 as part of second-line treatment at UUH and conditioning at ASCT2 with either BBM or HDM.

## 2.3 Exclusion Criteria

The exclusion criteria were tandem (double) ASCT in first-line treatment or failure to meet the minimal data requirements defined as: date of ASCT1 and ASCT2, date of progression and start of induction treatment following ASCT1 and ASCT2, medical records from hospitalisation for ASCT2 and at least one follow-up visit (unless early death before the first follow-up visit).

## 2.4 Endpoints

As we wanted to investigate whether BBM could offset the anticipated reduction in TNT and PFS after ASCT2 versus ASCT1, the primary endpoint was set to the Kaplan–Meier estimated relative difference in median TNT after ASCT2 compared to ASCT1. The secondary endpoints were the corresponding difference in median PFS, the Kaplan–Meier estimated median TNT, PFS and OS after ASCT2, OS at 2 years after ASCT2, the average TNT after ASCT2 compared to ASCT1 for each individual patient, and the depth of best response after ASCT2. The TNT, PFS and OS were calculated from the day of ASCT. Additional secondary endpoints were the treatment-related mortality rate, the mean duration of neutropenia (absolute neutrophil count [ANC] < 0.5 × 109/L) at ASCT2, the mean time until engraftment at ASCT2, the mean duration of hospitalisation after stem cell infusion at ASCT2, and the frequency of severe AEs.

## 2.5 Conditioning Protocols

The BBM-protocol consisted of bortezomib 1.3 mg/m2 subcutaneously on Days −5 and −2, bendamustine 100 mg/m2 intravenously on Days −3 and −2 and melphalan 200 mg/m2 intravenously (or 140 mg/m2, if the glomerular filtration rate was estimated to be below 30 mL/min) on Day −2. On Day −2, bendamustine was administered first, followed by bortezomib and melphalan. In the HDM-protocol, melphalan was administered as described above. See Appendix S1 for further information on procedures.

## 2.6 Statistical Analysis

Statistical analysis was performed with R v4.4.3 (using the packages ggplot2, survival, survimner, tidyverse devtools, lmtest, tidyr and gtsummary). Data are summarised using frequencies for categorical variables, medians and interquartile ranges for discrete variables and means and standard deviations for continuous variables. Frequencies are presented with a 95% confidence interval. To determine statistically significant differences between proportions, Fisher’s exact test was used. Differences in TNT, PFS and OS were estimated using the log-rank test in Kaplan–Meier plots. Unpaired Student’s t-test was used to determine statistical significance for means and proportions with normal distribution. Two-sample z-test was used to determine statistical significance for sample proportions. Univariable and multivariable linear regression analyses were performed to estimate the effects of key confounding variables. A two-tailed p-value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## 3 Results

## 3.1 Patient Inclusion

We identified 64 patients treated with ASCT2 between 1 November 2011 and 31 October 2018. Nine did not fulfil the inclusion criteria and 12 met at least one exclusion criterion. The final BBM-cohort consisted of 43 patients. We identified 27 consecutive patients treated with HDM before November 2011 and 53 after October 2018, of which 21 and 22 remained, respectively, after exclusions. The main reasons for exclusion were tandem transplantation in first-line treatment and ASCT2 performed later than in second treatment line (Figure 1). Data were collected between 4 January and 17 February 2025.

## 3.2 Patient Characteristics

The patients in the BBM-cohort had their ASCT2 between November 2011 and June 2018, and those in the HDM-cohort between June 2006 and July 2011 and November 2018 and February 2023, respectively. The cohorts were balanced overall. There were trends toward less deep responses prior to ASCT2 and more patients with plasmacytomas in the BBM-cohort, and more female and older patients and higher Charlson Comorbidity Score in the HDM-cohort. The median time between ASCT1 and ASCT2 was similar between the cohorts, 42.0 versus 42.3 months. For the BBM-treated patients, all were previously exposed to bortezomib, and one patient to bendamustine (Table 1).

## 3.3 Myeloma Treatment

The study period spans more than two decades; hence, the treatment of MM has changed dramatically over time (Table 2). Consolidation was only used in HDM-patients, one in first-line and three in second-line treatment. Maintenance therapy, primarily single-agent lenalidomide, was used in eight BBM-patients after ASCT2 for an average of 10 months, whereas 15 patients received maintenance therapy with single-agent thalidomide, lenalidomide, or bortezomib in the HDM-cohort, for an average of 16 months, p = 0.088.

## 3.4 In-Patient Care

All patients were mobilised using cyclophosphamide and granulocyte-colony stimulating factor (G-CSF), except one HDM-patient who received G-CSF only. Stem cell harvest for both ASCTs was performed before ASCT1, except for six patients in the first HDM-cohort, who had another harvest prior to ASCT2. The average time to neutrophil granulocyte count reaching 0.5 × 109/L or more was 13.4 (±1.70) days for the BBM-cohort, and 13.0 (±2.86) for the HDM-cohort, p = 0.43. G-CSF was used in six (12%) BBM-patients and 23 (53%) HDM-patients, p < 0.0001. The mean time until platelets were 20 × 109/L or more was shorter in the BBM-cohort; 11.3 (±1.60) days versus 13.2 (±3.16) for HDM-patients, p < 0.001. The average time until engraftment was 13.7 (±2.11) days for BBM-patients, and 14.1 (±3.63) for HDM-patients, $p \ : = \ : 0 . 5 4$ . Twenty BBMand 17 HDM-patients were treated in outpatient care for at least some part of the period of neutropenia. The average time of hospitalisation after stem cell infusion, including such outpatient care, was 16.7 days (±4.38) for BBM-patients and 15.3 days (±2.57) for HDM-patients, p = 0.074.

## 3.5 Primary Endpoint

The Kaplan–Meier estimated reduction in median TNT at ASCT2 compared to ASCT1 was 26% for BBM-patients and 39% for HDMpatients, p = 0.198 (Figure 2).

## 3.6 Secondary Endpoints

In Kaplan–Meier analysis, the decrease in median PFS after ASCT2 compared to ASCT1 was significantly smaller in the BBM-cohort, 15% compared to 39% in the HDM-cohort, p = 0.0122 (Figure 2). The median TNT after ASCT2 was 21.4 months (CI 15.6–29.7) in the BBM-cohort, and 19.0 months (CI 14.3–39.6) in the HDM-cohort, p = 0.84. The median PFS was 18.4 months (CI 14.0–23.7) versus 15.4 months (CI 11.2–26.7), p = 0.92, and the median OS was 72.2 (CI 40.8–125.3) months versus 51.5 months (CI 29.5–81.4), p = 0.14. After 2 years, the OS was 88% for BBMand 79% for HDM-treated patients, p = 0.24. The TNT after ASCT2 was on average 0.75 (±0.45) of the TNT after ASCT1 for each individual patient in the BBM-cohort compared to 0.66 (±0.41) for the HDM-cohort, p = 0.41 (Figure 3). In each cohort, 21 patients had improved at least one step in depth of best response (steps: stable disease, partial remission, very-good partial remission/complete remission) from ASCT2 compared to after ASCT1.

We conducted a univariable and multivariable regression analysis to assess the most important confounders affecting TNT after ASCT2. The analysis revealed that time between ASCT1 and ASCT2 was the most important confounder, followed by the Charlson Comorbidity Index and depth of best response prior to

ASCT2. In the multivariable analysis, conditioning with BBM was associated with a 5.0-month longer TNT, p = 0.163 (Appendix S2).

## 3.7 Safety

One patient in the BBM-cohort died 2.5 years after ASCT2, from chronic diarrhoea that had begun at ASCT2. In the HDMcohort, one patient died 13 days after ASCT2, resulting in a treatment-related mortality rate of 2.3%. The patient had prostate cancer, diabetes mellitus type II, and developed influenza and bacterial pneumonia in the neutropenic phase after ASCT2, which was complicated by acute respiratory distress syndrome and supraventricular arrhythmias.

In the BBM-cohort, 29 patients had died at the end of the study, of which 25 cases were due to relapse or progressive disease, three due to cardiovascular disease, and one from late complications of ASCT described above. In the HDM-cohort, there were 27 deaths, out of which 24 were due to relapse or progressive disease. Apart from the ASCT-related case already described, one patient died of sepsis and one from hyperinflammatory syndrome.

There was no statistically significant difference in the frequency of the total number of AEs. Oral mucositis and hypokalaemia were more common in the BBM-cohort, whereas nausea was more common in the HDM group. In the BBM-cohort, 13 patients received parenteral nutritional support, compared to 19 patients in the HDM-cohort, p = 0.18. The most common severe AE was febrile neutropenia in both cohorts. Febrile neutropenia occurred in 26 BBM-patients versus 17 HDM-patients, p = 0.052 (Table 3).

## 4 Discussion

The primary finding of this retrospective cohort study is that adding bortezomib and bendamustine to HDM as conditioning treatment at ASCT in second-line for MM was associated with a trend toward higher effectiveness in all endpoints, including TNT, PFS and most notably OS. Patients treated with BBM had a significantly smaller decline in PFS following ASCT2 compared to ASCT1 than HDM-patients. AEs with BBM were manageable and consistent with expectations for HDM alone, although a tendency toward increased febrile neutropenia was observed.

To account for inter-patient variability, we used the difference in TNT and PFS between ASCT1 and ASCT2 within each cohort to allow for better comparisons between the treatments. The study design with a control group before and after the BBMperiod was chosen to reduce the likelihood that early patients had a disadvantage in less effective treatment for MM, and correspondingly, late patients benefited from an advantage. We generally used medians as endpoints for that reason, and also to reduce the risk of outliers affecting the results. In Figure 3, the HDM curve crosses the BBM curve for TNT and PFS, most likely because the HDM-cohorts were enrolled at different periods. In contrast, no such pattern was observed for OS, which showed a consistent, though not statistically significant, advantage for BBM over HDM.

The reasons for any advantage of BBM are difficult to assess. Potentially, sensitisation of bendamustine-exposed myeloma cells to the effect of melphalan can play a role [23], and another is that the triplet BBM combination might suppress certain myeloma clones to a greater extent.

As BBM is a more intensive conditioning regimen, the trend toward more febrile neutropenia should be given attention. The frequency of febrile neutropenia was 52% in the early HDMcohort, compared to 27% in the latter, p = 0.0923. This indicates that there was a diminished risk of febrile neutropenia over time, which could have influenced the result. The more frequent use of G-CSF in HDM-patients could be another reason. We recommend watchfulness and strict adherence to preventive actions to minimise the risk of febrile neutropenia if using the BBM-protocol.

BBM over HDM is stronger in this study compared to previous studies of either bortezomib or bendamustine in combination with HDM. The IFM studies reported non-significant trends of superiority for bortezomib and HDM conditioning despite larger cohorts in the Phase III trial than in this study [17, 18]. It is possible that the addition of bendamustine in our study improved the outcomes. The effectiveness was better in this study compared to Mark and Gomez-Arteaga’s Phase I and II studies of bendamustine and HDM conditioning, but they also reported no increase in toxicity [24, 27]. It seems that adding a triplet treatment as conditioning improves effectiveness and possibly contributes to increased toxicity, as can be seen in the trend of febrile neutropenia.

This BBM-protocol uses available and approved drugs for MM. It takes three additional chronological days to administer, even if Days −5 to −3 could be given in an outpatient setting. The time to engraftment, although G-CSF was used more frequently in the HDM-cohort, was comparable. The trend toward longer hospitalisation after ASCT in the BBM-cohort might be explained by the trend toward more febrile neutropenia as discussed above.

There are several limitations of this study due to its design and rather small cohort sizes. First, much of the treatment and management of MM has changed during this study period. How, and to what extent, that impacted the results is difficult to judge. Second, as the management of the patients was done in routine care, local and individual traditions could have affected when, how and how often patients were assessed, when treatment was initiated, and which treatments were used. However, a good example that the cohorts could be compared is the almost identical time between ASCT1 and ASCT2, 42.0 versus 42.3 months, respectively, a factor that has been repeatedly recognised as the most important predictive factor of the effectiveness of

ASCT2 [32]. This was confirmed in our regression analysis. Third, as the cohorts were separated in time, there are relatively more patients in the second HDM-cohort who have not yet progressed, been treated or died at the time of data collection, which could underestimate the effectiveness of HDM in the latter cohort. Fourth, we did not include genetic risk status due to excessive missing data, but most likely there were more genetically highrisk patients included in the BBM-cohort, as only four patients were excluded because of tandem transplantation, compared to 25 patients in the HDM-cohort. Another limitation is the retrospective design with the inherent risk of information bias and uncontrolled confounders that could affect the results of the study. The regression analysis identified the Charlson Comorbidity Index, which was higher in the HDM-cohort, and depth of best response at ASCT2, which was lower in the BBM-cohort, as important factors affecting the TNT.

The use of either consolidation or maintenance therapy after ASCT2 was 19% in the BBM-cohort and 42% in the HDM-cohort, p = 0.019. However, maintenance was also used significantly less for BBM-patients compared to HDM-patients after ASCT1, 4.7% versus 23%, p = 0.013. Daratumumab was used in eight HDMpatients as induction or consolidation treatment for ASCT2. Of these eight patients, two had PR at the time of ASCT2, and six had achieved VGPR (75%), which can be compared with 34% achieving VGPR or better in the rest of the HDMcohort, p = 0.034, and 35% in the BBM-cohort at ASCT2. The more frequent use of consolidation and maintenance, including daratumumab, could constitute an advantage for HDM in this study.

In conclusion, compared with standard HDM, the BBM-protocol in ASCT2 was associated with a smaller decline in PFS relative to ASCT1. Patients treated with BBM also showed a consistent trend toward improved outcomes across other measures, particularly OS. These findings suggest that BBM may help preserve efficacy in the setting of repeated transplantation and underscore the need for larger prospective studies to confirm these results.
