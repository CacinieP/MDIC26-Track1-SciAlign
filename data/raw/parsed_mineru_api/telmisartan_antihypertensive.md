## ABSTRACT

Background Poly-ADP ribose polymerase inhibitors (PARPi) are well-known to treat tumors that are deficient in homologous recombination (HR) DNA repair. However, PARPi can treat breast cancer gene (BRCA) wild-type tumors, the mechanisms behind which are not fully elucidated. We previously reported that tumor-intrinsic programmed death ligand-1 (PD-L1) signals promote HR, and that genetic tumor PD-L1KO impairs HR and sensitizes tumors to PARPi. Despite significant PARPi efficacy in PD-L1KO tumors, US Food and Drug Administration (FDA)- approved anti-PD-L1 antibodies did not improve PARPi. Thus, additional clinically translatable approaches to target tumor-intrinsic PD-L1 signals to elicit PARPi sensitivity are needed.

Methods We used control and genetically modified murine and human tumor cell lines to demonstrate signaling outcomes and treatment efficacy of olaparib and telmisartan alone or combined in vitro with immunoblots, 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)−2,5-diphenyl-2H-tetrazolium bromide (MTT) viability assays, and quantitative reverse transcription-PCR (qRT-PCR). We tested treatment efficacy changes in the tumor immune microenvironment and immune treatment mechanisms in vivo using transplantable murine cancer models and flow cytometry. Results The FDA-approved angiotensin II receptor blocker (ARB) antihypertensive telmisartan depleted tumor PD-L1, synergized with PARPi, and augmented PARPi-induced DNA damage. Surprisingly, telmisartan improved PARPi independently of tumor PD-L1, which expands telmisartan applicability to improve PARPi in PD-L1− tumors. Telmisartan was the only ARB tested that depleted tumor PD-L1 and synergized with PARPi, suggesting that PARPi synergy in tumor cells is not an ARB class effect but is unique to telmisartan. Telmisartan potently increased cytosolic DNA and immunogenic tumor stimulator of interferon genes (STING) signals, including type I interferon (IFN-I) production, when combined with PARPi. Consistent with these data, telmisartan potently improved PARPi efficacy in vivo, but its efficacy was abolished by eliminating tumor STING or all IFN-I signals. ## WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

Here, we identified a novel anticancer application of telmisartan in synergizing with poly-ADP ribose polymerase inhibitors (PARPi), augmenting PARPi-induced DNA damage and immunogenic stimulator of interferon genes (STING) signals, and improving PARPi efficacy and tumor immunogenicity in vivo dependent on tumor STING and type I interferon (IFN-I). Our study identifies telmisartan as a novel agent to induce PARPi sensitivity in breast cancer gene (BRCA)-wild-type (WT) and homologous recombination (HR)- proficient tumors and the potential to overcome tumor PARPi treatment resistance. We additionally identify telmisartan as a tumor programmed death ligand-1 (PD-L1) depleting drug by promoting its ubiquitination, which lends additional therapeutic potential for telmisartan in cancer through inhibition of detrimental tumorintrinsic signals.

## HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE ORPOLICY

Our work provides strong experimental support for the use of telmisartan as an adjuvant to PARPi to broaden PARPi clinical applicability to non-BRCA mutant or HR-proficient tumors and identifies a novel function of telmisartan in cancer treatment. Telmisartan is orally bioavailable and relatively safe and tolerable, including in normotensive individuals, making it an ideal candidate for clinical translation. We have initiated a clinical trial (NCT06168487) testing telmisartan efficacy with PARPi and other FDA-approved DNA damaging agents in metastatic, castrate-resistant prostate cancer to assess utility of telmisartan for this purpose.

Tumors with an impaired DNA damage response (DDR) are generally more immunogenic than DDRproficient tumors due to factors including increased tumor mutational burden and increased type I interferon (IFN-I) from accumulated cytosolic DNA.11–13 PARPi are well known for inducing immunogenic stimulator of interferon genes (STING) signals contributing to their efficacy14–17 and thus could improve immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) efficacy by improving tumor immunogenicity.17–23 Despite compelling preclinical data, clinical trials combining ICB with PARPi have shown limited treatment efficacy, and none have led to FDA approvals.24 25 Further, small molecule STING agonists have not elicited significant clinical activity to date.26 Alternative strategies to augment PARPi-induced immunogenicity are thus also highly clinically relevant.

Programmed death ligand-1 (PD-L1, CD274) is an immune checkpoint molecule that engages immune cell programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1) to inhibit antitumor immunity when surface-expressed.27 PD-L1- blocking ICB (αPD-L1) inhibits this canonical surface PD-1/PD-L1 interaction to protect PD-1-expressing immune cells from inhibitory tumor or immune cell PD-L1 signals.28 However, recent work from us and others describes pathologic tumor-intrinsic PD-L1 signals that promote tumor treatment resistance.29 We recently described tumor-intrinsic PD-L1 signals that promote the DDR through supporting HR and Chk2 DNA damage signals leading to chemotherapy and small molecule DDR inhibitor resistance.30 31 We showed that genetic tumor PD-L1 depletion sensitizes HR-proficient, BRCA/Chk2 wild-type tumors to PARPi and Chk1 inhibitors (Chk1i) in vitro and in vivo and enhances olaparib-induced immunogenic tumor STING signals.30 31 However, αPD-L1 ICB did not replicate PD-L1KO or improve olaparib efficacy.30 Thus, pharmacologic inhibition of tumor-intrinsic PD-L1 signals is a translationally relevant strategy to elicit novel treatment vulnerabilities.

We previously described a high-throughput drug screen that identified FDA-approved drugs that are rapidly clinically translatable as repurposed tumor PD-L1 depletion drugs (PDDs)31–33 that improved treatments through PD-L1-supported immunogenicity and Chk2 targets.31–33 Here, we sought to identify a PDD that depleted tumor PD-L1 to impair HR, induce BRCAness, and sensitize tumors to PARPi. Our high throughput screen identified the angiotensin type II receptor blocker (ARB) antihypertensive drug telmisartan as a PDD. Although telmisartan potently depleted tumor PD-L1 content and synergized with the PARPi olaparib, it unexpectedly improved olaparib efficacy independently of tumor PD-L1 status and HR effects. Telmisartan significantly augmented olaparib-induced immunogenic STING signals, including the production of immunogenic chemokines and IFN-I, and sensitized BRCA wild-type (WT) tumors to olaparib in vivo through augmented IFN-I signals. Thus, telmisartan is a rapidly translatable drug to induce olaparib sensitivity in BRCA-WT tumors and generate novel treatment vulnerabilities through a mechanism distinct from BRCAness that could be further exploited for PD-L1 depletion effects.

## In vitro proliferation and viability assays

Tumor cells were seeded in 6-well plates and treated the following day with indicated drugs and concentrations without replenishment. Cells were trypsinized and harvested, and viable cells were counted using a Vi-CELL XR instrument at indicated days post treatment for proliferation assays.

For 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)−2,5-diphenyl-2H-tetraz olium bromide (MTT) viability assays, tumor cells were seeded into 96-well flat-bottom plates. Drugs were added the following day at indicated concentrations and duration. Plates were then incubated with MTT for 2 hours. Medium was fully decanted and 200 µL of dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) was added to each well. Plates were read at 490 nm with a BIO-TEK Synergy HT plate reader.

## Quantitative reverse transcription-PCR

Cells were lysed with TRIzol, and total RNA was isolated via phenol chloroform extraction. RNA quantity and quality were determined by NanoDrop. Complementary DNA was reverse transcribed from RNA with SuperScript III Reverse Transcriptase. Gene expression was determined by quantitative reverse transcription-PCR (qRT-PCR) using TaqMan Gene Expression Assays (Applied Biosystems) and a QuantStudio 3 qRT-PCR machine (Applied Biosystems). Values were normalized to GAPDH using the ΔΔCq method. All TaqMan primers were purchased from Thermo Fisher Scientific.

Cytosolic DNA was detected using an amended protocol as previously described.35 ID8agg cells were treated for 24 hours as indicated in the figure legend. Cells were lysed using a digitonin buffer, and the cytosolic fraction was extracted by serial centrifugation. Gene expression was determined by qRT-PCR using SYBR Green (Bio-Rad) and equipment as above. Values were normalized as described above, according to the Livak method. Primers were purchased from Integrated DNA Technologies (IDT) and include the following 5’−3’ sequences: β-actin (CCT CCC TGG AGA AGA GCT ATG, TTA CGG ATG TCA ACG TCA CAC), GAPDH (AAC TTT GGC ATT GTG GAA GG, GGA TGC AGG GAT GAT GTT CT), PPIA (GGG TTC CTC CTT TCA CAG AA, GAT GCC AGG ACC TGT ATG CT), and 18S (GGC CGT TCT TAG TTG GTG GAG CG, CTG AAC GCC ACT TGT CCC TC).

## RESULTS

## Telmisartan depletes tumor PD-L1 through a posttranslational mechanism

We conducted a high-throughput drug screen of molecules in the Prestwick and LOPAC libraries as previously described31–33 to identify drugs that potently deplete tumor PD-L1. Among hits identified from this screen was telmisartan, an FDA-approved ARB antihypertensive drug. Telmisartan potently depleted tumor PD-L1 from various cancer cell lines, including murine 4T1 TNBC, MC38 colon cancer, and ID8agg ovarian cancer models by immunoblots (figure 1a) and flow cytometry (online supplemental figure S1a,b). ID8agg cells were generated by us through multiple in vivo passages of parental ID8 cells to make them more aggressive and treatment resistant.39 Tumor PDDs we previously reported (chlorambucil, cefepime, ceftazidime) deplete tumor PD-L1 through a post-translational mechanism.32 33 Similarly to other drugs described, telmisartan modestly reduced CD274 transcripts in MC38 cells but not in 4T1 cells (figure 1b), implicating a post-translational mechanism for PD-L1 depletion. Consistently, PD-L1 was highly ubiquitinated in MC38 cells in telmisartan-treated cells versus vehicle-treated cells (figure 1c). To evaluate whether telmisartan PD-L1-depletion effects were specific to tumor cells, we cultured splenocytes from C57/BL6 (WT) mice in vitro with telmisartan and found it did not affect PD-L1 expression or prevalence of splenocytes expressing PD-L1 (figure 1d) or splenocyte viability (online supplemental figure S1c). In addition to telmisartan, there are seven other FDA-approved ARBs (candesartan, irbesartan, olmesartan, losartan, eprosartan, valsartan and azilsartan medoxomil).40 Of the six available ARBs tested, telmisartan was the only one that potently depleted tumor PD-L1 (figure 1e, online supplemental figure S1d). These data support telmisartan as a bona fide PDD and that its PDD properties are not an ARB class effect.

Further, in contrast to PD-L1KO, which generally suppressed mTORC1 signals in our prior studies of genetic and pharmacologic PD-L1 depletion31 39 41, telmisartan increased mTORC1 signals detected as increased phospho-P70S6K and phospho-4EBP1,42 but autophagy remained unchanged (online supplemental figure S2g). ## Telmisartan synergizes with olaparib to increase cell cytotoxicity and DNA damage in vitro

Another important tumor-intrinsic PD-L1 signal outcome that we previously reported is its promotion of HR. Reliably, genetic tumor PD-L1 depletion impairs HR and sensitizes BRCA WT tumor cells to PARPi in vivo, thus inducing BRCAness. However, anti-PD-L1 antibody did not improve olaparib efficacy in vivo, 30 highlighting the efficacy of targeting tumor-intrinsic PD-L1 signals distinctly from surface PD-L1 to enhance PARPi efficacy. Telmisartan significantly improved the efficacy of the PARPi olaparib in vitro in multiple tumor cell lines (figure 2a), which was synergistic by formal Bliss synergy analysis of ID8agg cells (figure 2b). Telmisartan also significantly augmented olaparib-induced DNA damage as seen by increased phospho-histone H2AXSer139 (γH2AX) in multiple tumor cell lines (figure 2c). Notably, telmisartan did not induce DNA damage in normal mouse splenocytes (figure 2d), consistent with relative tumor PD-L1 depletion selectivity.

## Telmisartan enhances G2/M cell cycle arrest and uniquely improves olaparib efficacy among ARBs

Telmisartan reportedly elicits cell cycle arrest at the G0/ G1 transition,43–48 except in one report where telmisartan induced G2/M cell cycle arrest in renal cell carcinoma.49 Given that olaparib induces G2/M cell cycle arrest50–52 but previous reports predominantly implicate telmisartan in affecting G0/G1, we sought to determine telmisartan effects on the cell cycle when combined with olaparib. In contrast to previous studies in other cell lines, we found that telmisartan alone elicited minimal effects on the cell cycle in both ID8agg and MC38 cells (figure 3a–d). While olaparib alone increased G2/M cell cycle arrest as expected, telmisartan potently augmented olaparibinduced G2/M cell cycle arrest (figure 3a–d). Previous studies assessing telmisartan effects on the cell cycle used concentrations 10-fold higher than the 10 µM used in our study.43–47 Thus, there may be concentration-dependent effects, cell line-dependent cell cycle effects, or off-target effects of telmisartan, especially at high concentrations. Nonetheless, our data support telmisartan effects in the G2/M portion of the cell cycle to augment olaparib efficacy.

We sought to determine whether telmisartan cytotoxic synergy with olaparib is an ARB class effect. As for tumor PD-L1 depletion, telmisartan was the only ARB tested that synergized with olaparib for cytotoxicity, validated in two distinct cell lines in vitro (figure 3e,f). These data support a telmisartan-specific effect, not an ARB class effect, and suggest telmisartan effects are independent of the angiotensin II receptor.

## Telmisartan augmentation of olaparib cytotoxicity is tumor PD-L1-independent

We expected to confirm PD-L1 dependence of telmisartan synergy with olaparib, as all other PDDs we have assessed to date elicited tumor cytotoxicity through tumor PD-L1 depletion.31–33 Surprisingly, telmisartan improved olaparib efficacy in genetically PD-L1-depleted cell lines to the same extent as respective PD-L1-replete cell lines (compare figure 4a to figure 2a). Additionally, telmisartan induced significant DNA damage by γH2AX immunoblot in multiple genetically PD-L1-deficient cell lines similarly to DNA damage in PD-L1-replete lines (figure 4b), supporting PD-L1 independence of telmisartan-induced DNA-damage synergy with olaparib. Thus, telmisartan could be more widely applicable to treat cancers versus other PDDs, as it has significant PD-L1-independent ability to augment olaparib cytotoxicity.

Previously, we showed that the PDD, cefepime, suppressed tumor PD-L1 expression during ongoing, drug-induced DNA damage.31 However, in in vitro cultures of telmisartan plus olaparib, both PD-L1 protein content (figure 4c) and CD274 transcripts (figure 4d) were significantly elevated versus single agent olaparib. These data are consistent with previous reports describing tumor PD-L1 upregulation with DNA damage.11 53 As cefepime can suppress tumor PD-L1 during ongoing DNA damage,31 33 we hypothesized that suppressing tumor PD-L1 with cefepime would elicit PD-L1-dependent telmisartan-mediated olaparib synergy or augment its potency. However, adding cefepime to telmisartan plus olaparib did not significantly alter the telmisartan synergy effect (online supplemental figure S3a), further supporting PD-L1 independence of telmisartan synergy with olaparib. Finally, to understand the lack of PD-L1 dependence of telmisartan in these effects, we found that it also could not reduce PD-L1 induction by IFN-γ or tumor necrosis factor-α (online supplemental figure S3b), known tumor PD-L1-inducing cytokines.54 55 These data collectively indicate that telmisartan cannot maintain tumor PD-L1 depletion either during ongoing DNA damage or in the presence of these PD-L1-inducing antitumor cytokines.

We next investigated whether telmisartan functionally impairs HR DNA repair, which is known to elicit PARPi sensitivity1 and is a consequence of genetic tumor PD-L1 depletion.30 BRCA1 initiates HR through promoting DNA end resection that produces 3’ singlestranded DNA at the double-stranded DNA break site.56 Adding telmisartan to olaparib significantly increased phospho-RPA32 that stabilizes single-stranded RNA during double-strand DNA damage repair, by immunoblot (figure 4e), indicative of functional DNA end resection.56 BRCAness is defined as the sensitivity of tumor cells to PARPi in the presence of WT BRCA1 due to other defects that impair HR.4 As the tumors tested are BRCA WT, we tested whether telmisartan impaired HR. We found that total protein content of BRCA1 was unaffected by telmisartan (figure 4f) and that the total content of 53BP1, a major protein in non-homologous end joining (NHEJ), was also unchanged (figure 4f). Given that defects in HR elicit PARPi synthetic lethality and NHEJ defects augment PARPi efficacy,57 these data suggest that the mechanism for olaparib synergy is not due to impaired HR or NHEJ effects. To confirm the lack of suppressed HR and NHEJ, we showed that telmisartan did not alter functional HR, NHEJ, or single-strand annealing using validated functional assays (figure 4g). These data are surprising given that PARP inhibitors canonically treat HR-deficient tumor cells.1 However, these data support that telmisartan improves olaparib efficacy independently of HR, NHEJ, and single-strand annealing effects, and suggest an alternative mechanism for augmented DNA damage and PARPi efficacy induced by telmisartan. Additionally, these data are consistent with tumor PD-L1 independence of telmisartan effects on olaparib sensitivity, as tumor-intrinsic PD-L1 induces PARPi sensitivity through impairing HR.30

STING can be activated when damaged DNA enters the cytoplasm and is sensed by cyclic GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS).59 We assessed whether telmisartan increases cytosolic DNA and subsequently STING signals when combined with olaparib through enhancing DNA damage. We used qRT-PCR to assess distinct nuclear gene content in the cytoplasm and compiled normalized transcripts for the nuclear genes GAPDH, 18S, β-actin, and PPIA per a validated protocol35 (figure 5b, online supplemental figure S4b). We aggregated data for individual nuclear genes together to assess trends among general cytosolic DNA of nuclear origin, as has been previously shown in an electrophoresis protocol.35 60 Consistent with the foregoing data, telmisartan potently augmented accumulation of cytosolic DNA of nuclear origin when combined with olaparib but not as a single agent (figure 5b). These data support that telmisartan augments DNA damage from olaparib to increase cytosolic DNA for STING activation.

We next assessed downstream STING signal induction and effector molecules by telmisartan. STING signals canonically induce transcription of inflammatory chemokines and cytokines, including $\mathrm { I F N - I } . ^ { 6 1 }$ Telmisartan potently augmented the transcription of STING effectors58 including CCL5, CXCL9, and the IFN-I IFN-β (figure 5c). Telmisartan also significantly increased the transcription of IFN-stimulated genes including MX1, ISG20, ISG15, IRF7, IFIT3, IFIT1, and IFIT4 (figure 5c), all indicative of increased tumor IFN-I signals. As telmisartan potently increased IFN-I production when combined with olaparib, and IFN-I increases Cd274 messenger RNA, we hypothesized that increased IFN-I in the combination drove PD-L1 expression beyond the ability of telmisartan to suppress its expression. We blocked IFN-I signals with αIFNAR1 or isotype control in tumor cell cultures using telmisartan plus olaparib in vitro. Strikingly, the significant increase in PD-L1 with the combination of telmisartan and olaparib was abolished with IFNAR1 blockade, while DNA damage was relatively unaffected (figure 5d), implicating IFN-I signals as mediators of increased PD-L1 expression during DNA damage but not of telmisartanmediated DNA damage augmentation with olaparib.

## Type I interferon production contributes to in vivo telmisartan synergy with olaparib

To assess in vivo consequences of IFN-I production elicited by telmisartan and olaparib-induced STING activation, we challenged C57/BL6 (WT) mice subcutaneously with MC38 cells and treated them with telmisartan, olaparib, or their combination. A schematic of all in vivo experiments in figure 6 is included for clarity (online supplemental figure S5). While single-agent telmisartan and olaparib each had negligible effect on tumor growth control in vivo, telmisartan combined with olaparib potently controlled tumor growth (figure 6a,b). We next tested immune contributions to telmisartan synergy with olaparib in vivo by using ${ \bf R A G 1 } ^ { \mathrm { K O } }$ mice that lack functional adaptive immunity. While telmisartan still improved olaparib efficacy, the effect in ${ \bf R A G 1 } ^ { \mathrm { K O } }$ mice was modest compared with that in WT mice (figure 6c,d), suggesting a largely adaptive immune contribution to in vivo efficacy, though direct effects on tumor cytotoxicity are not excluded. Given that telmisartan potently augments IFN-I production with olaparib, we tested IFN-I contributions to in vivo efficacy. We challenged WT mice with MC38 cells and treated them with telmisartan plus olaparib ± αIFNAR1 (figure 6e). Strikingly, blocking IFN-I signals with αIFNAR1 eliminated combination efficacy in vivo (figure 6e,f). For further validation, we challenged WT and $\mathrm { I F N A R 1 ^ { \mathrm { K O } } }$ mice with MC38 cells and treated them with telmisartan and olaparib. Combination efficacy was similarly abrogated in $\mathrm { \Phi _ { I F N A R 1 } ^ { \mathrm { \tiny { K O } } } }$ mice (figure 6g,h), supporting host IFN-I as a key efficacy mediator and implicating contributions from non-tumor cell IFN-I signals to tumor control. A previous report found that MC38 tumors have accelerated growth in IFNAR1KO mice.62 In some experiments, IFNAR blockade elicited faster tumor growth versus in the absence of IFNAR blockade (compare figure 6e to figure 6a). In a complementary experiment, we challenged WT mice with MC38 cells and treated them with αIFNAR1 ± olaparib or the combination of telmisartan + olaparib. These data established that IFNAR signals were essential for olaparib efficacy as expected. Further, combination treatment efficacy was again abrogated (figure 6i,j) when tumor growth was comparable to growth in the absence of IFNAR blockade (compare figure 6i to figure 6a), confirming IFN-I signals contribute to telmisartan + olaparib efficacy in vivo.

## Telmisartan augments olaparib efficacy through tumor STING activation

We next tested whether telmisartan efficacy with olaparib in vivo was due to increased tumor STING signals that augment IFN-I as we showed in vitro. We generated empty vector CTRL and $\mathrm { S T I N G } ^ { \mathrm { K O } }$ MC38 cells with CRISPR/ Cas9 (online supplemental figure S5), challenged WT mice with them subcutaneously, and treated with olaparib ± telmisartan. As predicted, $\mathrm { S T I N G } ^ { \mathrm { K O } }$ tumors grew significantly faster than CTRL tumors (p=0.0002 by twoway ANOVA in figure 6k; p=0.0012 by unpaired t-test in figure 6l), consistent with loss of tumor immunogenicity through inhibiting STING and its downstream immunogenic signals. The combination of olaparib with telmisartan potently controlled CTRL MC38 tumor growth versus olaparib alone (figure 6k,l), consistent with our previous data. However, combination treatment efficacy was abrogated in $\mathrm { S T I N G } ^ { \mathrm { K O } }$ tumors (figure 6k,l). Collectively, these data support an efficacy mechanism in which telmisartan elicits tumor STING when combined with olaparib in vivo and are consistent with the requirement for IFN-I in treatment efficacy (figure 6i,j).

## Blocking IFN-I signals reduces telmisartan and olaparibinduced antitumor immunity

To assess the tumor immune microenvironment (TME), we performed flow cytometry on tumors from figure 6e,f that were treated with telmisartan plus olaparib ± isotype control or αIFNAR1 to block all IFN-I signals in vivo. Strikingly, the prevalence of IFNγ + and granzyme $\mathbf { B } ^ { + } \thinspace \mathrm { C D 8 ^ { + } ~ T }$ cells and their per-cell IFN-γ and granzyme B production by mean fluorescence intensity (MFI) were significantly reduced with αIFNAR1 treatment (figure 7a–d). Nonetheless, total T-cell content (by CD3 expression) and CD8+ T-cell content were unchanged (online supplemental figure S6a,b). These data are consistent with αIFNAR1 suppression of telmisartan and olaparib combinationinduced antitumor T-cell functions but not content. Total CD4+ T-cell prevalence was also unchanged by αIFNAR1, but prevalence of deleterious $\mathrm { C D 2 5 } ^ { \mathrm { h i } }$ FoxP3+ CD4+ regulatory T cells $\left( \mathrm { T r e g s } \right) ^ { 6 3 }$ was significantly decreased (online supplemental figure S6c,d) which could contribute to treatment efficacy, requiring further investigation.

In further support of IFN-I signal blockade suppressing telmisartan and olaparib combination treatment-induced antitumor immunity, αIFNAR1 potently and significantly reduced γδT cell per cell IFN-γ production by MFI and trended to decrease the prevalence of IFN-γ + γδT cells although without reaching statistical significance (figure 7e,f). αIFNAR1 also significantly decreased the prevalence and production of granzyme B in γδ T cells (figure 7g,h) and increased total γδ T-cell prevalence in the TME (online supplemental figure S6e), suggesting that blocking IFN-I signals augments their tumor infiltration but reduces their effector functions. In antitumor natural killer (NK) cells, αIFNAR1 did not affect IFN-γ-expressing NK cell prevalence or per-cell IFN-γ production but did significantly decrease granzyme B-expressing NK cell prevalence and per-cell granzyme B production (figure 7i–l). This data is similar to that of γδ T cells, however total NK cell prevalence was unchanged (online supplemental figure S6f).

## DISCUSSION

FDA-approved PARPi include those used to treat cancers that are BRCA-mutant or otherwise deficient in HR DNA repair.2 64 While effective, their utility is hampered by the need for defective tumor HR for optimal efficacy and the almost inevitable development of PARPi resistance.9 10 Despite these limitations, some PARPi are effective in treating select BRCA WT cancers.3 Inhibiting selected targets, including CDK165 and EZH266 among others,4 can induce BRCAness and sensitize HR-proficient tumors to PARPi by suppressing HR directly or indirectly. We recently showed that inhibiting tumor-intrinsic PD-L1 signals through genetic tumor PD-L1 depletion impairs HR and sensitizes BRCA WT tumors to PARPi, thus inducing BRCAness.30 Others have reported that tumor-intrinsic PD-L1 signals support the DDR through additional mechanisms, including promoting mRNA stability and DNA double-strand break repair that elicits tumor sensitivity to radiation and chemotherapy.67 68

We initially identified telmisartan as a PDD and tested telmisartan effects on HR function and olaparib sensitivity in the context of impairing tumor-intrinsic PD-L1 signals. Surprisingly, telmisartan synergy with olaparib for tumor cytotoxicity was PD-L1-independent, which expands its clinical utility to both PD-L1-replete and PD-L1-deficient tumors. Contrasting with reported agents that augment PARPi efficacy by interfering with HR,4 65 66 we showed that telmisartan potently augmented PARPi efficacy and PARPi-induced DNA damage without impairing functional HR or reducing content of major HR proteins. Telmisartan also did not interfere with non-homologous end joining or single-strand annealing DDR mechanisms. Nonetheless, augmented cytotoxicity, DNA damage, and G2/M cell cycle arrest elicited by telmisartan plus olaparib were observed across multiple distinct cell lines. This novel mechanism for improving PARPi efficacy could be exploited clinically in BRCA WT tumors and in PARPi resistance.

PARPi elicit significant STING activation .11 14 We found that telmisartan alone had negligible effect on STING activation but synergistically augmented olaparib-mediated STING signals that are known to increase tumor immunogenicity.11 14 Telmisartan further increased cytoplasmic DNA content with olaparib. Thus, the mechanism for STING activation here is likely from the additional significant DNA damage elicited by adding telmisartan leading to increased cytoplasmic DNA and ensuing STING activation59. We validated that both tumor STING and downstream IFN-I signals are required for olaparib treatment enhancement by telmisartan in vivo. Clinical trials combining PARPi with ICB have not yet yielded significant efficacy results despite encouraging preclinical data.24 25 Testing telmisartan to improve PARPi effects on ICB efficacy through increasing tumor immunogenicity by augmenting immunogenic STING signals merits further investigation.

We found that telmisartan increased cytoplasmic DNA when combined with olaparib, which activates STING. Cytoplasmic DNA can accumulate in cancer cells from significant DNA damage, which we demonstrated is induced by telmisartan when combined with olaparib. While PARPi famously induces irreparable DNA damage and synthetic lethality in HR-deficient tumor cells,1 we found that telmisartan does not impair HR, nonhomologous end joining, or single strand annealing. However, other work has investigated mechanisms independent of HR that induce olaparib sensitivity. For example, one study showed that inhibiting the ABC transporter MDR1 induced olaparib sensitivity in PARPiresistant tumor cells.69 The alkylating agent temozolomide induced PARPi sensitivity independently of HR.70 In addition, a clinical trial in metastatic castrate-resistant prostate cancer demonstrated that the hormonal agent abiraterone induced sensitivity to olaparib independent of tumor HR status.71 Further work is required to elucidate the specific mechanism behind telmisartan-induced olaparib sensitivity in HR-proficient tumor cells without impeding HR.

Although we initially identified telmisartan as a PDD, it did not elicit many reported PD-L1-dependent outcomes seen with genetic PD-L1KO39 41 or when using other FDAapproved drugs that we repurposed as PDDs, including chlorambucil, cefepime, and ceftazidime.32 33 Previously, we showed that cefepime and ceftazidime impair the Chk2 DDR and sensitize tumor cells to Chk1i.31 33 However, cefepime did not sensitize tested cancer cells to PARPi,33 indicating select functions of PDDs in inhibiting tumorintrinsic PD-L1 signals that merit further investigation.

Our study has limitations. Here, we show that telmisartan potently and synergistically augments olaparib efficacy in selected cancers in vitro and in vivo yet does not functionally impair HR, non-homologous end joining, or single strand annealing. We did not exclude additional mechanisms for telmisartan immunogenicity induction, all areas worthy of additional studies. We cannot exclude a telmisartan/olaparib synergy effect that includes ARB signals, but our data support a non-ARB moiety as mediating the PARPi synergy effects studied here given the lack of synergy of other ARBs with olaparib.

In summary, we identified the antihypertensive ARB drug telmisartan as a rapidly translatable drug to improve PARPi efficacy and overcome PARPi resistance clinically. Telmisartan potently synergized with olaparib for cancer cell cytotoxicity in multiple distinct tumor models, augmented olaparib-induced DNA damage and immunogenic STING signals, and improved olaparib efficacy in vivo in BRCA WT tumors, demonstrating potential clinical utility. The mechanism of PARPi synergy with telmisartan differs from other approaches requiring de novo or elicited HR defects. As telmisartan is FDA approved, relatively safe, tolerable, and orally bioavailable, it is an excellent candidate for clinical translation as an adjuvant to PARPi. In this regard, we recently opened a clinical trial (NCT06168487) testing telmisartan efficacy in combination with PARPi among other treatments in metastatic, castrate-resistant prostate cancer.
