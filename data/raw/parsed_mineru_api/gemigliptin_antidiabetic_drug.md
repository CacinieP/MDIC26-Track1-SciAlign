## ABSTRACT

Envlo (enavogliflozin) 0.3 mg, an SGLT2 inhibitor, was approved in Korea in 2022 for glycemic control in type 2 diabetes (T2DM). This study evaluates its safety, pharmacokinetics, and food effects. Healthy subjects (age ≥ 19, weight ≥ 50.0 kg, body mass index (BMI) 18.0–30.0 kg/m2 ) were enrolled. Study I was a randomized, two-way crossover study with an 8-day washout, where subjects received either a separate or a fixed-dose combination (FDC) of enavogliflozin 0.3 mg/gemigliptin 50 mg. Study II, a randomized, open-label, two-way crossover design with a 7-day washout, compared the food effect of a single dose of enavogliflozin in fed versus fasted after a high-fat meal. In study I, enavogliflozin reached peak plasma concentration at 1.25 h (median) in both groups. The mean half-life $( t _ { 1 / 2 } )$ was also comparable, recorded as 12.56 ± 4.04 h for the separate combination and 12.23 ± 3.46 h for the FDC. Gemigliptin peaked at 2.00 h in the separate combination and at 3.00 h in the FDC. The mean $t _ { 1 / 2 }$ was $1 8 . 0 4 \pm 1 . 7 5 \mathrm { h }$ for the separate combination and $1 8 . 6 7 \pm 2 . 5 4 \mathrm { h }$ for the FDC. In study II, the plasma concentration of enavogliflozin 0.3 mg peaked at 1.25 and 2.00 h (median), indicating a delayed $T _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ in the fed group. The average $t _ { { } _ { 1 / 2 } }$ was similar at 12.49 ± 2.12 h and 12.30 ± 2.81 h, respectively. The FDC of enavogliflozin and gemigliptin demonstrated pharmacokinetic equivalence and comparable safety to their co-administration as separate agents. Furthermore, the systemic exposure of enavogliflozin was not affected by food intake, supporting its potential for flexible, meal-independent use in clinical settings.

## 1 | Introduction

Diabetes is a chronic, metabolic disease characterized by elevated levels of blood glucose, which, over time, leads to serious damage to the heart, blood vessels, eyes, kidneys, and nerves. According to the current classification, diabetes is broadly divided into type 1 diabetes (T1DM) and type 2 diabetes (T2DM)

[1]. While T1DM is an autoimmune disease in which the insulinproducing beta cells of the pancreas are attacked and destroyed by the immune system, T2DM is a metabolic disorder caused by either insufficient insulin production or insulin resistance [2]. Antidiabetic medications can be broadly classified into subcutaneous injections and oral agents. Considering factors such as patient convenience and the potential for combination therapy with other medications, clinicians predominantly prescribe oral agents for glycemic control in patients with T2DM. Among oral antidiabetic agents, metformin is widely recognized as the most preferred and cost-effective first-line therapy [4–7]. In 2002, it became the most widely prescribed oral hypoglycemic agent, and in 2005, the International Diabetes Federation established guidelines recommending metformin as the first-line therapy for T2DM [8]. As T2DM progresses, achieving and maintaining target glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) levels may necessitate combination therapy with multiple glucose-lowering agents, including sodium-glucose cotransporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors and dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (DPP-4) inhibitors [9]. DPP-4 inhibitors stimulate insulin secretion in a glucose-dependent manner while simultaneously suppressing excessive glucagon secretion, thereby improving glycemic control in patients with T2DM [10].

In contrast, SGLT2 inhibitors reduce blood glucose levels by selectively inhibiting SGLT2, a key transporter responsible for glucose reabsorption in the renal proximal tubule, independent of insulin secretion. This inhibition promotes renal glucose excretion, thereby contributing to glycemic control [11–13]. The inhibition of SGLT2 works in a blood glucose-dependent manner, allowing approximately 60–100 g of glucose to be excreted in the urine each day. Given that obesity is a major risk factor for prediabetes and T2DM due to its strong association with insulin resistance and β-cell dysfunction, SGLT2 inhibition-mediated weight reduction may offer additional metabolic benefits for patients with diabetes. SGLT2 inhibitors directly induce weight loss by promoting glucose excretion (and thus calorie loss) through the kidneys [14].

Daewoong Pharmaceutical Co. Ltd. developed Envlo 0.3 mg (enavogliflozin), an SGLT2 inhibitor approved in November 2022 as an adjunct to diet and exercise for improving glycemic control in patients with T2DM. It is administered once daily at a dose of 0.3 mg, regardless of meals, as either monotherapy or add-on therapy. As monotherapy, enavogliflozin 0.3 mg effectively lowers fasting blood glucose and exerts favorable effects on body weight, blood pressure, and lipid profile in patients with T2DM [11, 12, 15, 16].

Here, we conducted two studies to characterize the pharmacokinetic properties of enavogliflozin. One study assessed the safety and pharmacokinetic equivalence between separate combination and fixed-dose combination (FDC) of enavogliflozin/gemigliptin. Another study investigated the effect of food on the safety and pharmacokinetics of enavogliflozin. These studies aimed to provide additional insights into the safety and pharmacokinetic profile of enavogliflozin and explore the potential for expanding oral therapy options for patients with T2DM.

## 2 | Methods

All subjects provided written informed consent to participate in the studies. Eligible participants were healthy adults aged 19 years or older, with a body weight of at least 50.0 kg and a BMI between 18.0 and 30.0 kg/m2. They underwent clinical assessments, including physical examinations and clinical laboratory tests, during the screening process to determine eligibility for participation. Subjects were enrolled after thorough screening according to established inclusion and exclusion criteria (Table S1).

The study protocol and informed consent form were approved by the Korea Ministry of Food and Drug Safety and the Institutional Review Board (IRB) of Chungbuk National University Hospital (Study I: IRB No. 2022-12-014, Study II: IRB No. 2023-01-040). Two clinical trials were performed at the Clinical Trials Center, Chungbuk National University Hospital, Cheongju, Korea (ClinicalTrials.gov registry no.: Study I: NCT05737771, Study II: NCT05797922). Moreover, this investigation was conducted under the principles stipulated in the International Council on Harmonization Good Clinical Practice Guideline and the Declaration of Helsinki as amended in October 2013 (Fortaleza, Brazil).

## 2.1 | Study Design

Study I was a randomized, open-label, oral, single-dose, twoway crossover with an 8-day washout period designed to evaluate the combination of enavogliflozin/gemigliptin. The objective was to compare the pharmacokinetic equivalence and safety of the FDC versus separate combination. A total of 40 subjects were randomly assigned to two groups. All subjects were fasted except for water for 10 h overnight prior to administration. Depending on their assigned treatment group, they received either one tablet of enavogliflozin 0.3 mg/gemigliptin 50 mg FDC or a separate combination of enavogliflozin 0.3 mg (Daewoong Pharmaceuticals, Seoul, South Korea) and Zemiglo Tab 50 mg (gemigliptin tartaric acid 1.5 hydrate; LG Chem, Seoul, South Korea).

Study II was a randomized, open-label, single-dose, oral, twoway crossover study with a 7-day washout period conducted to assess the effect of food on the safety and pharmacokinetic properties of enavogliflozin. A total of 32 subjects were randomized into two groups. Subjects in the fed group received highfat meals comprising 800–1000 kcal (containing approximately 150 kcal of protein and 250 kcal of carbohydrates) with 50% fat. The participants consumed their meals within 30 min of the designated start time and then received one tablet of enavogliflozin 0.3 mg with approximately 240 mL of water. Subjects in the fasted group were fasted except for water for at least 10 h overnight and then received one tablet of the same investigational product with about 240 mL of water.

## 2.2 | Pharmacokinetics

Participants who received the investigational drug and completed all pharmacokinetic blood sampling schedules without significant protocol violations that could potentially affect pharmacokinetic evaluation were analyzed. The linear up log down method was applied using Phoenix WinNonlin Version 8.3 (Certara, NJ, USA) to calculate pharmacokinetic parameters and assess pharmacokinetic equivalence between treatment groups.

For the FDC study, the primary pharmacokinetic endpoints, $\mathrm { A U C } _ { 0 - \mathrm { t } }$ and $C _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of enavogliflozin and gemigliptin, were logtransformed to obtain geometric mean ratios (GMRs) and their 90% confidence intervals (CIs). If the transformed 90% CI fell within the range of 80.00%–125.00%, the pharmacokinetic properties of FDC and the separate combination of enavogliflozin/ gemigliptin were considered equivalent.

In the food effect assessment study, the primary pharmacokinetic endpoints, $\mathrm { A U C } _ { 0 - 1 }$ and $C _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ of enavogliflozin, were log-transformed to calculate the GMRs and their 90% CIs. If the transformed 90% CIs fell within the range of 80.00%– 125.00%, the pharmacokinetic properties of enavogliflozin between fasted and fed groups were considered equivalent. Additionally, key pharmacokinetic parameters, including time to reach the maximum blood concentration $( T _ { \mathrm { m a x } } )$ , elimination half-life $( t _ { 1 / 2 } ) _ { : }$ , apparent volume of drug distribution (Vd/F), and apparent clearance (CL/F), were evaluated in both studies.

For pharmacokinetic evaluation of enavogliflozin, blood samples were collected at pre-dose (0 h) and at 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1, 1.25, 1.5, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24, 48, and 72 h post-dose. For gemigliptin, blood samples were collected at pre-dose (0 h) and at 0.5, 1, 1.5, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 12, 24, 48, and 72 h post-dose. The samples were collected into sodium heparin tubes and centrifuged at 3000 rpm for 10 min at 4°C. Plasma was separated into polypropylene tubes and stored at approximately −70°C until analysis.

## 2.3 | Safety and Tolerability

To ensure subject safety throughout the study, clinical laboratory tests, vital signs monitoring, 12-lead ECG, blood glucose tests, and physical examinations were meticulously performed. All adverse events (AEs) and adverse drug reactions were documented to determine potential causal relationships with investigational products and occurrence of serious AEs (SAEs).

Standardized terminology from the Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities (MedDRA, study I: version 25.1, study II: version 26.0) was used for precise and comprehensive reporting. For the safety analysis, SAS Analytics Pro Version 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC, USA) was used.

## 2.4 | Statistical Analysis

All statistical analyses were performed using SAS software (SAS Analytics Pro, Version 9.4; SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC, USA). Continuous variables are presented as the number of subjects, mean, standard deviation (SD), median, minimum, and maximum values, while categorical variables are summarized using frequencies and percentages. The level of statistical significance was set at 5%, and all hypothesis tests were two-sided.

For continuous variables, comparisons between treatment groups were conducted using either a two-sample t-test or the Wilcoxon rank-sum test depending on the normality of the distribution. For categorical variables, the Chi-square test or Fisher's exact test was used as appropriate based on the distribution of the data.

## 3 | Results

## 3.1 | Demographics

For the FDC study, a total of 40 subjects were randomized, 2 of which were dropped; 38 subjects finally completed the study. In the food effect assessment study, a total of 32 subjects were randomized, 2 of which were dropped; 30 subjects finally completed the study and underwent pharmacokinetic assessment. The subjects' disposition for each study is shown in Figure S1.

## 3.2 | Safety and Tolerability

Both the separate combination and FDC of enavogliflozin 0.3 mg/gemigliptin 50 mg were considered safe, with no SAEs or abnormalities detected in vital signs, physical examinations, and 12-lead ECGs. Among the 40 subjects who received at least one dose of the study investigational product, 2 AEs occurred in 2 subjects. One AE (ureterolithiasis) was reported in one subject post-FDC administration but was considered unrelated to the investigational product. In addition, another AE (puncture site pain) occurred in one subject after the administration of the single agent combination, which was also considered unrelated to the investigational product.

Furthermore, in healthy adult subjects, a single dose of enavogliflozin 0.3 mg after a high-fat meal or in the fasted state was confirmed safe, with no SAEs detected. No abnormalities were observed in vital signs, physical examination, or 12-lead ECGs. Among the 32 subjects who received at least one dose of the study investigational product, 3 AEs (nausea, cough, and productive cough) occurred in 2 subjects receiving enavogliflozin after a high-fat meal, with nausea being the only event evaluated as related to the investigational product. All AEs were mild and resolved without further treatment (Table S2).

## 4 | Discussion

In this paper, we describe two trials related to enavogliflozin in healthy Korean adults. We demonstrated pharmacokinetic equivalence and comparable safety between enavogliflozin/ gemigliptin FDC and separate combination, supporting the feasibility of the FDC in clinical use. The observed pharmacokinetic parameters, including $C _ { \mathrm { m a x } }$ and $\mathrm { \Delta A U C _ { \mathrm { l a s t } } , }$ met the standard bioequivalence criteria, indicating that the FDC formulation does not alter the individual absorption or metabolism profiles of enavogliflozin and gemigliptin. In addition, previous studies have reported no clinically significant pharmacokinetic interactions when enavogliflozin is combined with metformin or DPP-4 inhibitors [12, 17, 18]. These findings are particularly relevant to the development of a simplified therapeutic regimen that could improve patient adherence without compromising efficacy. Although DPP-4 inhibitors and SGLT2 inhibitors work via different mechanisms of action, they complement each other in managing glycemic control in patients with T2DM [19–21]. The combination therapy of SGLT2 inhibitors and DPP-4 inhibitors in patients with T2DM has been shown to promote weight loss, reduce fasting and postprandial blood glucose levels, and lower systolic blood pressure compared to DPP-4 inhibitor monotherapy [22, 23]. Unlike earlier trials that focused on metformin-containing regimens, this study offers novel data on a metformin-free combination, which may be particularly useful for patients with contraindications to metformin.

In a previous study investigating the food effect on empagliflozin, a decrease in $\mathrm { { C } _ { \mathrm { { m a x } } } }$ was observed when empagliflozin was administered with food (GMR: 63.22%, 90% CI: 56.74–70.44); however, this finding was not considered clinically meaningful [24]. A pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic model of enavogliflozin identified a strong correlation between AUC and urinary glucose excretion (UGE), which is directly related to clinical outcomes [11]. Dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, and ipragliflozin have also shown a significant association between AUC and UGE [25–27], indicating that food intake does not substantially affect the efficacy of enavogliflozin and that it can be administered regardless of meal status. The observed food effect on enavogliflozin was consistent with findings reported for other SGLT2 inhibitors, such as dapagliflozin, empagliflozin, and ipragliflozin [28, 29]. From a patient adherence perspective, oral drugs that can be administered without regard to meals offer greater convenience and flexibility, potentially improving longterm treatment compliance in individuals with T2DM.

Similar findings have been reported for DPP-4 inhibitors, such as sitagliptin, linagliptin, and vildagliptin, where food intake did not significantly alter AUC, $C _ { \mathrm { m a x } } , \mathrm { o r } T _ { \mathrm { m a x } } .$ In clinical studies, sitagliptin did not show significant food-related effects on pharmacodynamic parameters, including active GLP-1, glucagon, insulin, and glucose levels. Vildagliptin demonstrated consistent bioavailability and glycemic control in both fed and fasting conditions. These observations support the idea that combination therapies, including SGLT2 inhibitors and DPP-4 inhibitors, may not require meal-time considerations, thereby supporting the flexibility of enavogliflozin administration in clinical practice.

Throughout the study, blood glucose levels remained within normal limits up to 4h after dosing, and no hypoglycemic events were observed. Although this study was conducted in healthy subjects whose glycemic profiles may differ from those of patients with diabetes, the results support previous findings that enavogliflozin is unlikely to pose a high risk of hypoglycemia [16].

With respect to the treatment objective of “cardiorenal risk reduction in high-risk patients with T2DM,” the 2023 guideline recommends SGLT2 inhibitors or GLP-1 receptor agonists as the primary option for patients with a history of atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, heart failure, and chronic kidney disease [2, 3]. It emphasizes using these agents independent of metformin, thus confirming the potential use of SGLT2 inhibitors as first-line therapy for patients with T2DM and such comorbidities. Given these considerations, enavogliflozin emerges as a promising treatment option for patients with T2DM, particularly those with kidney and heart disease. In addition, the development of an enavogliflozin/gemigliptin combination is expected to improve treatment adherence and convenience for patients with T2DM, which makes it a safe and effective approach to lower blood sugar in this patient population.

In conclusion, the FDC of enavogliflozin and gemigliptin exhibited no significant and no meaningful pharmacokinetic differences compared with separate combinations and maintained a favorable safety profile. Furthermore, we confirmed that food intake did not affect the systemic exposure of enavogliflozin, supporting its suitability for administration regardless of meal status. These findings contribute to the growing body of evidence supporting the use of combination antidiabetic therapies and are expected to provide valuable insights for optimizing treatment strategies in patients with T2DM.
