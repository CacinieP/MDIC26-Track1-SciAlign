# Morin Improves Cognitive Deficits in an in Vivo Model of Vascular Dementia by Modulating the N-methyl-D-aspartate Receptor Signaling Pathways

Chaochao Gu1  · Amirabas Bostani2  · Hong Wang1

Received: 7 September 2025 / Revised: 11 February 2026 / Accepted: 24 February 2026 / Published online: 16 March 2026   
© The Author(s) 2026

## Abstract

The research on the pathoetiology of vascular dementia (VaD) highlights a notable deficiency in effective therapies within present medical practices. Morin exhibits promising therapeutic benefits due to its strong antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. However, its specific functions and mechanisms in VaD require further elucidation. In this study, VaD animals were established by permanent bilateral common carotid artery occlusion (2VO). Cognitive functions and behavioral analysis were performed in rats. Moreover, the state of oxidative stress, inflammation, and apoptosis was evaluated. Western blotting and ELISA were performed to investigate synaptic plasticity-related proteins, such as SYP, PSD-95, and NMDA receptor proteins (NR1, NR2A, NR2B). The results revealed that morin reduced oxidative stress in the hippocampus by lowering MDA and recombinant reactive oxygen species modulator 1 (Romo-1) levels, while simultaneously enhancing the activities of SOD and GPx. In addition, morin increased the levels of anti-inflammatory cytokines (IL-10 and IL-4), while reducing the levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β and IL-6), and suppressed apoptosis through downregulation of caspase 3 and upregulation of BCL-2. Additionally, morin promoted the expression of PSD95, SYP, and NMDAR proteins in animals with VaD. The obtained data suggest that morin is associated with improved cognitive impairments in VaD rats, which may be mediated by the reduction of apoptosis, oxidative stress, and inflammation in the hippocampus, as well as by restoring the signaling of NMDARs.

Keywords Dementia · Cognitive · Memory · Phytotherapy · Neuron

## Introduction

Dementia is a neurological syndrome characterized by progressive cognitive decline that impairs the ability to perform daily activities and live independently [1]. Vascular dementia (VaD) is recognized as the 2nd most prevalent form of dementia, accounting for approximately 15–20% of all dementia cases [2]. VaD is a complex neurological disorder, occurring when cerebrovascular disease interrupts blood flow to the brain and causes impairment of cognition [3].

Reduced cerebral blood flow, commonly associated with heart disease or hypotension, together with hemorrhagic diseases, causes progressive brain damage and cognitive deficits by multiple pathways [4, 5].

The N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor (NMDAR) complex, consisting of two subunits known as NR1 and NR2, is an essential modulator of synaptic plasticity and formation of memory [6]. In VaD, ischemic damage impairs NMDAR functioning, inducing a cascade of excitotoxicity and neuronal deterioration [7]. The NR1 subunit is an essential component modulating receptor activation, whereas NR2 subunits, including NR2A and NR2B variants, modulate receptor characteristics through various regulatory pathways [8]. Notably, excessive NR2B activation contributes to both excitotoxic processes and inflammatory responses [9]. The pathological disruption of NR1 and NR2 subunit homeostasis is responsible for the progression of synaptic and cognitive impairment reported in patients with VaD [10]. Elucidating these intricate molecular mechanisms represents a crucial step toward understanding the fundamental pathophysiological processes underlying vascular dementia. Furthermore, oxidative stress and inflammation are pivotal factors in the onset and progression of VaD [11]. Oxidative stress indeed activates inflammatory pathways like NF-κB and NLR family pyrin domain containing 3 (NLRP3). Neuroinflammation is driven by the activation of microglia, systemic immune responses, and the presence of oxidative stress. Pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-α and IL-1β exacerbate neuronal damage, endothelial dysfunction, and blood-brain barrier disruption [12].

Currently, there are no established therapeutic approaches capable of effectively stopping or reversing the progression of VaD, with available treatments limited to managing symptoms and addressing associated risk factors [13]. Pharmacological options combine medication (e.g., antihypertensives, statins, and cognitive enhancers like Donepezil and Memantine (20)) and lifestyle (e.g., cognitive rehabilitation and structured exercise programs along with diet changes and smoking cessation [14]) strategies to improve symptoms and prevent progression. Nevertheless, current treatments for VaD remain limited, highlighting the urgent necessity for management strategies. While several flavonoids exhibit neuroprotective potential, Morin (3,5,7,2’,4’-pentahydroxyflavone) has been identified in recent preclinical studies for its superior multi-target activity relevant to cerebrovascular disorders [15–17]. Its efficacy extends beyond general antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects to include specific modulation of apoptotic cascades, promotion of neurogenesis, and crucially, the regulation of glutamatergic signaling—a system profoundly disrupted in hypoperfusion-induced injury [18–20]. The choice of the permanent 2VO model is particularly apt for investigating VaD, as it reliably induces chronic cerebral hypoperfusion, leading to progressive oxidative stress, neuroinflammation, and synaptic degradation, thereby recapitulating key features of human VaD [4, 21]. Although Morin’s benefits have been observed in acute ischemic models [22], a significant gap exists in understanding its capacity to mitigate cognitive deficits and restore synaptic function, specifically via the NMDAR pathway, in a model of chronic vascular insufficiency. Therefore, this study aims to elucidate whether Morin can ameliorate VaD-associated cognitive decline and to delineate its mechanistic actions on the NMDAR signaling cascade, synaptic plasticity proteins, and associated vascular integrity markers in the 2VO rat model.

Limitations in existing therapeutic methods have prompted significant research aimed at discovering new strategies. As previously noted, morin exhibits considerable therapeutic potential for neurological disorders, attributed to its properties that neutralize neuroinflammation, alleviate oxidative stress, prevent apoptosis, promote neurogenesis, and modulate relevant signaling pathways. Consequently, this study aimed to develop a rat model of VaD and assess the impact of morin on neurobehavioral outcomes, markers of oxidative stress, neuroinflammatory responses, apoptotic processes, and mechanisms of synaptic plasticity.

## Materials and Methods

## Animal Research Subjects

The sample size of n = 8 per group was determined based on extensive previous research using the 2VO model and similar behavioral and biochemical endpoints, which has consistently demonstrated robust effect sizes with this group size [21, 23]. A post hoc power analysis was conducted using G\*Power 3.1 software, focusing on the primary behavioral endpoints of Morris water maze escape latency and Novel Object Recognition discrimination index. This analysis, based on the observed effect sizes (F(3, 28) = 22.15, ηp² = 0.70 for escape latency and F(3, 28) = 18.73, ηp² = 0.67 for discrimination index, α = 0.05), confirmed that the achieved statistical power for these key comparisons exceeded 90%, well above the conventional 80% threshold.

Subjects consisted of 32 male Sprague-Dawley rats, all approximately eight weeks old and weighing 200–250 g, housed in a controlled environment (22 ± 3 °C, 50 ± 5% relative humidity) with a 12-hour light-dark cycle. The animals had free access to food and water for the duration of the study. Before initiating experimental procedures, the rats underwent a 7-day acclimatization period to adapt to the housing conditions.

## Preparation of Animal Models

Under chloral hydrate anesthesia (350 mg/kg, administered intraperitoneally), rats were positioned supine on a heating pad to ensure a core body temperature of 37 °C. Permanent bilateral common carotid artery occlusion (two-vessel occlusion, 2VO) was then conducted using a previously established method [21]. After removing cervical fur and sterilizing the designated surgical field, a midline ventral neck incision approximately 2–3 centimeters in length was created. The salivary glands were gently retracted laterally to facilitate exposure. Through careful blunt dissection, the sternocleidomastoid and omohyoid muscles were separated, allowing visualization of the common carotid arteries (CCAs). Each CCA was meticulously isolated from the vagus nerve and surrounding connective tissue using fine forceps. Permanent occlusion was achieved by ligating both CCAs with sterile 4 − 0 silk sutures, ensuring the absence of pulsatile blood flow distal to the ligation site. Following the procedure, the incision was carefully sutured and thoroughly disinfected. Throughout the surgery, the rats’ respiratory and heart rates were monitored. Post-operation, animals were kept individually until visible signs of incision healing were observed. In the sham-operated groups (Sham Operation Group and Morin Sham Group), all procedural steps were performed identically, except for the ligation of the carotid arteries.

## Study Design

In a randomized experimental design, rats were allocated into four distinct groups:

1. Sham Operation Group (Sham, n = 8): Rats underwent a surgical procedure without the induction of 2VO and were administered normal saline as a control vehicle.

2. Morin Sham Group (Morin+Sham, n = 8): Received morin supplementation following the sham surgical procedure.

3. 2VO Group (n = 8): Rats were subjected to 2VO surgery and were given normal saline.

4. Morin-Treated 2VO Group (Morin+2VO, n = 8): Rats underwent 2VO surgery and were treated with Morin.

Starting two days post-surgery, morin hydrate (Sigma-Aldrich, purity ≥ 95%) was dissolved in normal saline. The selected dose of 100 mg/kg, administered orally via gavage every other day for 28 days, was based on its established efficacy in mitigating neuroinflammation, oxidative stress, and cognitive deficits in prior rodent models of cerebral ischemia and neurodegeneration [24, 25]. The compound was fully dissolved by brief sonication and vortexing as necessary. To maintain consistency, the Sham and 2VO groups received an equivalent volume of the vehicle (normal saline) on the same schedule. Behavioral assessments were conducted 30 days after surgery in the following sequence, listed below. Each rat was allowed adequate rest between tests to prevent stress and fatigue. Neurological impairments were analyzed 30 days after the 2VO procedure by a blinded evaluator. On day 37 post-occlusion, the rats were anesthetized, and hippocampal tissue samples were then carefully extracted, rinsed with sterile phosphate-buffered saline, and stored at -80 °C for subsequent analysis.

## Bederson Test

Beyond cognitive-specific tests, the present study employed the Bederson score and pole climbing test to assess concomitant global neurological and motor deficits, which are hallmark features of the 2VO model and relevant to the clinical presentation of VaD. The neurological condition of the rats was evaluated using a modified Bederson Neurological Score [26, 27], a standardized scale for assessing global neurological function in rodent models of cerebral ischemia. The scoring system was as follows:

0: No observable neurological deficit ;

1: Failure to fully extend the contralateral forepaw;

2: Circling to the contralateral side;

3: Falling to the contralateral side;

4: Depressed level of consciousness or no spontaneous motor activity.

All neurological scoring was performed by a researcher who was blinded to the surgical and treatment status of the animals.

## Pole Climbing Assessment

For measuring the physical strength, coordination, and motor skills of rats, the pole climbing assessment was conducted. A vertically smooth-surfaced pole with a diameter of at least 2 cm and 50 cm was employed for testing. Rats were given a short period of acclimatization in the test room before presenting them for the test in order to eliminate or minimize stress and ensure optimal performance. During the test, each rat was positioned at the base of the pole and encouraged to climb to the top. The duration of the ascent was meticulously recorded. A trial was deemed failed if the rat fell from the pole, failed to use both forelimbs and hindlimbs effectively, or did not reach the summit within the time frame. The primary metric for evaluating performance was the time taken to complete the climb (in seconds), and this continuous variable was used for all statistical analyses. The scoring system in Table 1 is provided for descriptive context to categorize performance levels. Rats that were unable to climb or did not meet the specified criteria received a score of 0. This scoring system provided a standardized method for quantifying and comparing the physical capabilities of the subjects. The time to ascend was recorded by a blinded observer.

## Morris Water Maze Test

The Morris Water Maze (MWM) test protocol was used to study rat cognitive function following 28 days of continuous treatment. There were two distinct methodological phases encompassed by the behavioral testing: a spatial probe test and hidden platform training. These procedures were utilized to evaluate the learning and memory capabilities of the rats. The assessment consisted of two phases: a 5-day acquisition (learning) phase followed by a single probe (memory) trial on day 6. During the acquisition phase, animals underwent four trials per day with a 60-minute inter-trial interval. Each trial allowed a maximum of 90 s to locate a submerged platform; if unsuccessful, the rat was gently guided to it and remained there for 15 s. Starting positions were varied pseudo-randomly between four cardinal points. On day 6, the platform was removed for a single 60-second probe trial to assess spatial memory retention. All trials were recorded and analyzed using the (EthoVision XT) automated tracking system. The experimenter performing the analysis was blinded to the group allocations. Improved learning and memory were indicated by shorter escape latencies during training, a higher number of crossings over the platform’s former location, and increased time spent in the target quadrant during the probe test [28].

## Novel Object Recognition Test

The Novel Object Recognition (NOR) test was employed to test cognitive and memory abilities in rats by monitoring them when they are interacting with familiar objects and novel objects within a previously familiar environment. The test was conducted over three phases in a single day. First, a 10-minute habituation phase in the empty arena. After a 60-minute interval, a 5-minute familiarization phase was conducted where the rat explored two identical objects (Object A1 and A2). Following a 2-hour retention interval, a 5-minute test phase began, during which one familiar object was replaced with a novel object (Object B). The arena and objects were thoroughly cleaned with 70% ethanol between trials to remove olfactory cues. Exploration (defined as the rat’s snout oriented toward and within < 2 cm of the object) was manually scored from video recordings by an observer blinded to treatment groups. The discrimination index (DI) was calculated as follows [29, 30]:

$$

$$

A positive DI indicates intact memory, as the rat shows a preference for the novel object.

## General Protocol for Biochemical Assays

Hippocampal tissues were homogenized in ice-cold phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) or appropriate lysis buffer containing protease and phosphatase inhibitors. Homogenates were centrifuged at 12,000 × g for 15 min at 4 °C, and the supernatants were collected. The total protein concentration of each supernatant was determined using a bicinchoninic acid (BCA) assay kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, #23225), with bovine serum albumin as the standard. For all subsequent enzyme-linked immunosorbent assays (ELISAs), supernatants were diluted to a standardized concentration of 1.0 mg/mL total protein with the appropriate assay buffer to ensure consistent loading. All assays were performed in strict accordance with the manufacturer’s protocols. Absorbance was measured using a microplate reader. Analytic concentrations were determined by interpolating absorbance values against a standard curve generated from serially diluted standards provided with each kit, using a four-parameter logistic (4-PL) regression model unless otherwise specified.

## The Measurement of Apoptosis Markers

The concentrations of BCL-2 (catalog number: ER0762, FineTest, Hubei, China) and Caspase 3 (CASP-3, catalog number: ER0143, FineTest, Hubei, China) proteins were assessed utilizing the ELISA technique to evaluate the status of apoptosis. To achieve this, commercially available ELISA kits were acquired, and the assays were conducted following the instructions provided by the manufacturer.

## Quantification of NMDAR Downstream Signaling Proteins

The phosphorylation status of another key NMDAR downstream effector, CREB at Ser133 (p-CREB), was quantified in hippocampal homogenates using a commercial ELISA kit (Phospho-CREB [Ser133] ELISA Kit, Boster Bio, #EKC2382). Supernatants were diluted to 1 mg/mL total protein (determined by BCA assay). Assays were performed per manufacturer protocols. Absorbance was read at 450 nm. Moreover, phosphorylation levels of calcium/ calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II isoforms CAMK2A and CAMK2D at Thr286 (p-CAMK2A, p-CAMK2D) were quantified using the Colorimetric Cell-Based ELISA Kit (CAMK2A/CAMK2D (Phospho-Thr286), Boster Bio, #EKC2366). Hippocampal tissue homogenates were diluted to 1 mg/mL total protein in phosphate-buffered saline. Samples were added to antibody-precoated wells and incubated for 90 min at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . After washing, detection antibodies specific for phosphorylated Thr286 were added (60 min, $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ , followed by HRP-conjugated secondary antibody (30 min, 37 °C). Color development used TMB substrate (20 min, dark), stopped with sulfuric acid. Absorbance was read at 450 nm. Concentrations were calculated from standard curves using a 4-parameter logistic regression and normalized to total protein.

## Assessment of Vascular Integrity Markers

Hippocampal levels of tight junction proteins (Occludin, Claudin-5) and vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) were quantified using ELISA kits, including Rat Occludin ELISA Kit (MyBioSource #MBS725124), rat Claudin-5 ELISA Kit (MyBioSource #MBS3809259), and rat VEGF ELISA Kit (MyBioSource #MBS8421572). Homogenates were diluted to 1 mg/mL total protein. Assays followed manufacturer protocols, and concentrations were calculated from standard curves using 4-parameter logistic regression.

## Assessment of Oxidative Stress and Inflammatory Cytokines

Superoxide dismutase (SOD) and glutathione peroxidase (GPx) enzymatic activity, as well as the concentrations of reactive oxygen species modulator 1 (Romo-1) and malondialdehyde (MDA), were measured using MyBioSource ELISA kits following the standard procedure mentioned in the manufacturers’ manuals for ensuring consistency and accuracy during the experimental protocol. In addition, MyBioSource ELISA kits were used to measure the levels of Pro-inflammatory cytokines, specifically IL-1β, IL-6,

IL-10, and IL-4. All procedures were performed according to the manufacturer’s protocols.

## Western Blot

Western blot analysis was conducted to assess the expression levels of main synaptic proteins, including postsynaptic density protein 95 (PSD-95) and synaptophysin (SYP) in rat hippocampal tissue. β-actin was employed as the loading control for normalization. Primary antibodies, all diluted at 1:1000, were sourced from Santa Cruz Biotechnology and included the following: PSD-95 (sc-32290, Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Inc.), SYP (sc-17750, Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Inc.), and β-actin (sc-517582, Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Inc.).

Hippocampal tissues were homogenized in RIPA buffer containing protease and phosphatase inhibitors (Sigma-Aldrich, CAT number PPC1010). Following centrifugation at $1 2 , 0 0 0 \times \mathbf { g }$ for 15 min at 4 °C, supernatants were collected for further analysis. BCA protein assay kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, catalog number 23225) was used to measure protein concentrations. Equal amounts of protein (30 µg per sample) were loaded onto 10% SDS-PAGE gels and separated via electrophoresis, followed by transfer to PVDF membranes using a wet transfer apparatus (Bio-Rad). Membrane blocking was achieved with 5% non-fat dry milk in Tris-buffered saline containing 0.1% Tween-20 (TBST) for 60 min at room temperature. Following blocking, the membranes were incubated with primary antibodies diluted in TBST with 5% bovine serum albumin (BSA) overnight at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ After thorough washing with TBST, the membranes were incubated with horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated secondary antibodies (donkey anti-goat IgG, sc-2020, Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Inc., 1:5000 dilution) at room temperature for 1 h. Then three additional TBST washes, protein bands were detected using an enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL) detection system (Bio-Rad). Band intensities were quantified using ImageJ software (NIH), and protein expression levels were normalized to β-actin as the loading control. All analyses were conducted in triplicate.

## Quantification of Hippocampal Glutamate

Glutamate concentration in hippocampal homogenates was measured using the Glutamate ELISA Kit (Abcam, #ab83389). Samples were deproteinized with 10% trichloroacetic acid, centrifuged at 10,000 × g for 10 min, and neutralized with 1 M NaOH. Supernatants were diluted 1:20 in assay buffer. 50 µL of sample/standard was added to antibody-coated wells, incubated with glutamate oxidase (30 min, $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ , followed by chromogen (20 min, dark).

Absorbance was read at 450 nm. Concentrations were calculated from a glutamate standard curve (0–100 µg/mL).

## Measurement of NMDAR Subunit Proteins

The protein levels of NMDAR subunits (NR1, NR2A, and NR2B) in homogenized hippocampal tissue were quantified utilizing commercial ELISA kits based on the manufacturers’ protocols. Specifically, the following kits were employed: Rat NR1 ELISA Kit (Catalog #MBS3809102, MyBioSource, USA), Rat NR2A ELISA Kit (Catalog #MBS3809533, MyBioSource, USA), and Rat NR2B ELISA Kit (Catalog # MBS3808701, MyBioSource, USA). According to the manufacturer’s datasheets, these kits have been validated for specificity in rat brain tissue homogenates, demonstrating minimal cross-reactivity with other isoforms and related proteins. Hippocampal tissue supernatants were diluted to a concentration of 1 mg/mL total protein (determined by BCA assay) before analysis. All incubations, washing steps, and absorbance measurements at 450 nm were performed strictly in accordance with the technical manuals provided with each kit. Samples were run in duplicate, and protein concentrations were calculated from standard curves using a 4-parameter logistic regression model.

## Statistical Analysis

The statistical analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism 9.5 and SPSS software. The normality of the data was evaluated. The statistical analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism 9.5 (and/or SPSS software). The assumption of normality was assessed for each dataset using the Shapiro-Wilk test, and the homogeneity of variances was verified using Levene’s test. Data that passed both normality and homoscedasticity assumptions (p > 0.05) were analyzed using a one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey’s post hoc test for multiple comparisons. Datasets that violated the assumption of normality or homoscedasticity (p < 0.05 in either test) were analyzed using the non-parametric Kruskal-Wallis test, followed by Dunn’s post hoc test with a Bonferroni correction. For significant ANOVA results, effect sizes are reported as partial eta-squared (ηp²). No data points were excluded from the analyses. All statistical tests were performed on the full dataset (n = 8 per group). A p-value of less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## Results

## Morin Improved Neurobehavioral Impairments in Rats with VaD

Figure 1a, b depicts the neurobehavioral scores for each group of rats. The results indicated that the 2VO group exhibited a significant increase across both the Bederson and pole climbing test scores compared to the Sham group (p-value < 0.001 for both tests). In contrast, the Morin+2VO group demonstrated a significant decrease in these scores when compared to the 2VO group (for each p < 0.001). Additionally, there were no significant differences in Bederson and Pole Climbing Test scores between the Morin+Sham group and the Sham group (p-value > 0.05 for both). Overall, these results suggest that morin may alleviate the neurobehavioral impairments observed in rats with VaD.

## The Mitigation of Learning and Memory Impairments in VaD Animals by Morin

The results from both the training and probe phases of the study are illustrated in Fig. 1c–f. During the training phase, all animals underwent a five-day training period, during which a consistent reduction in escape latency was noted (Fig. 1c). From day 2 to day 5, the 2VO group exhibited a significant increase in escape latency compared to the Sham group (p-value < 0.001). In contrast, the Morin+2VO group demonstrated a notable decline in escape latency when compared to the 2VO group (P < 0.001). Escape latency did not differ significantly between the Morin+Sham and Sham groups (p > 0.05), demonstrating that morin effectively reversed the spatial learning deficits seen in the 2VO group.

The spatial probe phase results are shown in Figs. 1d–f. The 2VO group indicated a notable reduction in both the time spent in the target quadrant and the number of platform crossings, in comparison with the Sham group (p-value < 0.001). Conversely, the Morin+2VO group exhibited a significant improvement in these measures relative to the 2VO group (p-value < 0.001). No significant differences were observed between the Morin+Sham group and the Sham group in these parameters (p-value > 0.05). Furthermore, there were no notable differences in average swimming speed across all experimental groups (p-value > 0.05). Therefore, the findings propose that morin significantly improves learning and memory functions in VaD-affected rats.

## Morin Improved Short-Term Memory in VaD Rats

To assess the efficacy of morin in improving short-term memory, the NOR test was conducted, as illustrated in Fig. 1g–h, which presents both the discrimination index and total exploration time. ## Morin Mitigated Apoptosis in the Hippocampus of VaD Animals

RT-qPCR and ELISA methods were implemented to assess the impact of morin on apoptosis within the hippocampus of rats suffering from VaD. Figure 2 displays the expression levels of genes encoding BCL-2 and CASP-3, as well as the corresponding protein levels. The results indicated that, in comparison to the Sham group, the 2VO group exhibited downregulation of the antiapoptotic protein BCL-2, while there was a significant upregulation of the apoptotic protein CASP-3 (p-value < 0.001). No significant differences were detected between the Morin+Sham and Sham groups regarding the expression of BCL-2 and CASP-3 markers (p-value > 0.05). These findings could suggest that morin may play a protective antiapoptotic role in the hippocampus of rats with VaD (Figs. 3 and 4).

## Discussion

Dementia, marked by a gradual deterioration of cognitive functions, impacts millions worldwide, particularly in the elderly population [31]. It is estimated that 15–20% of cases with dementia are suffering from VaD, and this condition affects 1–2% of elderly people over 65, with its prevalence doubling every decade after this age [32]. It is widely suggested that the interplay between cerebrovascular neuropathology and neurodegeneration is a critical parameter in the progression of VaD [33]. Thromboembolism, small vessel disease, and hypoperfusion result in decreased cerebral blood flow that triggers hypoxia, oxidative stress, and inflammation [34]. These alterations contribute to the dysfunction of the neurovascular unit, induce mitochondrial damage, and BBB, ultimately promoting the onset of VaD. Despite considerable advancements in understanding the pathophysiology of VaD, definitive therapeutic interventions remain elusive. This research aimed to examine the potential ameliorating effects of morin within an animal model of disease and to elucidate the underlying mechanisms. Morin was administered 48 h after the 2VO procedure, simulating clinical scenarios where therapeutic interventions are initiated following ischemic insult. This experimental design permits an evaluation of therapeutic efficacy in the context of established pathology, consistent with delayed treatment strategies employed in VaD. The outcomes provide significant insights into the capacity of morin to mitigate cognitive deficits in VaD and suggest potential modes of action. Specifically, the findings revealed that morin treatment improved neurobehavioral deficits in rats with VaD, enhanced their capacity for learning and memory, and resulted in a higher discrimination index during the NOR test.

It is determined that oxidative stress results in endothelial dysfunction, disruption of the BBB, mitochondrial impairment, and neuronal injury [35]. Ischemic events in VaD increase ROS, leading to neuronal apoptosis and cognitive decline [36]. The interplay between oxidative stress and inflammation creates a vicious cycle, accelerating disease progression. Therapeutic strategies, including antioxidants and anti-inflammatory agents, show potential but require further research [11]. Indeed, cognitive impairments in VaD are often linked to oxidative stress and inflammatory responses [37, 38]. Prior research has indicated that morin can suppress neuroinflammatory responses, diminish oxidative stress, and prevent cell apoptosis in neural-damaged animals [24, 39]. In line with these earlier findings, the results of the current study indicated that morin was capable of upregulating the level of BCL-2, an apoptosis inhibitor, whereas it downregulated CASP-3, a serine protease active in the apoptotic cascade. In addition, this study revealed that morin reduced the inflammatory response by lowering the pro-inflammatory cytokine levels while elevating the anti-inflammatory cytokine levels in the hippocampus. Furthermore, the study demonstrated that morin treatment favorably mitigated oxidative imbalance by decreasing MDA and Romo-1 levels, while increasing SOD and GPx levels in the hippocampus of animals with VaD. Additionally, morin may regulate the synaptic plasticity. Hence, the possible involved pathways were investigated.

Modulation of the NMDAR pathway is a pivotal process in synaptic plasticity and cognitive functions [40]. A recent report provided strong evidence for the association of NMDAR with VaD, which was depicted by downregulation of NMDAR in the hippocampus in VaD patients [40]. The data in this investigation indicates that morin administration might enhance synaptic plasticity. This is evidenced by an increase in the expression of SYP and PSD-95 within the hippocampus, accompanied by a restoration of cognitive capabilities. Furthermore, ELISA quantification results suggest that morin could elevate the protein concentrations of NMDAR subunits, hinting at a potential role in mediating synaptic plasticity through NMDAR proteins. The observed upregulation of NMDAR subunits and their associated downstream effectors implies that the cognitive advantages conferred by morin may be attributable to the stabilization of glutamatergic signaling cascades. The multifaceted nature of morin supports its investigation as a potential candidate for VaD management strategies. It was previously demonstrated that spatial memory and learning improvement in VaD rats could be achieved by the upregulation of proteins involved in synaptic plasticity, i.e., NMDAR subunits [41– 43]. Concordantly, hippocampal examination uncovered downregulation of NR1, NR2A, and NR2B (NMDAR subunits) and SYP and PSD95 (synaptic plasticity-associated proteins) in VaD cases, indicating the involvement of the NMDAR signaling in cognitive impairments post-morin treatment in VaD.

The previous research aimed at oxidative imbalance in VaD models has largely centered on pharmacotherapeutic and traditional Chinese medicine interventions [41–43]. The research on acupuncture has revealed that electroacupuncture can enhance the activity of antioxidant enzymes and learning and cognitive performance in ischemic rat models [41–43]. Similarly, pharmacologic interventions with anti-inflammatory and antioxidant activities (e.g., N-Palmitoylethanolamine-oxazoline and Timosaponin B-II) have embodied antioxidant and anti-inflammatory actions, leading to enhanced cognitive performance in models under study [23, 44]. Moreover, other phytochemicals such as quercetin [45] and resveratrol [46] have been effective in decreasing oxidative stress and enhancing cognitive performance in VaD rats. Unlike prior acupuncture and pharmacotherapeutic strategies, this study explored the therapeutic efficacy of morin, recognized for its capacity to diminish apoptosis, oxidative stress, and inflammatory responses in the hippocampus [25, 47]. The intervention with morin in this model highlights its potential for further exploration as a therapeutic avenue for VaD. Additionally, this study uncovered a remarkable connection between the neuroprotective effects of morin and the NMDAR signaling, providing insights into its mechanism of action and potential future treatment approaches.

Critically, the recovery of p-CAMK2A, p-CAMK2D, and p-CREB provides direct evidence that morin restores NMDAR functional output. As CaMKII autophosphorylation is indispensable for synaptic tagging, gene transcription, and long-term memory [48], these findings mechanistically bridge morin-induced NMDAR upregulation to cognitive improvement in VaD, and provide mechanistic validation beyond subunit quantification alone. It should be noted that current data reveal that morin attenuates pathological glutamate accumulation in the hippocampus, directly linking NMDAR upregulation and CaMKII activation to functional synaptic recovery [48, 49]. This complements present findings of increased SYP/PSD-95 as structural markers by demonstrating improved neurotransmitter homeostasis. Importantly, the recovery of p-CAMK2A, p-CAMK2D, and p-CREB provides direct evidence that morin restores NMDAR functional output [50, 51]. It should be noted that reduced glutamate levels, by mitigating NMDAR overactivation and calcium excitotoxicity, directly facilitate physiological CaMKII phosphorylation at Thr286 [52]. This creates a permissive environment for synaptic plasticity, where normalized glutamate homeostasis enables CaMKII to orchestrate downstream memory consolidation processes [53]. This complements our findings of increased SYP/ PSD-95 as structural markers by demonstrating functional restoration of the glutamate-CaMKII synaptic axis. Additionally, vascular marker analyses reveal that morin significantly preserves BBB integrity and modulates angiogenesis in VaD. The upregulation of Occludin and Claudin-5, critical tight junction proteins, confirms morin’s protective effect against BBB disruption, a hallmark of VaD pathogenesis [54]. Concurrently, morin reduced pathological VEGF overexpression, which is known to exacerbate BBB permeability and neuroinflammation in chronic hypoperfusion [55]. The observed upregulation of Occludin and Claudin-5, critical components of tight junctions, suggests that morin may help preserve the molecular underpinnings of BBB integrity. Concurrently, the reduction in pathological VEGF overexpression points to a potential normalization of aberrant angiogenic signaling. These improvements in vascular biomarkers likely contribute to morin’s neuroprotective effects, for instance, by potentially enhancing nutrient delivery and mitigating neuroinflammation. It is important to note that these are biochemical surrogates; future studies using direct imaging techniques (e.g., Evans Blue extravasation, immunohistochemistry) are necessary to conclusively demonstrate functional protection of the BBB and changes in cerebral blood flow.

This investigation is subject to several inherent limitations. Primarily, the staggered execution of behavioral assessments may have inadvertently induced stress between successive testing sessions. Moreover, the employed methodologies possess intrinsic restrictions, including the reliance on enzyme-linked immunosorbent assays (ELISAs) for quantifying protein levels such as NMDAR subunits and apoptosis markers, which would be strengthened by complementary validation techniques like Western blotting or cleaved caspase-3 assays. The estimation of total hippocampal glutamate also fails to delineate its precise cellular origins. A significant omission in this study is the absence of histological analyses, such as neuronal counting or Nissl staining, which would offer structural correlations to the observed functional and molecular enhancements. Additionally, the interpretations concerning synaptic plasticity and NMDAR function are derived from correlational evidence; definitive mechanistic understanding necessitates direct electrophysiological data, for instance, long-term potentiation (LTP) measurements, and an examination of receptor localization. Lastly, although the study’s model and dosage regimen are well-founded, the hierarchical interplay between morin’s antioxidant/anti-inflammatory properties and NMDAR modulation, alongside its pharmacokinetic profile and potential for human therapeutic application, warrants further in-depth exploration. These identified constraints delineate promising avenues for subsequent research to advance this therapeutic paradigm.

## Conclusion

This thorough examination of the results supports the theoretical assertion that morin can alleviate cognitive decline in rats suffering from VaD. Notably, the study revealed that morin exerts its effects, at least in part, by modulating key pathways involved in apoptosis, oxidative stress, neuroinflammation, and NMDAR signaling pathway, which collectively improve the cognitive impairments typically associated with VaD. Consequently, this preclinical study identifies morin as a promising candidate and provides a rationale for its further investigation in the development of future therapeutic approaches for VaD. Notably, these promising findings were obtained in a preclinical rodent model, and further pharmacokinetic and clinical studies are essential to validate the safety, efficacy, and appropriate dosing of morin in humans.
