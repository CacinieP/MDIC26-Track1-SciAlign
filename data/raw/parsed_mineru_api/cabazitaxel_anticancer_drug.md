# Treatment Strategies in Metastatic Castration‑Resistant Prostate Cancer: A Narrative Review of Considerations in the Post‑ARTA, Post‑Docetaxel Setting

Kenrick Ng  · Birth‑Lynn Tyers · Aruni Ghose · Abbas Kassamali · Reem El Debri · Jonathan Shamash

Received: September 11, 2025 / Accepted: December 19, 2025 / Published online: January 19, 2026

## ABSTRACT

The management of advanced prostate cancer has evolved significantly with the advent of androgen receptor-targeted agents (ARTAs) and docetaxel, which now form the cornerstone of frst-line systemic therapy in advanced disease. However, as increasing numbers of patients progress after exposure to both an ARTA and docetaxel, optimal treatment sequencing in the post-ARTA, post-docetaxel metastatic cas‑ tration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) set‑ ting remains an area of clinical uncertainty, with limited comparative data to guide deci‑ sions. This narrative review synthesises the cur‑ rent evidence surrounding treatment strategies for mCRPC in this later-line setting. Available therapeutic options include radioligand ther‑ apy (e.g. lutetium-177–PSMA-617), cabazitaxel, PARP inhibitors (particularly for patients with DNA repair gene alterations), second-line ARTAs, further chemotherapy and immunotherapeutic approaches. However, direct head-to-head tri‑ als comparing these modalities are sparse, and

much of the available data comes from retro‑ spective or subgroup analyses, necessitating a nuanced interpretation of outcomes and limi‑ tations. Beyond effcacy, this article emphasises the critical role of practical and individualised considerations in therapy selection. These include toxicity profles, patient comorbidities and preferences, biomarker-driven stratifica‑ tion (e.g. BRCA status, PSMA expression, sites of metastases), and health system factors such as drug availability and reimbursement policies, which may signifcantly infuence access to opti‑ mal care. Finally, the review provides an over‑ view of promising emerging therapies poised to expand the treatment landscape for mCRPC. These include bispecifc T cell engagers, andro‑ gen receptor degraders, and antibody–drug con‑ jugates, which are currently under investigation in clinical trials and may soon offer new avenues for treatment beyond traditional mechanisms.

Keywords: Prostate cancer; Metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer; Androgen receptor targeted agents; Radioligand therapy; Chemotherapy

## Key Summary Points

Optimal treatment sequencing in the post-ARTA, post-docetaxel metastatic castrationresistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) setting remains an area of clinical uncertainty, with limited comparative data to guide decisions. The most common standard of care arm in trials, an ARTA switch, is associated with poor response rates and a progression-free survival ranging between 3 and 5 months. Following docetaxel and ARTA, radioligand therapy offers better response rates and tolerability than cabazitaxel, but there is no overall survival beneft in using radioligand treatment frst. In patients with BRCA mutation, mono‑ therapy with a PARP inhibitor post ARTA is associated with a good but often not a dura‑ ble response. Docetaxel rechallenge is a reasonable option in selected situations, with the option of a 2-weekly regimen for frailer patients. Platinum-based chemotherapy is standard of care for aggressive variant prostate cancer.

## INTRODUCTION

Over the past two decades, the treatment landscape for metastatic prostate cancer has evolved signifcantly. The integration of doc‑ etaxel and androgen receptor-targeted agents (ARTAs)—abiraterone, enzalutamide, apaluta‑ mide, and darolutamide—has improved both survival and quality of life. Initially reserved for castration-resistant disease, these agents now form the backbone of frst-line therapy in combination with androgen deprivation ther‑ apy (ADT) [1]. Current consensus supports the use of both docetaxel and an ARTA as the frst two lines of treatment when clinically appro‑ priate—either as upfront triplet therapy [2, 3] in the metastatic castration-sensitive setting or as ARTA duplet therapy upfront [4–7], followed by docetaxel therapy on progression.

Despite these advances, many patients ulti‑ mately progress after receiving both docetaxel and an ARTA, creating an urgent need for effec‑ tive post-resistance strategies. Beyond these lines, treatment selection is often guided more by biomarker status, imaging access, comor‑ bidities, and reimbursement policies than by head-to-head comparative data.

The CARD study, investigating the relative effec‑ tiveness of cabazitaxel against a second ARTA, is one of the very few studies providing valuable but limited evidence for treatment sequencing in this setting[8]. Since then, phase III trials such as VISION (Lu-177 PSMA-617) [9] and PROfound (olaparib) [10] have shown beneft in select patient subsets. Nonetheless, the lack of randomised comparisons with these novel therapies presents an evidence gap and a dilemma in treatment sequencing. Real-world evidence from a US cohort of nearly 15,000 men with metastatic castrationresistant prostate cancer illustrates these gaps: 22% received no life-prolonging therapy, and only 42% received two or more lines [11]. The most com‑ mon sequence—switching between ARTAs (33%)— stands in contrast to the consensus opinion of the Advanced Prostate Cancer Consensus Conference (APCCC) [12], refecting the inconsistencies in treatment strategies in later lines of treatment of metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer.

This review critically evaluates post-doc‑ etaxel, post-ARTA treatment options and the clinical evidence guiding their use. This article is based on previously conducted studies and does not contain any new studies with human participants or animals performed by any of the authors.

## CABAZITAXEL VS. LUTETIUM‑177: FRONTLINE CONTENDERS

## Foundational Evidence: TROPIC and VISION Trials

Two pivotal phase III clinical trials have estab‑ lished cabazitaxel and lutetium-177 (Lu-177  PSMA-617) therapy as firm treatment options post progression on one ARTA and doc‑ etaxel chemotherapy. The TROPIC trial laid the foundation for the use of cabazitaxel in men with metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) progressing after docetaxel, and consolidated by the CARD trial in 2019 [8]. Cabazitaxel signifcantly improved median overall survival (OS) compared to mitoxantrone (15.1 vs. 12.7 months; HR 0.70), establishing it as the standard second-line chemotherapy. How‑ ever, the treatment was associated with substan‑ tial hematologic toxicity, particularly neutrope‑ nia and febrile neutropenia, necessitating careful patient selection [13].

A decade later, the VISION trial demonstrated the effcacy of Lu-177 PSMA-617, a radioligand therapy targeting PSMA-expressing tumours, in patients with mCRPC who had progressed after ARTA and taxane chemotherapy. Among PSMA-PET-positive patients, which constitutes 70–80% patients in the mCRPC setting, Lu-177 PSMA-617 signifcantly improved radiographic progres‑ sion-free survival (PFS; 8.7 vs. 3.4 months) and OS (15.3 vs. 11.3 months; HR 0.62; p < 0.001) compared to standard care alone [9]. These fnd‑ ings led to widespread regulatory approval, but the comparator arm did not include cabazitaxel, leaving unanswered questions about the relative effectiveness between the two agents.

## TheraP Trial: Direct Comparison in a Selected Population

The TheraP trial (ANZUP1603) was the frst pro‑ spective, randomised phase II study to directly compare Lu-177 PSMA-617 with cabazitaxel in men with mCRPC previously treated with docetaxel and an ARTA. Patients were selected based on PSMA-PET positivity and FDG-PET exclusion criteria, with 28–30% of screened patients excluded due to low or heteroge‑ nous PSMA expression or discordant lesions. Lu-177 PSMA-617 demonstrated higher pros‑ tate specifc antigen (PSA) response rates (66% vs. 37%) and a lower rate of grade ≥ 3 adverse events (33% vs. 53%) [14]. Lu-177 PSMA-617 also demonstrated superiority in response even in patients with visceral metastases, although the outcomes for patients with visceral metastases were poorer regardless of treatment arm. However, an updated analysis in 2024 did not demonstrate any difference in OS, sug‑ gesting a significant crossover between the two arms of the study [15]. Nevertheless, given fewer grade 3–4 adverse events and improved patient-reported outcomes with Lu-PSMA-617, it is reasonable to conclude that quality of life during treatment was better with Lu-PSMA-617 than cabazitaxel.

## FRAMCAP Registry: Broader Observational Evidence

Data from the FRAMCAP registry—an observa‑ tional cohort of 373 patients with mCRPC who have received docetaxel—provides a retrospec‑ tive comparison between the two treatment strategies. Patients treated with Lu-177 PSMA-617 had a numerically longer OS compared to those receiving cabazitaxel though confound‑ ing factors and potential selection bias limit direct comparison [16]. Notably, many patients in the Lu-177  PSMA-617 group were older (median 72 years vs. 66 years) and had poorer performance status (PS 2 or more 12% vs. 5%), suggesting that real-world effectiveness may approach or exceed that seen in trials, even in less ft populations.

A further multicentre retrospective study (LuCaS) involving 68 patients who received sequential treatment with Lu-177 PSMA-617 and cabazitaxel demonstrated that there was no difference in response rates to either agent [17], nor OS in either cohort regardless of which agent was delivered frst. However, this does not account for the attrition of patients on either arm of treatment who did not receive subsequent life-prolonging therapy.

## Conclusion: Effcacy vs. Accessibility and Patient Selection

Collectively, the evidence from TheraP, FRAMPCAP, and LuCaS suggests that both Lu-177 PSMA-617 and cabazitaxel are effec‑ tive treatment options in the post-docetaxel, post-ARTA setting. Lu-177  PSMA-617 may offer superior PSA response and disease con‑ trol, with a more favourable toxicity profle in PSMA-PET-selected populations. However, consistent OS advantages have not been dem‑ onstrated in either randomised or retrospective comparisons.

Importantly, the broader use of Lu-177 is constrained by practical limitations—namely the requirement for PSMA-PET imaging, radio‑ ligand infrastructure, and high treatment costs, which limit availability in many healthcare set‑ tings (Table 1). In contrast, cabazitaxel is more widely accessible, supported by long-standing phase  III data, and remains an appropriate standard for patients who are ineligible for Lu-177 or in jurisdictions where radioligand therapy is not available. Treatment decisions should be individualised, taking into account disease biology, imaging eligibility, logistics, and patient preference.

## RADIUM‑223

Radium-223 dichloride is a bone-targeting alpha emitter approved for the treatment of mCRPC with symptomatic bone metastases and no known visceral disease. Its approval was based on the ALSYMPCA trial, a phase III study enrolling 921 patients with symptomatic bone-predominant mCRPC [18]. Approximately 57% of patients had received prior docetaxel chemotherapy. Radium-223 signifcantly improved OS compared to placebo (median OS 14.9 vs. 11.3 months, HR 0.70, p<0.001), with favourable effects on time to frst symptomatic skeletal event (SSE) and preser‑ vation of quality of life (QoL). The treatment was generally well tolerated, with low rates of myelo‑ suppression and gastrointestinal toxicity, making it an appealing option for patients with limited treat‑ ment tolerance.

A subsequent network meta-analysis com‑ pared the effcacy and safety of second-line sys‑ temic therapies in patients with mCRPC who had progressed after docetaxel [19]. The anal‑ ysis showed that radium-223 and cabazitaxel had comparable OS benefts, but radium-223 exhibited a signifcantly more favourable safety profile, with the lowest incidence of serious adverse events among all agents studied. In con‑ trast, cabazitaxel was associated with the high‑ est rate of grade ≥ 3 adverse events, particularly haematologic toxicity. These fndings suggest that in patients with symptomatic bone-only disease and limited tolerance for chemotherapy, radium-223 offers a survival beneft on par with cabazitaxel, while minimising treatment-related burden.

The recently reported RAPSON trial evaluated sequencing strategies of radium-223 and doc‑ etaxel in a multicentre, open-label randomised study [20]. Patients with bone-predominant mCRPC were randomised to receive either radium-223 followed by docetaxel, or the reverse sequence. While no signifcant differences in OS or radiographic PFS were observed between arms, initiating treatment with radium-223 was associated with improved health-related quality of life, lower hematologic toxicity, and better tolerability.

Further supporting a contemporary role of radium-223 in an evolving era of radioligand therapy, a retrospective study assessed the feasi‑ bility of 177Lu-PSMA-617 following progression on radium-223 [21]. Among 28 patients treated with Lu-PSMA within 8 weeks of radium-223 failure, 64% achieved a ≥ 50% PSA decline, with median PFS of 10.6  months and OS of 18.4 months, suggesting that Lu-PSMA is an effective salvage strategy post radium-223.

These findings underscore that radium-223 remains a viable, well-tolerated treatment option for patients with symptomatic, bone-only mCRPC, especially where access to next-genera‑ tion imaging and genetic testing is limited, and where patients are less likely to tolerate chemo‑ therapy. However, its use is constrained by avail‑ ability and eligibility limited to patients with‑ out visceral metastases, reinforcing the need for timely administration and thoughtful sequencing within the evolving treatment landscape.

## PARP INHIBITORS

PARP inhibitors are now a standard treatment for patients with mCRPC with homologous recombination repair (HRR) gene alterations, found in about 20–25% of cases, with BRCA2 mutations being the most common (approx. 8–10%) and BRCA1 less frequent (approx. 1–2%) [22]. Most commonly used after progression on ARTAs and typically a line of taxane chemo‑ therapy, approval for their use varies regionally: olaparib is authorised in Europe for germline/ somatic BRCA1/2 mutations, while the US label, based on the PROFOUND trial, includes a broader range of DNA repair gene alterations. Rucaparib is also FDA-approved for patients with mCRPC with deleterious BRCA1/2 mutations.

Both trials showed limited effcacy in non-BRCA HRR mutations—for instance the radio‑ graphic response rate in PROfound for patients with a BRCA2 mutation was up to 43%, 20–25% for BRCA1, and other HRR defciencies less than 15%, underscoring the importance of individu‑ alising decision-making to the specifc genetic aberration detected.

Combination regimens incorporating an ARTA with a PARP inhibitor—such as olapa‑ rib plus abiraterone in PROPEL, niraparib plus abiraterone in MAGNITUDE, and talazoparib plus enzalutamide in TALAPRO-2—have shown effcacy in the frst-line mCRPC setting. While the regulatory labels do not explicitly preclude prior ARTA exposure, all three pivotal trials either excluded or tightly restricted prior use of second-generation AR pathway inhibitors in the mCRPC setting. Consequently, these studies do not provide evidence for patients who have progressed on a prior ARTA, which is the popula‑ tion of interest for this review. For this reason, ARTA–PARP inhibitor combinations are not dis‑ cussed in the context of post-ARTA therapeutic sequencing.

PARP inhibitors are generally well tolerated, including in patients with ECOG performance status 2. Common side effects include anaemia, fatigue, nausea, and thrombocytopenia, with hematologic toxicity more frequent in combi‑ nation treatments. Serious events like myelodys‑ plastic syndrome are rare (< 1%). Regular blood monitoring and dose adjustments help manage toxicities without compromising effcacy.

In summary, PARP inhibitors represent a standard treatment option for patients with mCRPC with selected HRR mutations, with the greatest benefit seen in BRCA2-mutated tumours. Treatment decisions should consider the precise mutation status to optimise sequenc‑ ing and outcomes.

## OTHER CHEMOTHERAPY OPTIONS

## Docetaxel Rechallenge

Docetaxel and cabazitaxel are both taxane chem‑ otherapies targeting microtubules, but struc‑ tural differences give cabazitaxel an advantage in overcoming resistance. Specifcally, cabazi‑ taxel has a reduced affnity for P-glycoprotein effux pumps, allowing it to remain effective in cases where docetaxel has failed, supporting its use as a standard second-line chemotherapy in mCRPC.

On this background, the FIRSTANA trial was conducted in frst-line mCRPC to compare doc‑ etaxel and cabazitaxel, but failed to demon‑ strate superiority of cabazitaxel over docetaxel [25]. Efforts to directly compare these agents later down the treatment sequence (docetaxel rechallenge vs. cabazitaxel) were hindered by poor recruitment in the CANTATA phase III trial, which was terminated early [26]. This leaves cli‑ nicians with limited high-level evidence to guide the choice between docetaxel rechallenge and cabazitaxel.

Recently, a large retrospective analysis was presented at the Genitourinary (GU) ASCO 2025 comparing docetaxel rechallenge (rDOC) to cabazitaxel (CAB) in 669 patients with mCRPC previously treated with docetaxel [27]. The study mandated that an interval of at least 90 days from the last dose of docetaxel. Docetaxel rechallenge showed a higher proportion of patients achieving a≥90% PSA decline (PSA90 11% vs. 3%, p<0.001) and longer OS (12.5 vs. 9.6 months, p < 0.001) compared to cabazitaxel. Time to next systemic treatment was numerically longer with docetaxel rechallenge (16.4 vs. 12.4 months, p=0.2). PSA50 response rates were similar between groups (8% vs. 9%, p = 0.31).

Toxicity profles differ and impact treatment selection. Cabazitaxel is associated with higher rates of neutropenia and diarrhoea but less neuro‑ toxicity than docetaxel, making it a better option for patients with pre-existing neuropathy or good performance status who can tolerate intensive chemotherapy. Docetaxel rechallenge, particu‑ larly at a 2-weekly regimen of docetaxel 50 mg/ m2 based on the phase III PROSTY trial [28], may be preferred in patients with signifcant comor‑ bidities or concerns about cabazitaxel-related toxicities.

The decision to rechallenge with docetaxel generally depends on the depth and duration of response to the initial docetaxel course. The APCCC voted in consensus that the majority would not offer docetaxel rechallenge if a patient relapsed within 12 months of receipt of docetaxel in the metastatic hormone-sensitive prostate can‑ cer (mHSPC) setting, but voted overwhelmingly to consider this option if it has been more than 36 months since receipt of docetaxel [12].

In summary, although cabazitaxel remains the standard second-line taxane post docetaxel, recent real-world data suggest that docetaxel rechallenge may offer comparable or even superior outcomes in selected patients. Further prospective studies are needed to defne optimal sequencing and per‑ sonalise treatment in this setting.

## Aggressive Variant Prostate Cancers: Platinum‑Based Regimens

Between 10% and 20% of patients with mCRPC develop neuroendocrine or aggressive variant prostate cancer (AVPC) features following prior exposure to androgen deprivation therapy (ADT). This subtype often demonstrates dis‑ tinct clinical behaviour including low PSA pro‑ duction despite high tumour burden, visceral metastases, and resistance to conventional androgen receptor targeted therapy.

The Aparicio criteria are commonly used to identify AVPC, incorporating clinical, biochem‑ ical, and pathological features. These include neuroendocrine histology, visceral metastases, elevated lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), low PSA relative to disease burden, bulky tumours, and biomarker elevation such as carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) and hypercalcaemia [29]. Chem‑ otherapy with platinum-based agents remains the cornerstone of treatment for AVPC. In a phase II randomised trial by Corn et al. involv‑ ing 160 patients with mCRPC enriched for AVPC features, platinum-based chemotherapy in the form of carboplatin and cabazitaxel showed the greatest beneft in patients with

AVPC, with improved PFS and overall response compared to non-AVPC subgroups [30].

## Other Regimens

Beyond standard taxane chemotherapy, a range of alternative chemotherapy regimens have been investigated in the mCRPC setting, particularly for patients who are heavily pretreated or have poor performance status. While most of these approaches lack phase III evi‑ dence, several phase II studies and retrospec‑ tive series suggest that select regimens may offer palliative beneft or modest disease con‑ trol (Table 2). These include platinum-based combinations, metronomic strategies, and rechallenge with older agents. Although not widely adopted in standard practice, such regi‑ mens may be considered in later-line settings or where access to newer therapies is limited. Key fndings from representative studies are summarised below.

## LIMITED ROLE OF 2ND ANDROGEN RECEPTOR‑TARGETED AGENTS

Second-line use of androgen receptor-targeted agents (ARTAs) remains a common real-world practice where the most frequent treatment sequence for mCRPC is abiraterone followed by enzalutamide, or vice versa [11]. However, evidence supporting the clinical benefit of this sequencing is limited. The pivotal ABI-ENZA phase II trial showed promising response of an abiraterone to enzalutamide sequencing strat‑ egy but overall responses remained low and no survival benefit was demonstrated. Further‑ more, both the CARD trial and the PSMAfore trial have shown that cabazitaxel and lute‑ tium-177 PSMA-617, respectively, offer supe‑ rior survival and response rates compared to a second ARTA—highlighting the inferiority of ARTAs as a control arm, especially chemo‑ therapy-naïve patients (Fig. 1). As such, use of a second ARTA should generally be reserved for patients who are either unfit for or decline alternative life-prolonging therapies such as chemotherapy, radioligand therapy, or PARP inhibitors. ## RECENT UPDATES AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS IN MCRPC POST‑DOCETAXEL AND ARTA

## CONTACT‑02: Atezolizumab+Cabozantinib

The CONTACT-02 phase  III trial evaluated atezolizumab, a programmed cell death  1 ligand  1 (PD-L1) inhibitor, in combination with cabozantinib, a multikinase inhibitor, in patients with mCRPC with extrapelvic soft tissue metastases previously treated with a next-generation ARTA, and may or may not have received a line of taxane chemotherapy. Recently published in June 2025, the study enrolled 575 patients randomized to atezoli‑ zumab–cabozantinib versus a second ARTA (abiraterone or enzalutamide). While the com‑ bination improved radiographic PFS (median rPFS 6.3 vs. 4.2  months; HR 0.67), it failed to meet its co-primary endpoint of OS, with no significant OS benefit at interim analysis (median OS 16.7 vs. 14.8 months; HR 0.89) [40].

## Immunotherapy

While prostate cancer has traditionally been considered immunologically “cold”, a small subset of patients may benefit from immune checkpoint inhibitors. In particular, micros‑ atellite instability-high (MSI-H) or mismatch repair deficient (dMMR) tumours—found in approximately 2–5% of mCRPC cases— have demonstrated meaningful responses to PD-1 blockade [41]. On the basis of this, the FDA granted tumour-agnostic approval for pembrolizumab in dMMR or MSI-H cancers, including prostate cancer. Although PD-L1 expression is more variably present in mCRPC and not a reliable predictive biomarker for immunotherapy response in this setting [42], some small studies suggest that high PD-L1 may correlate with an immune-inflamed phenotype [43]. Nonetheless, beyond the dMMR/MSI-H cohort, checkpoint inhibitors have shown limited efficacy as monotherapy in unselected patients. Ongoing research is investigating combination approaches (e.g. with radiation, PARP inhibitors) to sensitise tumours to immunotherapy and expand its role in prostate cancer care.

## Emerging Radioligand Therapies and Next‑Generation Targets

Following the success of 177Lu-PSMA-617, new radioligand therapies are under investigation, particularly in patients with disease progression post-LuPSMA. These include actinium-225-based alpha emitters (e.g. 225Ac-PSMA-I&T, 225Ac-J591) [44]. Alpha particles, with higher energy and shorter range, may overcome resistance to beta-emitting agents and are being evaluated in early-phase trials. However, their use is currently limited by toxicity (especially xerostomia) and availability of isotopes.

## Cellular Therapies and Antibody–Drug Conjugates

CAR -T  cell therapy targeting PSMA (e.g. P -PSMA -101) is under early -phase clinical eval ‑ uation [49]. Preliminary responses have been observed, though tumour microenvironment immunosuppression and toxicity manage ‑ ment (especially CRS) remain key challenges (Table  4). Antibody–drug conjugates (ADCs) such as MLN2704 (anti -PSMA linked to may ‑ tansinoid) and newer agents targeting B7 -H3 or TROP2 are being explored for patients with advanced mCRPC resistant to standard therapies [50]. While data remain preliminary, these thera‑ pies may allow for selective cytotoxic delivery in biomarker -enriched populations.

## CONCLUSION

In the post -ARTA, post -docetaxel landscape of mCRPC, treatment decisions must balance clin ‑ ical evidence with individual patient factors, biomarker status, and real -world constraints (Table  4). While therapeutic options continue to expand, the lack of direct comparative data underscores the need for personalised, pragmatic approaches to care. Ongoing research into novel agents and predictive biomarkers holds promise for refning and improving treatment sequenc ‑ ing in this challenging setting.
